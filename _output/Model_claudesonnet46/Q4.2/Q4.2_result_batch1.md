## Hg²⁺ Donor-Atom Preference: **S >> N >> O**

*A data-driven HSAB analysis using NIST SRD-46*

---

### Hypothesis

**Hg²⁺ overwhelmingly prefers sulfur-donor ligands**, followed by nitrogen-donors, with oxygen-donors being the weakest binders. This is a direct consequence of **Pearson's Hard-Soft Acid-Base (HSAB) theory**: Hg²⁺ is one of the *softest* metal ions in the periodic table — large, highly polarizable, and with low charge density — so it forms the most stable complexes with *soft* donor atoms (S, I, C) that can engage in significant covalent bonding, and the weakest complexes with *hard* donors (O) that interact primarily electrostatically.

---

Hydroxide forms complex polynuclear species (Hg₂(OH)⁺, Hg₃L₃, etc.) rather than simple ML complexes, reflecting Hg²⁺'s reluctance to bind O strongly.

### Three Key Chemistry Insights

1. **The halide litmus test for soft acids.** The progression Cl⁻ < Br⁻ < I⁻ in both VLM richness and complex stoichiometry is the classic diagnostic for a soft Lewis acid. Hg²⁺ passes this test emphatically — it forms stable HgI₄²⁻ complexes while HgF₂ is essentially ionic and weakly associated in solution.

2. **Biological toxicity explained.** Hg²⁺'s extreme toxicity arises directly from this S-preference: it binds irreversibly to cysteine –SH groups in enzymes (e.g., thioredoxin reductase, glutathione peroxidase), with estimated log K > 20 for thiolate binding. This strips sulfur from catalytic active sites and disrupts cellular redox chemistry — a consequence written directly in the stability constant data.

3. **Covalency vs. electrostatics.** The enormous gap between S-donor and O-donor constants reflects the dominant role of *covalent bonding* in Hg²⁺ complexes. Unlike hard metals (Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺) that bind O-donors electrostatically, Hg²⁺ achieves stability through orbital overlap with polarizable donor atoms — a fundamentally different bonding regime that the SRD-46 data makes quantitatively vivid.
