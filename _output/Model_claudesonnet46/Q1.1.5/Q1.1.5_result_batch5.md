## Oxalic Acid pKa Values — SRD-46 Results

Oxalic acid (**ethanedioic acid**, SMILES: `O=C(O)C(=O)O`) is a **diprotic acid** with **two dissociation steps**:

| Step | Equilibrium | pKa | T (°C) | I (M) |
|------|-------------|-----|--------|-------|
| 1st dissociation | H₂L → HL⁻ + H⁺ | **−1.2** | 25 | 0.1 |
| 2nd dissociation | HL⁻ → L²⁻ + H⁺ | **3.8** | 25 | 0.1 |

### How many dissociation steps?
**Two** — corresponding to the sequential removal of each of the two carboxylic acid protons (–COOH groups).

### Chemistry Insight
- The **first pKa of −1.2** is remarkably low (even negative!), meaning H₂C₂O₄ is a *strong* acid for its first proton — comparable in strength to HCl. This is due to the strong electron-withdrawing effect of the adjacent carbonyl group stabilizing the HL⁻ anion.
- The **second pKa of 3.8** is more typical of a weak carboxylic acid. The second proton is harder to remove because the already-negatively-charged HL⁻ resists further deprotonation.
- The large gap (~5 pKa units) between the two steps means that at physiological pH (~7.4), oxalate exists almost entirely as the fully deprotonated **oxalate dianion (L²⁻)** — the form responsible for kidney stone formation when it precipitates with Ca²⁺.

*Source: NIST SRD-46 (ligand_8872, metal_68/H⁺, 25 °C, I = 0.1 M)*

### Verdict & Explanation

**Verdict:**
The subagent correctly identified two dissociation steps but reported a clearly erroneous first pKa of −1.2; oxalic acid's accepted pKa1 is ~1.25, not negative.

**Explanation:**
Oxalic acid is a diprotic acid with two sequential carboxyl dissociations. The well-established values are pKa1 ≈ 1.25 and pKa2 ≈ 4.27 (25°C, dilute solution). The subagent's pKa1 = −1.2 is scientifically incorrect—oxalic acid is a weak acid, not a strong acid comparable to HCl—suggesting a data retrieval or sign error in the reported value.
