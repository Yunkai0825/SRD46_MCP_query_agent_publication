### The pH Calculation

**Step 1 ‚ÄĒ Fraction of free oxalate dianion (őĪ‚āā) as a function of pH**

Using the two pKa values (pKa‚āĀ = ‚ąí1.2, pKa‚āā = 3.8):

$$\alpha_2(\text{pH}) = \frac{1}{1 + 10^{(pK_{a2}-\text{pH})} + 10^{(pK_{a1}+pK_{a2}-2\cdot\text{pH})}}$$

| pH | őĪ‚āā (fraction as C‚āāO‚āĄ¬≤‚ĀĽ) |
|----|--------------------------|
| 2.0 | ~0.0006 (0.06%) |
| 3.0 | ~0.06 (6%) |
| 3.8 | ~0.50 (50%) |
| 5.0 | ~0.99 (99%) |
| 6.0 | ~1.00 (‚Čą100%) |

**Step 2 ‚ÄĒ Effective ion product vs. Ksp**

For the monohydrate (the clinically relevant whewellite form):

$$K_{sp} = [\text{Ca}^{2+}][\text{C}_2\text{O}_4^{2-}] = 10^{-8.75}$$

The effective solubility product in terms of *total* oxalate concentration is:

$$[\text{Ca}^{2+}] \cdot \alpha_2 \cdot C_{\text{ox,total}} \geq K_{sp}$$

For typical physiological/laboratory concentrations (e.g., [Ca¬≤‚Āļ] ‚Čą 2.5 mM, [oxalate]_total ‚Čą 0.5 mM):

$$\text{IP} = (2.5\times10^{-3}) \times \alpha_2 \times (5\times10^{-4})$$

Setting IP = Ksp = 10‚ĀĽ‚Āł¬∑‚Ā∑‚ĀĶ ‚Čą 1.78 √ó 10‚ĀĽ‚ĀĻ:

$$\alpha_2 \geq \frac{1.78\times10^{-9}}{1.25\times10^{-6}} \approx 0.0014$$

This őĪ‚āā ‚Čą 0.14% is reached at **pH ‚Čą 2.6** ‚ÄĒ but this is the *thermodynamic* onset. In practice, the aqueous CaC‚āāO‚āĄ complex (log K = +3.19) sequesters a significant fraction of both ions, delaying visible precipitation.

### Bottom Line

> **Calcium oxalate precipitation becomes significant above approximately pH 4‚Äď5.**

| pH Range | What Happens |
|----------|-------------|
| < 3 | Oxalate exists mainly as H‚āāC‚āāO‚āĄ / HC‚āāO‚āĄ‚ĀĽ; [C‚āāO‚āĄ¬≤‚ĀĽ] too low to exceed Ksp |
| 3‚Äď4 | Thermodynamic threshold crossed for high concentrations; aqueous CaC‚āāO‚āĄ complex buffers precipitation |
| **4‚Äď5** | **őĪ‚āā rises sharply past 50%; precipitation becomes robust and observable** |
| > 6 | Essentially all oxalate is C‚āāO‚āĄ¬≤‚ĀĽ; precipitation is rapid and nearly complete |

The critical driver is **pKa‚āā = 3.8**: below this pH, the dianion fraction is suppressed and the ion product stays below Ksp (10‚ĀĽ‚Āł¬∑‚Ā∑‚ĀĶ). Above pH ~4‚Äď5, őĪ‚āā ‚Üí 1 and the full oxalate concentration is available to precipitate calcium.

**Clinical relevance:** Kidney stones (calcium oxalate) form in urine at pH 5‚Äď7, exactly where this analysis predicts robust precipitation. Acidic urine (pH < 5.5) slightly inhibits oxalate stone formation by protonating oxalate ‚ÄĒ a fact exploited in dietary management of hyperoxaluria.
