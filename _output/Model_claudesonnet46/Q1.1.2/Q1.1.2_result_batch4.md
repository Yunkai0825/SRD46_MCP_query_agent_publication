## Chemistry Insights

1. **Ionic strength effect:** All three pKa values rise systematically with increasing ionic strength (from I = 0.15 to 3.0 mol/L), a classic salt effect — higher ionic strength stabilises the more highly charged deprotonated species less effectively, making deprotonation appear slightly harder and raising the apparent pKa.

2. **Electrolyte dependence:** At the same ionic strength (e.g. I = 0.15 mol/L), KNO₃ consistently gives slightly lower pKa values than NaClO₄, reflecting subtle differences in ion-pair interactions with the citrate anions.

3. **Biological relevance:** Citric acid's three pKa values (≈ 2.1, 4.4, 5.8 at physiological-ish conditions) mean that at blood pH 7.4, citrate exists almost entirely as the fully deprotonated trianion L³⁻ — the form that chelates Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, and Fe³⁺ so effectively in the Krebs cycle and in anticoagulant blood-storage solutions.
