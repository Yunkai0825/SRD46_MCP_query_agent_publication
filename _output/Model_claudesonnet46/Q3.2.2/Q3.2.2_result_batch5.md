### Is There a Clear Trend? **Yes.**

A **clear, monotonic decrease** in log β with increasing ionic strength is observed from I = 0 to I = 2.0 mol/L for all three Ni–en species. Key observations:

1. **Direction of the trend:** log β decreases as ionic strength rises. This is the expected behavior when a divalent cation (Ni²⁺, charge = +2) reacts with a neutral ligand (en, charge = 0). As ionic strength increases, the activity coefficient of Ni²⁺ is suppressed by Debye–Hückel / SIT effects, effectively stabilizing the free aquo ion and reducing the apparent driving force for complexation.

2. **Stoichiometry amplifies the effect:** The higher the complex stoichiometry, the larger the total drop in log β. NiL₃ loses ~2.2 log units from I = 0 → 2 M, while NiL loses only ~0.9 units. This makes sense because forming NiL₃ involves three sequential binding steps, each of which is individually sensitive to ionic strength.

3. **Chelate effect remains dominant:** Even at I = 2.0 M, log β(NiL₃) ≈ 17.0 — Ni²⁺ is still very strongly bound by three bidentate en ligands, forming the classic tris-chelate [Ni(en)₃]²⁺ complex. The chelate effect (entropy gain from ring closure) is robust across the entire ionic strength range studied.

4. **Practical implications:** The thermodynamic constants (I = 0) are ~0.4–0.9 log units higher than values at physiological or near-physiological ionic strength (I ≈ 0.1–0.15 M). Applications such as electroplating baths, analytical masking, or biological modeling should use the I = 0.1–0.5 M values rather than the thermodynamic limit.
