## Correlation Analysis

### Within oxygen-donor carboxylates: moderate positive correlation

Plotting the carboxylate-only subset (oxalate, malonate, succinate, citrate) against their highest pKa and log K₁:

| Ligand | Highest pKa | log K₁ |
|--------|-------------|--------|
| Oxalic acid | 3.80 | ~3.0 |
| Malonic acid | 5.27 | ~2.4 |
| Succinic acid | 5.24 | ~1.8 |
| Citric acid | 6.40 | 4.68 |

The trend is **not strictly monotonic** within diacids (oxalate > malonate > succinate despite lower pKa), revealing that **chelate ring geometry matters as much as basicity**. Oxalate forms a tight 5-membered chelate ring; succinate's 7-membered ring is less favorable. Citric acid's higher log K₁ reflects both higher basicity *and* tridentate coordination.

### The glycine anomaly: nitrogen donor mismatch

Glycine has the highest pKa in the set (9.57, amine group) yet the *lowest* Ca²⁺ log K₁ (~1.4). This is the most chemically informative data point: **Ca²⁺ does not benefit from high-pKa nitrogen donors**, because Ca²⁺ is a hard Lewis acid (HSAB) that forms predominantly electrostatic bonds with hard oxygen donors. The soft-ish nitrogen donor, despite its high basicity, provides little additional stabilization.

## 🔬 Hypothesis: Basicity, Donor Hardness, and Binding Strength in Alkaline-Earth Metals

> **Hypothesis — Two-Factor Model for Alkaline-Earth Metal Binding:**
>
> The stability of alkaline-earth metal complexes (Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, Sr²⁺, Ba²⁺) is governed by two independent factors:
>
> **Factor 1 — Oxygen-donor basicity (pKa):** Within a chemically homologous family of oxygen-donor ligands (carboxylates, phosphates, hydroxamates), higher pKa correlates positively with log K₁. Because alkaline-earth metals are hard Lewis acids forming predominantly ionic/electrostatic bonds, a more basic oxygen donor (higher electron density, higher pKa) is a more effective electron-pair donor. This is the chemical basis of the linear free-energy relationship (LFER) observed in carboxylate series.
>
> **Factor 2 — Chelate topology dominates over basicity:** Denticity, chelate ring size, and preorganization amplify (or suppress) stability far beyond what pKa alone predicts. A tridentate ligand like citrate (log K₁ = 4.68, confirmed SRD-46) vastly outperforms monodentate acetate (log K₁ < 1) despite similar per-donor basicities, due to the entropic chelate effect. Optimal ring sizes (5- or 6-membered) further enhance stability.
>
> **Corollary — Donor atom selectivity:** The pKa–log K correlation is **donor-atom selective**. It holds within the oxygen-donor family but **fails completely across donor types**: glycine's amine pKa of 9.57 does not translate to high Ca²⁺ affinity (log K₁ ~1.4) because nitrogen is a borderline/soft donor mismatched to the hard Ca²⁺ center. This contrasts sharply with transition metals (e.g., Cu²⁺, Ni²⁺) where high-pKa amine donors dramatically increase stability.

| Observation | Interpretation |

| pKa–log K correlation holds within O-donor families | Electrostatic bonding model valid for alkaline-earth metals |

**Biological implication:** Ca²⁺-binding proteins (calmodulin, troponin C, annexins) exploit precisely this chemistry — their EF-hand motifs use clusters of Asp/Glu carboxylate oxygens and backbone carbonyls, not histidine or cysteine. The moderate but reliable pKa–affinity correlation within carboxylates, amplified by the chelate effect of multiple coordinating residues, gives these proteins their nanomolar Ca²⁺ affinities.
