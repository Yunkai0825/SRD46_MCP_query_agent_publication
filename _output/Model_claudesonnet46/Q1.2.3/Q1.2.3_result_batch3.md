## The Ligand: L-Cysteine

Cysteine (`N[C@@H](CS)C(=O)O`) is a tridentate amino acid with three ionizable groups, making it a rich source of speciation:

| Protonation Step | Transition | pKa (25°C, I=0.1 M) |
|---|---|---|
| Carboxyl (–COOH) | H₃L → H₂L | −1.90 |
| Thiol (–SH) | H₂L → HL | 8.18 |
| Amino (–NH₃⁺) | HL → L | 10.30 |

The fully deprotonated form **L²⁻** carries a carboxylate, thiolate, and free amine — a powerful tridentate donor set (O, S, N) that binds Zn²⁺ very strongly.

## Chemistry Highlights

1. **Exceptionally strong 1:2 complex (log β₂ = 18.12):** Cysteine's soft thiolate sulfur is a superb match for Zn²⁺ (a borderline-soft Lewis acid), and the tridentate O/N/S coordination geometry saturates the metal's coordination sphere. This rivals EDTA-type complexes in stability.

2. **Trinuclear cluster Zn₃L₄ (log β = ~49):** The network contains a remarkable **trinuclear species** where three zinc ions are bridged by four cysteine ligands. Such polynuclear clusters are rare and biologically relevant — they resemble the zinc-thiolate clusters found in **metallothioneins**, proteins that store and traffic zinc and cadmium in living cells.

3. **Protonated mixed species (ZnHL₂, Zn(HL)₂):** At mildly acidic pH, partially protonated cysteine can still coordinate Zn²⁺ through its thiolate, giving rise to protonated complex species. This is important for understanding zinc speciation in physiological pH ranges (6–8).
