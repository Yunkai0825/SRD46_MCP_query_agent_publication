## 2. Hg²⁺ — Stability Constants with Key Ligands

Hg²⁺ is a **Class B (soft)** metal ion and follows the HSAB principle: it binds most strongly to soft donors (S, I, CN⁻) and weakly to hard donors (F⁻, O-donors).

## 4. Mercury(I) — Hg⁺

Hg⁺ is unusual: it exists as the **dimeric Hg₂²⁺** ion in solution and tends to disproportionate to Hg⁰ + Hg²⁺ unless stabilized. The database records 83 VLMs across 31 ligands — primarily halides and a few soft-donor ligands that kinetically stabilize the Hg–Hg bond.

**Methylmercury (MeHg⁺)** is the most environmentally dangerous form — produced by bacterial methylation of Hg²⁺ in sediments, it bioaccumulates in fish and binds selectively to thiolate groups in proteins. Its database coverage (148 VLMs, 79 ligands) reflects active research interest in its speciation in biological and environmental matrices.

## 6. Chemistry Highlights

- **Softness extremity:** Hg²⁺ is the softest divalent metal in the periodic table. Its log β values with soft donors (I⁻, CN⁻, RS⁻) dwarf those of Zn²⁺ or Cd²⁺ by 10–20 log units.
- **Environmental relevance:** In seawater and chloride-rich media, HgCl₂ and HgCl₃⁻ dominate speciation — these neutral/anionic species are membrane-permeable, explaining Hg's bioavailability.
- **Remediation chemistry:** The extreme stability of Hg–thiolate and Hg–cyanide complexes is exploited in gold mining (cyanide leaching co-mobilizes Hg) and in chelation therapy (DMSA, a dithiol, is used to treat mercury poisoning).
