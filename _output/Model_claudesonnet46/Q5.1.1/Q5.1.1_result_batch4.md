## Key Chemistry Insights

### 🔴 Hg²⁺ is a Classic "Soft" Lewis Acid (HSAB Theory)
Mercury(II) shows a dramatic preference for **soft donor atoms** in the order:
> **S >> I > Br > Cl >> N > O**

This is why HgS (cinnabar) is extraordinarily insoluble (log Ksp ≈ −52), and why thiourea, thiocyanate, and sulfide ligands dominate the strongest complexes. The cyanide system (37 VLMs, up to Hg(CN)₄²⁻) is also exceptionally strong due to CN⁻'s soft character.

### 🟡 Halide Complexes — A Rich Series
Hg²⁺ forms the complete halide series HgX⁺, HgX₂, HgX₃⁻, HgX₄²⁻ for Cl, Br, and I, with stability increasing I > Br > Cl. The chloride system alone has 38 measured values across ionic strengths from near-zero to 3.15 M — critical for understanding mercury speciation in seawater and industrial brines.

### 🟢 Organomercury Cations (MeHg⁺, EtHg⁺, PhHg⁺)
Methylmercury (MeHg⁺) is the most data-rich organomercury species with 79 ligand partners and 148 complexes. It forms linear 2-coordinate complexes and has an even stronger preference for sulfur donors than Hg²⁺ — explaining why it bioaccumulates by binding to cysteine thiol groups in proteins. This is the species responsible for Minamata disease.

### 🔵 Hydrolysis Network
The hydroxide system shows complex polynuclear species including Hg₂(OH)²⁺, Hg₃(OH)₃³⁺, and even solid HgO (red and black polymorphs), measured across 29 VLMs. This is important for understanding mercury behavior at near-neutral to alkaline pH.
