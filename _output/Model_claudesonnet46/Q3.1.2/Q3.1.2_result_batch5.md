### Why Histidine Wins: The Chemistry Argument

**Histidine** (ligand_5898) is the standout candidate for Cu²⁺/Ni²⁺ selectivity at pH 7, for several converging reasons:

#### 1. Irving–Williams Series Advantage
Cu²⁺ sits at the top of the Irving–Williams series (Mn < Fe < Co < Ni < Cu > Zn), meaning it forms intrinsically stronger complexes than Ni²⁺ with virtually all N/O donors. The **gap is largest for nitrogen-rich, multidentate ligands** — exactly what histidine provides.

#### 2. Histidine's Unique Tridentate Binding Mode
Histidine offers three donor atoms: **–NH₂ (amine), –COO⁻ (carboxylate), and imidazole-N**. Cu²⁺ exploits all three in a square-planar geometry (Jahn-Teller distorted d⁹), while Ni²⁺ (d⁸, octahedral preference) binds less optimally to the imidazole nitrogen. This gives Cu²⁺ an extra chelate ring advantage.

#### 3. pH 7 Compatibility
Histidine's imidazole pKa = **6.05** means that at pH 7, the imidazole nitrogen is predominantly **deprotonated and available for metal binding** — a critical advantage over ligands whose donor groups are still protonated at pH 7.

For glycine (pKa₂ = 9.57), the amine is still largely protonated at pH 7, reducing the effective free ligand concentration. Histidine's lower pKa₂ makes it far more effective at physiological pH.
