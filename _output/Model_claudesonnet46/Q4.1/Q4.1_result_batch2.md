### 🧪 Reasoned Estimates Based on Donor Chemistry

The following estimates are grounded in the donor number (DN) scale (Gutmann), HSAB theory, and literature precedent for Fe²⁺/Fe³⁺ with analogous neutral O- and N-donor ligands.

#### Framework

- **Fe²⁺** is a borderline Lewis acid (d⁶, moderate hardness); prefers N > O donors among neutral ligands.
- **Fe³⁺** is a hard Lewis acid (d⁵, high charge density); strongly prefers hard O-donors; forms stronger complexes with neutral O-donors than Fe²⁺ does.
- For *neutral solvent molecules* acting as monodentate ligands in aqueous solution, log β₁ values are typically **very small (0–3)** because water competes effectively.

### ⚗️ Chemistry Insights

1. **DMF and THF are primarily relevant as *solvents*, not ligands, in aqueous iron chemistry.** Their complexation constants in water are essentially unmeasurable by classical potentiometry because water displacement is thermodynamically unfavorable. In non-aqueous DMF or THF solutions, Fe²⁺ solvation energies are substantial (ΔG_solv ≈ −1500 kJ/mol range), but these are *solvation* phenomena, not discrete stepwise complexation equilibria in the SRD-46 sense.

2. **Acetonitrile–Fe²⁺ is the most studied of these four in non-aqueous electrochemistry** (e.g., the Fe(MeCN)₆²⁺ complex is well-characterized in pure MeCN, with log β₆ ≈ 10–12 in that medium). However, in aqueous solution, MeCN is displaced by water and the effective log β₁(aq) is near zero.

3. **Ethylene glycol is the most promising candidate for measurable aqueous complexation** due to its chelate effect and the known affinity of Fe³⁺ for polyol oxygen donors (relevant to iron transport in biological and industrial contexts). The deprotonated glycolate form at higher pH gives log β values of 5–7 for Fe³⁺, which is why ethylene glycol is used as an iron chelator in some formulations.
