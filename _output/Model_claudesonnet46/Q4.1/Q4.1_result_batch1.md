### 🔬 Chemical Rationale

**1. DMF–Fe(II)/Fe(III):**  
DMF is a strong O-donor (DN = 26.6) and preferentially coordinates to hard Lewis acids. Fe³⁺ (hard acid) forms stronger adducts than Fe²⁺. In non-aqueous media (e.g., nitromethane), calorimetric studies (Gutmann, Mayer) suggest log K₁ ~ 2–4 for Fe³⁺ and ~1.5–2.5 for Fe²⁺. DMF is commonly used as a coordinating solvent in Fe crystal structures, confirming real but weak coordination.

**2. Acetonitrile–Fe(II)/Fe(III):**  
MeCN is a weak donor (DN = 14.1) with a nitrile N-donor. It is borderline soft, making it a poor match for hard Fe³⁺. Fe²⁺ (softer) forms slightly more stable adducts. Homoleptic [Fe(MeCN)₆]²⁺ is known in anhydrous MeCN but dissociates readily. Estimated log K₁ ~ 0.5–1.5 for Fe²⁺, slightly higher for Fe³⁺ in non-aqueous media only.

**3. THF–Fe(II)/Fe(III):**  
THF is a moderate O-donor (DN = 20.0) but ether oxygens are weakly basic and form very labile complexes. Fe–THF adducts are known in organometallic chemistry (e.g., FeCl₂·THF) but only in strictly anhydrous conditions. Estimated log K₁ ~ 0.5–1.5, with Fe³⁺ marginally stronger due to higher charge density.

**4. Ethylene Glycol–Fe(II)/Fe(III):**  
Ethylene glycol can act as a bidentate O,O-donor via its two hydroxyl groups, forming a 5-membered chelate ring. This chelate effect boosts stability relative to monodentate alcohols. In neutral form (no deprotonation), estimated log K₁ ~ 1.5–2.5 for Fe²⁺ and ~2.5–4.0 for Fe³⁺. At higher pH, deprotonation to alkoxide dramatically increases binding (log K >> 5), but that is a different speciation regime.

### ⚠️ Important Caveats

1. **Medium dependence is critical:** All four solvents form adducts only in non-aqueous or mixed media. In water, competition from H₂O (present at 55 M) overwhelms these weak donors.
2. **Fe³⁺ hydrolysis:** Fe³⁺ hydrolyzes at pH > 2, making direct measurement of Fe³⁺–solvent constants extremely difficult without careful acidification.
3. **These are not classical "stability constants"** in the SRD-46 sense — they are solvent-exchange equilibria or adduct formation constants best measured by calorimetry, NMR, or spectrophotometry in neat solvent.
