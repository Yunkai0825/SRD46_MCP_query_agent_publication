### Chemistry Insight

The universal preference of Fe³⁺ over Fe²⁺ is rooted in **hard–soft acid–base (HSAB) theory**. Fe³⁺ is a harder Lewis acid — higher charge (+3 vs +2) and smaller ionic radius (~0.645 Å vs ~0.780 Å) — so it binds more strongly to hard donor atoms like N and O. The Δlog β advantage **grows with complex order** (ML → ML₂ → ML₃), because the charge difference is amplified across each successive binding step. For polydentate amines, the gap can exceed 5 log units (e.g., dien·ML₂: Δ = +5.85). This is precisely why biological siderophores — polyamine/polycarboxylate scaffolds — achieve extraordinary selectivity for Fe³⁺ over Fe²⁺ and other divalent metals in iron acquisition.
