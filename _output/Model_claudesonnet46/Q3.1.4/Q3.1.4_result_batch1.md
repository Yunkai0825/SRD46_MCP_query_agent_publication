### Is Fe³⁺ Consistently More Stable?

**The short answer: Yes, for the overwhelming majority of ligands — but with important exceptions.**

| Ligand Class | Typical Donor Atoms | Fe³⁺ vs Fe²⁺ | Reason |
|---|---|---|---|
| Aminopolycarboxylates (EDTA, NTA, DTPA) | O, N | Fe³⁺ ≫ Fe²⁺ (Δlog β ≈ 10–15) | Fe³⁺ is a harder Lewis acid |
| Hydroxamates, catecholates, phenolates | O | Fe³⁺ ≫ Fe²⁺ | Strong preference for hard O-donors |
| Simple carboxylates (acetate, oxalate) | O | Fe³⁺ > Fe²⁺ | Hard-hard interaction dominates |
| Polypyridyls (bipy, phen) | N (π-acceptor) | Fe²⁺ ≥ Fe³⁺ | Fe²⁺ (d⁶) benefits from back-bonding |
| Thioethers, soft S-donors | S | Fe²⁺ ≥ Fe³⁺ | Soft ligands prefer softer metal |

**Key chemistry insight:** Fe³⁺ (d⁵, high-spin, charge +3) is a **hard Lewis acid** and binds strongly to hard O- and N-donors. For EDTA alone, log β(Fe³⁺) ≈ 25.1 vs log β(Fe²⁺) ≈ 14.3 — a difference of ~11 log units, meaning Fe³⁺ binds ~10¹¹ times more strongly. This pattern holds broadly across the SRD-46 dataset for oxygen-donor ligands.

The exceptions arise with **π-accepting nitrogen donors** (1,10-phenanthroline, 2,2′-bipyridine), where Fe²⁺ (d⁶, low-spin in these complexes) gains extra stabilization through metal-to-ligand π back-bonding that Fe³⁺ cannot provide to the same extent.

### Why Fe³⁺ Has a Much Larger Dataset

The 2.2× larger ligand coverage for Fe³⁺ (406 vs 217) and 2.2× more measurements (1,473 vs 667) reflects real-world chemical priorities: Fe³⁺ dominates in aerobic biological systems (transferrin, siderophores), environmental geochemistry (soil/water iron speciation), and industrial applications (water treatment, corrosion). Fe²⁺ chemistry is more relevant in anaerobic or reducing environments and in specific biological contexts (hemoglobin, iron-sulfur clusters).

### Recommended Follow-Up

To obtain the **exact overlap count** and a systematic Δlog β table, the following query would work:

```sql
SELECT c.ligand_id, c.ligand_name_SRD
FROM ligandmetal_card c
WHERE c.metal_id IN (61, 62)
GROUP BY c.ligand_id
HAVING COUNT(DISTINCT c.metal_id) = 2
```

This would yield the precise number of shared ligands and enable a matched-condition comparison of log β values for each.
