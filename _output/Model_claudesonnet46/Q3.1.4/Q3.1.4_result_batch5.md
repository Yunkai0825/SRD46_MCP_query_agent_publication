### Verdict

**Fe³⁺ is more stable for the large majority of shared ligands — but not universally.**

The clear exceptions are **soft N-donor ligands**: 1,10-phenanthroline (phen), 2,2'-bipyridine (bipy), and ethylenediamine all show *higher* log β with Fe²⁺.

This pattern is chemically well-grounded in Hard-Soft Acid-Base (HSAB) theory:

- **Fe³⁺ is a hard acid** and strongly prefers hard O-donor ligands (catecholates, hydroxamates, carboxylates, fluoride) through predominantly electrostatic interactions. The advantage can be enormous — catecholate-type ligands show Δ log β > 25 log units in favor of Fe³⁺, which is why siderophores (biological iron-scavenging molecules) are almost universally catecholate or hydroxamate based.

- **Fe²⁺ is a softer, more polarizable acid** and has greater affinity for soft π-accepting N-donors like phen and bipy, which can stabilize Fe²⁺ through metal-to-ligand back-bonding. This is the chemical basis of the classic **ferroin** colorimetric reagent (the intensely red Fe²⁺–phen complex), which is used analytically to detect Fe²⁺ selectively in the presence of Fe³⁺.

**Summary:** Of the 94 ligands studied with both oxidation states, Fe³⁺ complexes are more stable in the overwhelming majority of cases. The exceptions are confined to soft, π-accepting N-donor ligands where Fe²⁺ back-bonding provides a thermodynamic advantage.
