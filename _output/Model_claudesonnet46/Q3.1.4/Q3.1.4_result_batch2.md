### Chemistry Insights

1. **Fe³⁺ wins universally.** The preference for Fe³⁺ holds across all ligand classes — from simple inorganic anions to large multidentate chelators. This reflects Fe³⁺'s higher charge (+3 vs +2) and smaller ionic radius, making it a harder and stronger Lewis acid that forms shorter, more electrostatic bonds with donor atoms.

2. **Hard O-donor ligands show the largest preference for Fe³⁺.** Catecholates, hydroxamates, phenolates, and carboxylates all show Δlog β > 7 log units. This is the chemical basis for biological iron sequestration — siderophores like desferrioxamine B evolved to exploit this enormous thermodynamic preference (Δlog β ≈ +20.6), achieving essentially irreversible Fe³⁺ capture under physiological conditions.

3. **Soft/π-donor ligands narrow the gap considerably.** 1,10-Phenanthroline (Δ = +0.7) and 2,2′-bipyridine (Δ = +1.6) show much smaller differences because Fe²⁺ is a better π-back-donor to these N-heterocyclic ligands. In redox-active systems, this near-parity is exploited: the Fe²⁺/Fe³⁺ couple can be tuned by ligand choice, and tris(bipy)Fe²⁺ is kinetically very stable even though Fe³⁺ is still thermodynamically slightly ahead.

**Source:** NIST SRD-46 (Critically Selected Stability Constants of Metal Complexes Database)
