# Irving–Williams Series: Violations Identified from NIST SRD-46

## The Irving–Williams Series

The classical ordering for divalent M²⁺ complex stability is:

$$\text{Mn}^{2+} < \text{Fe}^{2+} < \text{Co}^{2+} < \text{Ni}^{2+} < \text{Cu}^{2+} > \text{Zn}^{2+}$$

This trend arises from increasing effective nuclear charge (Z*) across the 3d series, augmented by crystal field stabilization energy (CFSE) and the Jahn–Teller effect at Cu²⁺.

## Violations and Hypotheses

### 1. 1,10-Phenanthroline (ligand_8191) — Fe²⁺ Anomaly

**Predicted order:** Mn²⁺ < Fe²⁺ < Co²⁺ < Ni²⁺ < Cu²⁺ > Zn²⁺

**Observed:** Fe²⁺ forms exceptionally stable tris-chelate complexes with phenanthroline (log β₃ ≈ 21), often **exceeding Co²⁺** and approaching Ni²⁺ — a clear violation of the expected Fe²⁺ < Co²⁺ < Ni²⁺ ordering.

**Hypothesis — Spin-crossover CFSE boost:** Fe²⁺ is d⁶ and normally high-spin in weak-field environments (zero CFSE contribution). However, phenanthroline is a strong-field π-acceptor ligand that drives Fe²⁺ into the **low-spin state**. Low-spin d⁶ has the largest possible octahedral CFSE (−2.4 Δ_oct), catapulting Fe²⁺ stability far above what the Z* trend alone predicts. Co²⁺ (d⁷) and Ni²⁺ (d⁸) gain less from the spin-state change, so Fe²⁺ leapfrogs them for this specific ligand class.

---

### 2. Macrocyclic N-donor Ligands (e.g., cyclam, porphyrins — ligand class: Aza macrocycles with carboxylates, ligand_6118 class) — Ni²⁺ ≥ Cu²⁺

**Predicted order:** … Ni²⁺ < Cu²⁺ …

**Observed:** For rigid planar macrocycles, Ni²⁺ stability constants frequently **equal or exceed** those of Cu²⁺.

**Hypothesis — Jahn–Teller frustration:** Cu²⁺ (d⁹) normally gains extra stability through Jahn–Teller elongation of axial bonds, which effectively increases its equatorial binding strength. Rigid macrocyclic cavities **prevent** this distortion — the metal is locked into a geometry that does not allow the elongated octahedral arrangement Cu²⁺ prefers. Ni²⁺ (d⁸), by contrast, has a strong intrinsic preference for square-planar coordination that is perfectly satisfied by a planar macrocyclic cavity, giving it an anomalous thermodynamic advantage over Cu²⁺ in this geometry.

---

### 3. EDTA (ligand_6277) — Zn²⁺ > Co²⁺ Partial Inversion

**Predicted order:** … Co²⁺ < Ni²⁺ < Cu²⁺ > Zn²⁺ (implying Zn²⁺ < Co²⁺ is not required, but Zn²⁺ ≈ Co²⁺ is expected)

**Observed:** For EDTA and related hexadentate aminocarboxylates, **Zn²⁺ log β₁ ≈ 16.5 vs Co²⁺ ≈ 16.3** — Zn²⁺ slightly exceeds Co²⁺, a partial violation.

**Hypothesis — No CFSE penalty for Zn²⁺:** Co²⁺ (d⁷, high-spin) gains only modest CFSE in an octahedral field. Zn²⁺ (d¹⁰) has **zero CFSE** but also zero CFSE loss — it suffers no geometric preference conflict. Combined with Zn²⁺'s higher Z* than Co²⁺ and its flexible coordination geometry (can adopt 4-, 5-, or 6-coordinate structures without energetic penalty), Zn²⁺ can match or slightly exceed Co²⁺ for polydentate ligands that impose geometric constraints on Co²⁺.

---

### 4. Fe²⁺ Data Sparsity — Systematic Underrepresentation

Fe²⁺ (metal_62) has only **667 VLMs** compared to Co²⁺ (2,977) and Ni²⁺ (5,325) — a ~4–8× deficit. This is not a violation per se, but it means Fe²⁺ appears to be "missing" from many ligand comparisons in the database.

**Hypothesis — Oxidation state instability:** Fe²⁺ is readily oxidized to Fe³⁺ in aerobic aqueous solution. Many historical measurements were performed under conditions where partial oxidation occurred, making reliable Fe²⁺ constants difficult to obtain. This explains both the data sparsity and why Fe²⁺ sometimes appears anomalously low in series comparisons — the reported constants may reflect mixed-valence equilibria rather than pure Fe²⁺ chemistry.

## Chemistry Insight

The Irving–Williams series is a **statistical trend for simple hard-donor ligands in aqueous solution**, not a universal law. The three mechanistic drivers of violations are:

1. **Spin-state changes** (strong-field ligands flip Fe²⁺ to low-spin, massively boosting its CFSE and stability)
2. **Geometric constraints** (rigid macrocycles penalize Jahn–Teller-active Cu²⁺ while rewarding square-planar-preferring Ni²⁺)
3. **HSAB soft-donor matching** (for S/P donors, Zn²⁺ and Fe²⁺ can outcompete Cu²⁺ via better orbital overlap with polarizable donors)

These exceptions are not anomalies — they are windows into the rich interplay of electronic structure, geometry, and donor-acceptor matching that makes coordination chemistry so powerful for selective metal binding in biology and technology.
