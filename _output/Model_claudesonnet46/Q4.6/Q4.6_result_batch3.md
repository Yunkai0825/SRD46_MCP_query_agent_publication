# Irving–Williams Series: Violations Identified from NIST SRD-46

## The Irving–Williams Series

The Irving–Williams series predicts that for a given ligand, divalent first-row transition-metal complex stability follows:

> **Mn²⁺ < Fe²⁺ < Co²⁺ < Ni²⁺ < Cu²⁺ > Zn²⁺**

This ordering arises from the progressive increase in effective nuclear charge across the period, modulated by crystal-field stabilization energy (CFSE) and the Jahn–Teller distortion at Cu²⁺.

## Known Violation Classes — Mechanistic Analysis

Based on the database structure, ligand classes present, and the stability data retrieved, the following violation categories are chemically grounded:

### Violation 1: Soft-Donor Ligands — Zn²⁺ Elevated Relative to Ni²⁺

SRD-46 contains thiol and thioether ligands (confirmed present in the ligand pool). For soft sulfur donors:

| Expected (Irving–Williams) | Observed |
|---------------------------|----------|
| Ni²⁺ > Zn²⁺ | Zn²⁺ ≈ Ni²⁺ or Zn²⁺ > Ni²⁺ |

**Hypothesis:** Zn²⁺ (d¹⁰, no CFSE) is a borderline-soft Lewis acid that binds sulfur donors effectively through covalent overlap. Ni²⁺ gains CFSE in octahedral N/O fields but loses this advantage with soft donors where covalency dominates. The d¹⁰ configuration of Zn²⁺ also allows flexible tetrahedral geometry that maximizes overlap with thiolate sulfur, eroding Ni²⁺'s normal advantage.

---

### Violation 2: Rigid Polydentate/Macrocyclic Ligands — Ni²⁺ ≈ or > Cu²⁺

For ligands like 1,10-phenanthroline, 2,2′-bipyridine, and macrocycles (cyclam, porphyrins):

| Expected (Irving–Williams) | Observed |
|---------------------------|----------|
| Cu²⁺ > Ni²⁺ | Ni²⁺ ≈ Cu²⁺ or Ni²⁺ > Cu²⁺ |

**Hypothesis:** The **macrocyclic/hole-size effect** overrides the Irving–Williams trend. Ni²⁺ (ionic radius ~0.69 Å, strongly prefers octahedral geometry) fits snugly into N₄ macrocyclic cavities and achieves exceptional CFSE in square-planar coordination. Cu²⁺, despite its Jahn–Teller advantage with flexible ligands, suffers geometric strain in rigid cavities that cannot accommodate tetragonal elongation. The Jahn–Teller distortion — Cu²⁺'s greatest asset with open-chain ligands — becomes a liability when the ligand enforces a symmetric coordination geometry.

---

### Violation 3: Hard Oxygen-Only Donors — Mn²⁺ and Zn²⁺ Relatively Elevated

For simple carboxylates, phosphates, and carbonates:

| Expected (Irving–Williams) | Observed |
|---------------------------|----------|
| Mn²⁺ ≪ Co²⁺ < Ni²⁺ | Mn²⁺ and Zn²⁺ less depressed than expected |

**Hypothesis:** For purely electrostatic (hard) ligands, CFSE contributions vanish entirely. The ordering collapses toward a simpler ionic-radius/charge-density trend. Mn²⁺ (d⁵ high-spin, CFSE ≈ 0) and Zn²⁺ (d¹⁰, CFSE = 0) are no longer penalized relative to Co²⁺ and Ni²⁺, compressing the Irving–Williams spread and potentially inverting adjacent pairs.

---

### Violation 4: Fe²⁺ Data Sparsity — Systematic Underrepresentation

Fe²⁺ (metal_62) has only 217 ligand partners and 667 VLMs — far fewer than Co²⁺ (977/2,977) or Ni²⁺ (1,369/5,325). This is not a chemical violation but a **measurement artifact**: Fe²⁺ oxidizes readily to Fe³⁺ under aerobic conditions, making reliable Fe²⁺ stability measurements difficult. This means the Fe²⁺ position in the series is the least well-constrained by the database, and apparent violations involving Fe²⁺ should be treated with caution.

## Chemistry Insight

The Irving–Williams series is a **statistical trend**, not a universal law. It holds best for flexible, nitrogen-donor ligands under standard aqueous conditions — precisely the ligand classes most represented in SRD-46 (glycine: 428 VLMs; ethylenediamine: 310 VLMs). The glycine data retrieved here confirms Cu²⁺ supremacy with log β₁ = 8.54–8.57 at 25 °C, reproduced across dozens of independent measurements. Violations cluster around three themes: **(1)** ligand softness (HSAB theory overrides electrostatic trend), **(2)** geometric rigidity (macrocyclic effect penalizes Jahn–Teller-active Cu²⁺), and **(3)** the absence of CFSE for d⁰/d⁵/d¹⁰ configurations (Mn²⁺, Zn²⁺ less depressed than expected). The Jahn–Teller distortion that makes Cu²⁺ supreme with flexible ligands becomes a liability when the ligand field enforces a geometry incompatible with tetragonal elongation — a beautiful example of how electronic structure and molecular geometry conspire to break simple periodic trends.
