### Are they significantly different?

**pKa₁ (carboxyl group): No difference at all.**
Both glycine and alanine have pKa₁ = **2.33** under identical conditions. The carboxyl group is essentially unaffected by the methyl substituent on the α-carbon.

**pKa₂ (ammonium group): Small but real difference.**
Alanine's amino pKa₂ (**9.71**) is **0.14 units higher** than glycine's (**9.57**). This is a modest but chemically meaningful difference — the methyl group in alanine is slightly electron-donating, making the –NH₃⁺ group marginally harder to deprotonate (i.e., the amine is a slightly stronger base). A 0.14-unit difference corresponds to alanine's –NH₂ being about **38% more basic** than glycine's at equilibrium (10^0.14 ≈ 1.38).

### Chemistry Insight

The near-identical pKa₁ values confirm that the α-methyl group of alanine has negligible inductive effect on the distant carboxylate. The slightly elevated pKa₂ of alanine reflects the weak +I (electron-donating) effect of the methyl group stabilizing the protonated amine form. In practice, both amino acids behave very similarly in solution — they share the same zwitterionic form at physiological pH, and their metal-binding behavior is closely comparable, as evidenced by the rich coordination chemistry of glycine (48 VLMs with H⁺ alone) versus the more modest dataset for alanine (19 VLMs).
