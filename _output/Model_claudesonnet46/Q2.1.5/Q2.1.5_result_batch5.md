### Why? The Chelate Effect

This dramatic difference is the textbook **chelate effect**. Ethylenediamine (en) is a bidentate ligand: its two –NH₂ groups simultaneously coordinate to Ni²⁺, forming a stable five-membered chelate ring. This confers:

1. **Entropic advantage** — binding one en molecule releases fewer degrees of freedom than binding two separate NH₃ molecules to occupy the same two coordination sites. The ΔS data confirm this: at I = 0.1 M, ΔS(Ni–en) = +9.2 J mol⁻¹ K⁻¹ vs. ΔS(Ni–NH₃) ≈ +1.7 J mol⁻¹ K⁻¹.
2. **Geometric preorganization** — once one –NH₂ of en is bound, the second is held in close proximity, making ring closure highly favorable.

The chelate effect is why polydentate ligands like EDTA (hexadentate) are used in industrial metal sequestration and analytical chemistry — their log K values can exceed 18 for Ni²⁺, dwarfing even en.

*Source: NIST SRD-46 (metal_112 = Ni²⁺, ligand_10103 = NH₃, ligand_7029 = en; beta_def_812 = ML)*
