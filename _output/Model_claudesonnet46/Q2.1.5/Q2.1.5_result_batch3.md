## Ni²⁺ Binding: Ammonia vs. Ethylenediamine

Here is a direct comparison of the first stepwise stability constant (log K₁) for Ni²⁺ with each ligand at 25 °C, from NIST SRD-46:

| Ligand | Reaction | log K₁ | T (°C) | I (M) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Ammonia (NH₃) | Ni²⁺ + NH₃ ⇌ [Ni(NH₃)]²⁺ | **2.72** | 25 | 0 |
| Ethylenediamine (en) | Ni²⁺ + en ⇌ [Ni(en)]²⁺ | **7.32** | 25 | 0 |

### Δlog K₁ = 7.32 − 2.72 = **+4.60 log units**

**Ethylenediamine binds ~40,000× more strongly** than ammonia to Ni²⁺ (10^4.60 ≈ 40,000).

---

### Why such a large difference?

This is a textbook illustration of the **chelate effect**. Both NH₃ and en donate nitrogen lone pairs to Ni²⁺, so the intrinsic metal–nitrogen bond strength is similar. However, en is a *bidentate* ligand: it forms a **five-membered chelate ring** upon binding, which provides a large **entropic advantage** — one en molecule displaces two NH₃ molecules, releasing two solvent/ligand molecules and increasing the disorder of the system. The thermodynamic data in SRD-46 confirm this: ΔH for Ni²⁺–en is ≈ 0 kJ/mol at 25 °C, while ΔS = +9.2 J/mol·K, meaning the binding is **entropy-driven** — a hallmark of chelation.

> **Source:** NIST SRD-46 (vlm_173185 for NH₃; vlm_122371 for en), both at 25 °C, I = 0 M (aqueous).
