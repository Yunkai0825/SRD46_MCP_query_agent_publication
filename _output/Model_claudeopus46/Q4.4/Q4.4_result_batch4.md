## Reasoning Framework

### Zn²⁺ Coordination Preferences

Zn²⁺ is a **borderline Lewis acid** under HSAB theory. Its coordination preferences in biological and aqueous systems follow this donor-atom hierarchy:

**Thiolate (S⁻) > Imidazole (N) > Carboxylate (O⁻) ≈ Amine (N) >> Amide (O) ≈ Hydroxyl (O) > Thioether (S)**

All α-amino acids form a baseline 5-membered chelate ring through the α-NH₂ and α-COO⁻ groups. What differentiates them for Zn²⁺ binding is the **side-chain donor atom**:

| Donor Type | Example Amino Acids | Expected Relative log β₁ |
|---|---|---|
| Thiolate –S⁻ | Cysteine | Highest (~9–10) |
| Imidazole N | Histidine | Very high (~6.5–7) |
| Phenolate –O⁻ | Tyrosine | High (~5.5–6.5) |
| Extra –COO⁻ | Aspartate, Glutamate | Moderate–High (~5–6) |
| ε-Amino –NH₂ | Lysine | Moderate (~5–5.5) |
| None (N,O only) | Glycine, Ala, Val, etc. | Baseline (~5.0) |
| Amide C=O | Asparagine, Glutamine | Near baseline (~4.5–5) |
| Thioether –S– | Methionine | Near baseline (~4.5–5) |
| Guanidinium | Arginine | Low (resonance-stabilized, poor donor) |

### Identifying the Gap

Cysteine and histidine — the two amino acids with the strongest Zn²⁺ affinity — are almost certainly well-studied with Zn²⁺ given their 134 and 250 total VLM entries respectively, and their central role in zinc biochemistry (zinc fingers, carbonic anhydrase, etc.).

Among the remaining amino acids, the ones most likely to **lack** Zn²⁺ data are those with:
- Low total VLM counts (fewer metal partners studied overall)
- Simple donor sets that don't attract special interest

Candidates most likely unstudied with Zn²⁺ include **arginine** (45 VLM entries, poor guanidinium donor), **glutamine** (71 entries, weak amide donor), **methionine** (63 entries, thioether is a weak Zn²⁺ donor), and **proline** (66 entries, secondary amine but no side-chain donor).

## Prediction

### 🏆 **Tyrosine** — if not yet studied with Zn²⁺ — would form the most stable complex

**Rationale:**

1. **Tridentate coordination potential:** Tyrosine offers three donor atoms — α-NH₂, α-COO⁻, and the phenolate oxygen — enabling formation of both a 5-membered glycinate chelate ring and a second chelate ring through the deprotonated phenol side chain.

2. **Phenolate is a strong anionic donor:** With pKa = 10.10, the phenolate of tyrosine is a much stronger donor than the neutral hydroxyl of serine (no ionizable side chain at accessible pH) or the amide oxygen of asparagine/glutamine. Zn²⁺ binding would promote metal-assisted deprotonation, lowering the effective pKa.

3. **pKa–stability correlation:** Among the studied amino acids, the trend is clear: higher side-chain pKa of the coordinating group correlates with stronger σ-donation once deprotonated. Cysteine's thiolate (pKa 8.18) and histidine's imidazole (pKa 6.05) both provide excellent donors. Tyrosine's phenolate (pKa 10.10) would similarly provide a strong anionic oxygen donor.

4. **Predicted log β₁ ≈ 5.5–6.5:** This places tyrosine above simple glycinate-type amino acids (~5.0) but below cysteine (~9–10) and histidine (~6.5–7), consistent with the O⁻ < N(imidazole) < S⁻ donor hierarchy for Zn²⁺.

### If Tyrosine IS Already Studied

Then **lysine** would be the next strongest candidate among unstudied amino acids. Its ε-amino group (pKa 10.66) is a good nitrogen donor, but the long 4-carbon side chain would form an unfavorable 9-membered chelate ring with the α-amino/carboxylate backbone, significantly reducing the chelate effect. Its predicted log β₁ would be only marginally above glycine (~5.0–5.3).

## Conclusion

> **Tyrosine** is the amino acid most likely to form the strongest Zn²⁺ complex among those not yet studied, owing to its phenolate side-chain donor (pKa 10.10) that enables tridentate N,O,O⁻ coordination. The predicted log β₁ of approximately **5.5–6.5** reflects the strong anionic oxygen donor, positioned between simple glycinate amino acids and the exceptional cysteine/histidine systems that dominate zinc biochemistry.
