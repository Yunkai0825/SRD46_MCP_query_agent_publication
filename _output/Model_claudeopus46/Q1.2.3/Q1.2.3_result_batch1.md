### Chemistry Insights

This 11-node, 55-edge network (every pair of species is thermodynamically linked) beautifully captures the **amphoteric behavior of zinc** in water. Three distinct families of species are present:

1. **Mononuclear hydroxo complexes** (nodes 1–4): As pH increases, Zn²⁺ successively coordinates 1–4 hydroxide ions. The increasingly negative log β values (−8.96 → −40.20) reflect the growing thermodynamic cost of adding each OH⁻. The tetrahydroxo zincate [Zn(OH)₄]²⁻ is the species responsible for zinc's re-dissolution in strongly alkaline solutions — the hallmark of amphoteric behavior.

2. **Polynuclear species** (nodes 5–6): The dinuclear [Zn₂(OH)]³⁺ and the tetranuclear [Zn₄(OH)₄]⁴⁺ form at intermediate pH where partial hydrolysis promotes oligomerization. The [Zn₄(OH)₄]⁴⁺ cluster is known to adopt a cubane-like Zn₄O₄ core — a classic structural motif in zinc coordination chemistry.

3. **Solid phases** (nodes 7–11): Five competing solid phases govern zinc solubility. ZnO(s) has the lowest Ksp (log Ksp = −16.66), making it the thermodynamically most stable solid, while amorphous Zn(OH)₂ (log Ksp = −15.52) is the least stable but often precipitates first due to kinetic factors. The multiple Zn(OH)₂ polymorphs (amorphous, β₁, β₂, δ) illustrate **Ostwald's rule of stages** — metastable phases form first and gradually transform to the most stable ZnO over time.

This network is essential for modeling zinc speciation in environmental waters, corrosion science, and zinc electroplating baths.
