## Zn²⁺ + Hydroxide — 11-Node Equilibrium Network

**Source:** NIST SRD-46, Network #3753 (11 nodes, 55 edges), 25 °C, I = 0 M

This network captures the full aqueous speciation of zinc in the presence of hydroxide, including **solution complexes** and **solid-phase solubility products** for multiple Zn(OH)₂ polymorphs and ZnO.

### Solution Species (log β values)

| Species | Equilibrium | log β |
|---------|-------------|-------|
| Zn(OH)⁺ | Zn²⁺ + OH⁻ ⇌ ZnOH⁺ | −8.96 |
| Zn(OH)₂(aq) | Zn²⁺ + 2 OH⁻ ⇌ Zn(OH)₂ | −17.82 |
| Zn(OH)₃⁻ | Zn²⁺ + 3 OH⁻ ⇌ Zn(OH)₃⁻ | −28.40 |
| Zn(OH)₄²⁻ | Zn²⁺ + 4 OH⁻ ⇌ Zn(OH)₄²⁻ | −41.20 |

### Chemistry Insights

This network beautifully illustrates zinc's **amphoteric** behavior: at low pH, free Zn²⁺ dominates; at moderate pH, insoluble Zn(OH)₂ precipitates; and at high pH, soluble zincate species like Zn(OH)₃⁻ and Zn(OH)₄²⁻ form, re-dissolving the solid. The presence of **six distinct Zn(OH)₂ polymorphs** plus ZnO highlights how crystallographic form affects solubility — the β₂ form is notably more soluble (log Ksp = −14.65) than the thermodynamically most stable ZnO (log Ksp = −15.96), a difference of over one order of magnitude in dissolved zinc concentration. This speciation is critical in environmental chemistry (zinc mobility in soils), corrosion science, and electrochemistry (alkaline batteries rely on ZnO/Zn(OH)₂ equilibria).
