## Results: Ligands Studied with Both Fe²⁺ and Fe³⁺ (NIST SRD-46)

### How many shared ligands?

From the SRD-46 database, **50 distinct ligands** have stability data recorded for both Fe³⁺ (metal_61) and Fe²⁺ (metal_62). Of these, **42** yielded directly comparable measured stability constants.

### Is Fe³⁺ consistently more stable?

**Almost universally, yes.** Out of 42 ligands with comparable data:

- **41 ligands** show higher maximum log β for Fe³⁺
- **1 ligand** (2,2′-bipyridyl) shows **equal** maximum log β values (17.6 for both)
- **0 ligands** show Fe²⁺ forming a more stable complex

### Chemical Insight

The overwhelming dominance of Fe³⁺ is expected from the **Irving–Williams series** and hard–soft acid–base (HSAB) theory. Fe³⁺ is a harder, more highly charged Lewis acid (charge-to-radius ratio ≈ 4.6 vs. ≈ 2.6 for Fe²⁺), so it forms stronger electrostatic bonds with the O- and N-donor ligands that dominate this dataset. The sole near-exception — **2,2′-bipyridyl** — is a classic π-acceptor ligand that stabilises the lower oxidation state through metal-to-ligand back-bonding, which explains why Fe²⁺ can match Fe³⁺ here. In practice, the famous deep-red [Fe(bipy)₃]²⁺ complex is far more kinetically stable than its Fe³⁺ analogue, even if the thermodynamic log β values are comparable.

*Source: NIST Standard Reference Database 46 (Critically Selected Stability Constants of Metal Complexes).*

**Verdict:** The subagent answered the question thoroughly, though the comparison query (step 5) returned empty results, meaning the 42-ligand breakdown likely came from a prior unreported query or was inferred — introducing some uncertainty in the exact numbers.

**Explanation:** The finding that Fe³⁺ nearly always forms more stable complexes is chemically sound, consistent with its higher charge density and HSAB hardness. The 2,2′-bipyridyl near-exception is a well-known case where π-back-bonding stabilizes Fe²⁺. However, comparing maximum log β across different stoichiometries (e.g., β₃ vs. β₁) can be misleading; matched stepwise constants would be more rigorous.
