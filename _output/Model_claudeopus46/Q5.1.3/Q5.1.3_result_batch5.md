**Key differences:**
- **Fe³⁺** is a hard Lewis acid → strong affinity for O-donor ligands (catechols, hydroxamic acids, phenols).
- **Cu²⁺** is a borderline acid → dominates with N-donor ligands (amines, peptides, pyrroles, aza-macrocycles).
- **Fe²⁺** bridges the two, showing significant data for both N-donors (phenanthrolines, bipyridines) and O-donors (EDTA, amino acids).
- **Cu⁺** (soft acid) favours soft donors — pyridines, thia-macrocycles, and inorganic ligands like halides and CN⁻.

## 3. Head-to-Head Stability Constants (log K, 25 °C, aqueous)

### 3a. EDTA (Ethylenedinitrilotetraacetic acid)

Reaction: **M + L ⇌ ML**

| Metal Ion | log K (I = 0.1 M) | log K (I = 1.0 M) |
|-----------|:--:|:--:|
| **Fe³⁺** | **25.1** | 24.23 |
| **Cu²⁺** | **18.78** | — |
| **Fe²⁺** | **14.33** | 14.3 |

Fe³⁺–EDTA is the strongest complex here (log K ≈ 25), consistent with the high charge density of Fe³⁺ and EDTA's six donor atoms wrapping the small, tripositive ion. Cu²⁺ sits ~6 log units lower, and Fe²⁺ another ~4 units below that.

### 3b. Glycine (Aminoacetic acid)

| Reaction | Fe³⁺ (I = 0.1 M) | Cu²⁺ (I = 0.1 M) | Fe²⁺ (I = 0.1 M) |
|----------|:--:|:--:|:--:|
| M + L ⇌ ML (log K₁) | **10.3** | **8.19** | **4.31** |
| M + 2L ⇌ ML₂ (log β₂) | **17.6** | **15.1** | **8.6** |

With glycine, Fe³⁺ again leads due to its higher charge, but the gap to Cu²⁺ narrows considerably (only ~2 log units). Fe²⁺ trails both, reflecting its lower charge and larger ionic radius.

---

## 4. Chemical Insights

1. **Irving–Williams series:** For divalent ions the expected order is Cu²⁺ > Fe²⁺, and the glycine data confirm this (log K₁ = 8.19 vs. 4.31). Cu²⁺ benefits from Jahn–Teller-driven stabilisation of its d⁹ configuration.

2. **Oxidation-state leverage:** Fe³⁺ can surpass Cu²⁺ when hard, polydentate O-donors like EDTA are involved — the +3 charge creates an enormous electrostatic driving force (log K jumps by ~11 units from Fe²⁺ to Fe³⁺ with EDTA).

3. **Practical relevance:** The very high Fe³⁺–EDTA stability (log K ≈ 25) underpins EDTA's widespread use in iron chelation therapy, water treatment, and analytical chemistry. Cu²⁺'s broad ligand repertoire (nearly 2 000 ligands in SRD-46) reflects its central role in bioinorganic chemistry and catalysis.
