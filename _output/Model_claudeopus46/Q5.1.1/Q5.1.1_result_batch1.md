## Key Chemistry Highlights

### 1. Hg(II) — The Dominant Species
Hg²⁺ is by far the most extensively studied mercury species, with **310 ligand partners** and over **1,000 measurements**. As a classic **soft Lewis acid** (Pearson's HSAB theory), Hg(II) shows a strong thermodynamic preference for:

- **S-donor ligands** (thiols, thiolates, sulfide, thiosulfate, dithiocarbamates) — these form the strongest complexes, with log β values often exceeding 20–40 for polydentate thiolate ligands like cysteine, glutathione, and EDTA-type chelators.
- **Halide ligands** — stability follows the soft-acid trend: **I⁻ > Br⁻ > Cl⁻ > F⁻**, the reverse of hard metal ions like Fe³⁺.
- **N-donor ligands** (amines, ammonia, pyridine) — moderate stability.
- **O-donor ligands** (carboxylates, hydroxide) — generally weaker binding.

The 68 distinct β-definitions reflect the rich speciation chemistry of Hg(II), including stepwise complexes (HgCl⁺, HgCl₂, HgCl₃⁻, HgCl₄²⁻), mixed-ligand species, and polynuclear hydroxo complexes.

### 2. Hg(I) — Mercurous Ion
Hg⁺ (which exists as the dinuclear Hg₂²⁺ in solution) has much more limited data — **31 ligands and 83 measurements**. Hg(I) is easily disproportionated to Hg(0) + Hg(II) in the presence of strong complexing agents, which limits the number of stable Hg(I) complexes. Common partners include halides and a few other inorganic anions.

### 3. Organomercury Species
The database tracks three organomercury cations as distinct "metals":

- **MeHg⁺** (methylmercury) is the most studied organomercury species (79 ligands, 148 measurements), reflecting its enormous environmental and toxicological importance. Methylmercury bioaccumulates in aquatic food chains, and its speciation with chloride, hydroxide, and thiolate ligands governs its bioavailability and transport.
- **EtHg⁺** and **PhHg⁺** have much smaller datasets (3–8 ligands each), but are relevant to pharmaceutical (thimerosal/ethylmercury) and industrial chemistry.

These organomercury species are treated as R–Hg⁺ "metal centers" that bind one additional ligand, forming linear R–Hg–L complexes.

## Real-World Relevance

Mercury's soft-acid character makes its coordination chemistry critically important in:
- **Environmental science** — Hg(II)–sulfide and MeHg–thiolate equilibria control mercury mobility in sediments and bioavailability in aquatic systems.
- **Toxicology** — Mercury's affinity for protein thiol groups (cysteine residues) underlies its neurotoxicity.
- **Analytical chemistry** — Hg(II)–halide and Hg(II)–EDTA equilibria are used in complexometric titrations and masking reactions.
