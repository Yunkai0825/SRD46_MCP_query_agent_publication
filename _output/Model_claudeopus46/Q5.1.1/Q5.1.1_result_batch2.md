## Key Chemistry Highlights

### 1. Hg(II) — The Dominant Species (metal_71)
Mercury(II) is by far the most extensively studied, with **310 ligand partners** and over **1,000 measurements**. As a classic **soft Lewis acid** (Pearson's HSAB theory), Hg²⁺ forms its strongest complexes with soft donors:

- **Sulfur donors** (thiolates, thioethers, dithiocarbamates) — extremely high log β values, often exceeding 20–40
- **Halides** — the classic series Cl⁻ < Br⁻ < I⁻ shows *increasing* stability, the reverse of hard metals, reflecting Hg²⁺'s softness
- **Cyanide (CN⁻)** — very strong complexes
- **Nitrogen donors** (amines, amino acids) — moderate to strong
- **Oxygen donors** (carboxylates, hydroxide) — generally weaker

With 68 different β-definitions, the database captures a rich variety of stoichiometries including stepwise (ML, ML₂, ML₃, ML₄) and mixed-ligand or protonated species.

### 2. Hg(I) — The Dimeric Ion (metal_72)
Mercury(I) exists as the **diatomic Hg₂²⁺** cation. With 31 ligand partners and 83 measurements, it is far less studied than Hg(II). Key features:

- Hg₂²⁺ readily **disproportionates** to Hg⁰ + Hg²⁺ in the presence of strong complexing agents (especially S-donors), which limits the range of stable Hg(I) complexes
- **Halide** complexes (Hg₂Cl₂ = calomel) are among the best-known
- The 12 β-definitions reflect the dimeric stoichiometry

### 3. Organomercury Species (MeHg⁺, PhHg⁺, EtHg⁺)
Organomercury cations retain one Hg–C bond and behave as **monovalent soft cations**:

- **MeHg⁺** (metal_93) is the most studied organomercury form (79 ligands, 148 measurements), reflecting its environmental and toxicological importance (bioaccumulation in aquatic food chains)
- **PhHg⁺** and **EtHg⁺** have limited data (8 and 6 measurements respectively)
- These species preferentially bind **thiolate sulfur** (e.g., cysteine, glutathione), which is central to understanding mercury's biological toxicity — MeHg⁺ binds protein thiol groups with very high affinity

---

## Why This Matters

Mercury's strong affinity for sulfur-containing biomolecules (cysteine, glutathione, metallothionein) underpins its **neurotoxicity and bioaccumulation**. The stability constant data in SRD-46 are essential for:

- **Environmental speciation modeling** — predicting mercury's chemical form in natural waters
- **Toxicology** — understanding how MeHg⁺ interacts with biological thiols
- **Analytical chemistry** — designing masking agents and separation schemes
- **Remediation** — selecting chelators for mercury detoxification

The classic **soft–soft interaction** pattern (Hg²⁺ preferring I⁻ > Br⁻ > Cl⁻ > F⁻, and S > N > O donors) is beautifully illustrated across the 310+ ligand systems available for Hg(II) in this database.
