## Key Chemical Insights

1. **Extreme soft-acid character.** Hg²⁺ forms some of the strongest known metal–ligand bonds with sulfide (log β > 50!) and thiolate donors. This underpins mercury's notorious toxicity — it binds tightly to cysteine residues in proteins, disrupting enzyme function.

2. **Halide selectivity.** The halide affinity order I⁻ ≫ Br⁻ > Cl⁻ (log β₄ ≈ 30 vs 22 vs 15) is a textbook illustration of Hard-Soft Acid-Base (HSAB) theory. The very stable HgI₄²⁻ complex is the basis for Nessler's reagent used in ammonia detection.

3. **Cyanide complexes.** Hg(CN)₂ and Hg(CN)₄²⁻ are exceptionally stable (log β₂ ≈ 41), rivaling even the famous Au(CN)₂⁻ system, and are important in analytical and industrial chemistry.

4. **Organomercury species.** MeHg⁺ (79 ligand partners, 148 measurements) is the most data-rich organomercury ion in SRD-46. It is environmentally critical as the bioaccumulating form of mercury in aquatic food chains. Its complexation with thiolates and amino acids is well-documented. EtHg⁺ and PhHg⁺ have more limited data (6 and 8 measurements, respectively).

5. **Hg₂²⁺ (mercurous ion).** With 31 ligand partners and 83 measurements, Hg⁺ data are more limited, reflecting the ion's thermodynamic tendency to disproportionate into Hg⁰ and Hg²⁺. Classic systems include Hg₂Cl₂ (calomel, used in reference electrodes) and Hg₂(CN)₂.

## Summary

Mercury's coordination chemistry in SRD-46 is dominated by Hg²⁺, which has by far the richest dataset (310 ligands). The data beautifully illustrate HSAB principles: sulfur donors like sulfide and thiolates produce extraordinarily high stability constants (log β up to ~52), followed by cyanide (~41), then halides in the order I⁻ > Br⁻ > Cl⁻. Organomercury cations (especially MeHg⁺) add an important environmental dimension, and the smaller Hg⁺ dataset captures the chemistry of the unique dimeric mercurous ion.

*Source: NIST Standard Reference Database 46 (Critically Selected Stability Constants of Metal Complexes).*
