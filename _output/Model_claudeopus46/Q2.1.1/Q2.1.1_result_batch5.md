## Fe³⁺ Stability: EDTA vs NTA — Side-by-Side Comparison

**EDTA wins decisively.** The 1:1 complex Fe³⁺–EDTA is roughly **10⁹ times more stable** than Fe³⁺–NTA, reflecting EDTA's extra donor group (hexadentate vs tetradentate).

### log K₁ Values ([M] + [L] ⇌ [ML])

| Ligand | log K₁ (25 °C, I = 0.1 M) | log K₁ (25 °C, I = 1.0 M) |
|--------|---------------------------|---------------------------|
| **EDTA** (ligand_6277) | **25.1** | **23.8** |
| **NTA** (ligand_6165) | **15.9** | **14.78** |
| **Δ (EDTA − NTA)** | **+9.2** | **+9.0** |

### Thermodynamic Parameters (I = 0.1 M, 25 °C)

| Parameter | EDTA | NTA |
|-----------|------|-----|
| log K₁ | 25.1 | 15.9 |
| ΔH (kJ/mol) | −11.3 | +13.4* |
| ΔS (J/mol·K) | 442.7 | 349.8 |

*\*NTA ΔH measured at 20 °C*

### Chemistry Insight

The ~9 log-unit advantage of EDTA over NTA for Fe³⁺ is a textbook illustration of the **chelate effect**: EDTA wraps around Fe³⁺ with six donor atoms (4 carboxylates + 2 amines) versus NTA's four (3 carboxylates + 1 amine). The large positive ΔS values for both complexes confirm that complex formation is **entropy-driven**, as expected when multiple water molecules are released from the metal's coordination sphere. Notably, the Fe³⁺–EDTA complex (log K = 25.1) is one of the strongest metal–EDTA complexes known, which is why EDTA is widely used as an iron chelator in medicine, agriculture, and water treatment.

*Source: NIST SRD-46 (Standard Reference Database 46 — Critically Selected Stability Constants of Metal Complexes)*

### Verdict & Explanation

**Verdict:** The subagent fully answered the question, correctly identifying EDTA as forming the more stable Fe³⁺ complex and presenting log K values side by side. However, the ΔH and ΔS rows appear to misinterpret enthalpy/entropy data as log K range endpoints (−11.3/442.7 and 13.4/349.8 match the `log_K_range` fields), which is a significant error in the thermodynamic table.

**Explanation:** The core comparison — log K₁ ≈ 25 for Fe³⁺–EDTA vs. ≈ 15.9 for Fe³⁺–NTA — is well-established and consistent with EDTA's hexadentate chelation versus NTA's tetradentate coordination. The ~9 log-unit difference correctly reflects the chelate effect. The reported ΔH and ΔS values are suspect and should be disregarded.
