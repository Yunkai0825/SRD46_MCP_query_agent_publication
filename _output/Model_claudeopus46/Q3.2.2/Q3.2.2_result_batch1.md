## Trend Analysis

**Yes, there is a clear and consistent trend: all stability constants decrease monotonically with increasing ionic strength.**

| Species | log K/β at I = 0 | log K/β at I = 2 M | Total drop | Approx. drop per unit I |
|---------|------------------|---------------------|------------|------------------------|
| ML      | 7.90             | 7.08                | −0.82      | ~0.4 per M             |
| ML₂     | 14.53            | 13.04               | −1.49      | ~0.7 per M             |
| ML₃     | 19.18            | 17.12               | −2.06      | ~1.0 per M             |

The decline is smooth and approximately linear above I ≈ 0.5 M, with a steeper initial drop between I = 0 and 0.1 M. This shape is consistent with Debye–Hückel behavior at low ionic strength transitioning to a more gradual specific-ion interaction regime at higher I.

---

## Chemical Insight

This trend is expected from electrostatic and activity-coefficient theory. Although ethylenediamine is a neutral ligand, increasing ionic strength raises the activity coefficient of the Ni²⁺ ion (via salting-out and ion-atmosphere effects) and modifies the solvation environment, effectively destabilizing the complex relative to the separated species. The effect **scales with the number of ligands**: β₃ drops about 2.5× more than K₁ over the same ionic strength range, because the cumulative constant compounds the per-step ionic-strength sensitivity. This is a textbook illustration of why reporting the ionic strength alongside stability constants is essential for meaningful thermodynamic comparisons.

It is also worth noting that the data span two different background electrolytes (KNO₃ at lower I, NaClO₄ at higher I). While both are considered "inert" media, the seamless continuation of the downward trend across them suggests the ionic-strength effect dominates over specific medium effects in this system.
