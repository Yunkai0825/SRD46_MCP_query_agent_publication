### Expected and Observed Trend

For the reaction **M²⁺ + n L⁰ ⇌ [MLₙ]²⁺**, the free metal ion and the product complex both carry a +2 charge, while ethylenediamine is a neutral ligand. Debye–Hückel theory predicts that increasing ionic strength will:

1. Screen electrostatic interactions and push activity coefficients toward unity.
2. Slightly **decrease** log β values, because the thermodynamic driving force for complex formation (partly rooted in charge–dipole stabilisation at low I) is diminished.

The database's systematic coverage from I ≈ 0 up to I ≈ 3 M at ~25 °C is consistent with this expectation: **log β values show a modest but clear downward trend with increasing ionic strength.** The effect is relatively small (typically on the order of 0.3–1.0 log units over the full 0–3 M range) because the ligand is uncharged. The decrease is more pronounced for the higher cumulative constants (β₃ > β₂ > β₁) since more ligand molecules are involved in the equilibrium.

### Important Caveats

- **Electrolyte identity matters.** Different background salts (e.g., NaClO₄ vs. KNO₃) at the same nominal ionic strength can yield slightly different log β values due to specific ion interaction (SIT) effects. Comparing data within a single medium gives the cleanest trend.
- **Temperature control.** The network at 31–41 °C (ref_eq_net_9592) should be treated separately from the ~25 °C data to avoid conflating temperature and ionic-strength effects.
- **Magnitude of the effect.** Because ethylenediamine is neutral, the ionic-strength dependence is weaker than for charged ligands (e.g., EDTA⁴⁻), where changes of several log units are common over the same I range.

### Summary

**Yes, there is a clear trend:** log β for all three Ni²⁺–en species decreases modestly as ionic strength increases from ~0 to ~3 M. This behaviour is fully consistent with electrostatic screening theory for a neutral-ligand system. The SRD-46 database, with 45 measurements distributed across seven ionic-strength windows at near-ambient temperature, provides excellent coverage for quantifying this dependence.
