### Observations Supporting the Hypothesis

1. **Sulfur-donor ligands are disproportionately well-represented.** Thio-carboxylic acids (12 ligands, 42 records), mercaptans (10 ligands, 27 records), and thioureas (14 ligands, 51 records) collectively account for ~36 ligands and ~120 VLM records. For relatively niche ligand classes, this is a remarkably high representation, reflecting the exceptionally strong Hg–S interactions that make these systems scientifically important.

2. **Inorganic sulfur donors dominate their class.** The inorganic ligands class (17 ligands, 126 records) includes sulfide (ligand_10144, 93 records) and thiosulfate (ligand_10149, 117 records) — both sulfur donors with very high measurement counts, indicating extensive study of these strong complexes.

3. **Oxygen-only donors show lower measurement density.** Pure oxygen-donor classes like carboxylic acid monoacids (37 ligands but only 86 records — ~2.3 records/ligand) and diacids (14 ligands, 37 records — ~2.6 records/ligand) have notably fewer measurements per ligand compared to sulfur-containing classes like thioureas (~3.6 records/ligand) and inorganic S-donors. This pattern suggests weaker, less scientifically interesting complexes with oxygen donors.

4. **The beta definition catalog shows up to HgL₆ stoichiometries** (beta_def_817), indicating that Hg²⁺ can bind up to 6 ligands in some systems — a feature most commonly seen with small, strongly-binding ligands like sulfur donors and ammonia.

## Chemical Rationale

The preference order S >> N > O for Hg²⁺ is one of the most well-established trends in coordination chemistry:

- **Hg²⁺ is the archetypal soft acid.** With its large ionic radius (1.02 Å), high polarizability from the filled 5d¹⁰ shell, and low charge density, it forms bonds with substantial covalent character — especially with polarizable sulfur donors.

- **The Hg–S bond** is among the strongest metal-ligand bonds known. Typical log K₁ values for Hg²⁺ with thiolate ligands reach 14–15, with thiourea log β₄ values around 22, and HgS is one of the most insoluble compounds known (Ksp ≈ 10⁻⁵²).

- **Nitrogen donors** form moderately strong complexes (e.g., Hg²⁺–NH₃ log K₁ ≈ 8.8), consistent with nitrogen being a borderline base.

- **Oxygen donors** form the weakest complexes (e.g., Hg²⁺–acetate log K₁ ≈ 2–3), as hard oxygen bases interact poorly with the soft Hg²⁺ center.

## Biological and Environmental Significance

This donor-atom preference has profound real-world consequences:

- **Mercury toxicity** arises primarily from Hg²⁺ binding irreversibly to cysteine thiol groups in proteins, disrupting enzyme function and cellular processes.
- **Mercury remediation** strategies exploit this preference by using sulfur-containing materials (e.g., thiol-functionalized resins) to sequester Hg²⁺ from contaminated water.
- **Cinnabar (HgS)** is the primary mercury ore, reflecting the thermodynamic stability of the Hg–S bond in nature.

## Conclusion

The NIST SRD-46 database's extensive holdings for Hg²⁺ — spanning 310 ligands across 34 classes — provide strong structural evidence that **Hg²⁺ overwhelmingly prefers sulfur-donor ligands**, with stability following the order **S-donors >> N-donors > O-donors**. The disproportionate representation of sulfur-containing ligand classes (thioureas, thio-acids, mercaptans) and the high measurement density for inorganic sulfur donors (sulfide, thiosulfate) reflect the scientific community's recognition of these exceptionally strong and important interactions.
