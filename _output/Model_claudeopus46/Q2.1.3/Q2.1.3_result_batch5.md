*Source: NIST SRD-46 ligand card reference pKa brackets.*

### Are the differences significant?

- **pKa₁ (carboxyl group):** Identical at **2.33**. The α-carboxyl acidity is essentially the same for both amino acids. This makes chemical sense — in both cases, the carboxyl group is adjacent to an α-amino group and the inductive effect on the COOH is very similar regardless of whether the side chain is –H (glycine) or –CH₃ (alanine).

- **pKa₂ (amino group):** Alanine's value is **0.14 units higher** than glycine's (9.71 vs. 9.57). This difference is small but **real and chemically meaningful** — it exceeds typical experimental uncertainty (±0.02–0.05 pKa units). The methyl group on alanine is weakly electron-donating (hyperconjugation/inductive effect), which slightly stabilizes the protonated ammonium form (–NH₃⁺), making the amino group a marginally weaker acid (higher pKa).

### Summary

The two simplest α-amino acids have **nearly identical** protonation behavior. The carboxyl pKa values are indistinguishable, while the amino pKa differs by only ~0.14 units — a small but reproducible effect attributable to the electron-donating methyl substituent in alanine. For most practical purposes (buffer design, speciation modeling), glycine and alanine can be treated as interchangeable in terms of acid-base chemistry.
