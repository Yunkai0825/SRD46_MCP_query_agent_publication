# pKa Comparison: Glycine vs. Alanine (α-Alanine)

Both glycine and alanine are α-amino acids with two ionizable groups — a carboxyl group (–COOH) and a protonated amino group (–NH₃⁺). Their protonation equilibria follow the scheme:

**H₂L⁺ ⇌ HL± + H⁺** (pKa₁, carboxyl)
**HL± ⇌ L⁻ + H⁺** (pKa₂, amino)

## Are the Differences Significant?

- **pKa₁ (carboxyl):** The values are **identical at 2.33** — no difference at all. This makes chemical sense: the carboxyl group in both amino acids is in a very similar electronic environment (α to the amino group), and the methyl substituent on alanine's α-carbon is too far from –COOH to exert a meaningful inductive effect.

- **pKa₂ (amino):** Alanine's value is **0.14 units higher** than glycine's (9.71 vs. 9.57). This small difference is **chemically real but modest**. The electron-donating methyl group on alanine's α-carbon slightly increases electron density at the nitrogen, making the ammonium group a marginally weaker acid (higher pKa). However, a Δ of 0.14 log units is within the range of experimental scatter seen across different ionic strengths and electrolyte media — typical measurement uncertainties for amino acid pKa values are ±0.05–0.15 units.

## Chemical Insight

The near-identical pKa values of glycine and alanine reflect the fact that a single –CH₃ group is a weak electron donor with limited influence on distant ionizable groups. This contrasts sharply with amino acids bearing strongly electron-withdrawing or aromatic side chains — for example, **phenylalanine** (also found in SRD-46 as ligand\_5777) has pKa₁ = 2.18 and pKa₂ = 9.09, both noticeably lower, due to the electron-withdrawing phenyl ring. Meanwhile, **β-alanine** (ligand\_5788), where the amino and carboxyl groups are separated by three carbons instead of two, shows dramatically different values (pKa₁ = 3.51, pKa₂ = 10.08), highlighting the importance of the distance between ionizable groups.

**Bottom line:** Glycine and alanine have **essentially the same pKa₁** and only a **marginally different pKa₂** (Δ = 0.14). The differences are **not significant** for most practical purposes in coordination chemistry or biochemistry.
