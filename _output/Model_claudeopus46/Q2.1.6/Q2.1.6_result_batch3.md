## Ranking Cd²⁺, Pb²⁺, and Hg²⁺ by log K₁ with Thiocyanate (SCN⁻)

Using comparable conditions (25 °C, aqueous, I → 0 or lowest available ionic strength) from NIST SRD-46:

| Metal | log K₁ | T (°C) | I (mol/L) | VLM ID |
|-------|--------|--------|-----------|--------|
| **Hg²⁺** | **9.64** | 25 | 0 | vlm_172457 |
| **Cd²⁺** | **1.93** | 25 | 0 | vlm_172411 |
| **Pb²⁺** | **0.49** | 25 | 1* | vlm_172483 |

\* No I = 0 log K value available for Pb²⁺; the lowest ionic strength entry (I = 1 M) is shown.

### Ranking (highest → lowest affinity):

> **Hg²⁺ ≫ Cd²⁺ > Pb²⁺**

### Which soft metal has the highest affinity?

**Mercury(II)** — by a very large margin. Its log K₁ of **9.64** is nearly 8 orders of magnitude larger than Cd²⁺ (1.93) and over 9 orders of magnitude larger than Pb²⁺ (0.49).

### Chemical Insight

This ranking is a textbook illustration of **HSAB (Hard-Soft Acid-Base) theory**. Thiocyanate coordinates through its soft sulfur donor to soft metal ions. Hg²⁺ is the quintessential soft acid (high polarizability, low charge density, d¹⁰ configuration), giving it an extraordinarily strong affinity for the soft S-end of SCN⁻. Cd²⁺ is borderline-soft and shows moderate binding, while Pb²⁺ — though large and polarizable — has a less favorable orbital match, resulting in the weakest complex. The strongly negative ΔH for Hg²⁺–SCN⁻ (−49 kJ/mol) combined with a positive ΔS (+20 J/mol·K) indicates both a strong covalent Hg–S bond and favorable desolvation entropy, driving the exceptionally high stability.

*Source: NIST Standard Reference Database 46 (Critically Selected Stability Constants of Metal Complexes).*
