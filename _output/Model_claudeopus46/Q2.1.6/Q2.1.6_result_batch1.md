Here is the ranking based on NIST SRD-46 data for the stepwise formation constant **log K₁** of M²⁺ + SCN⁻ ⇌ M(SCN)⁺ at **25 °C**:

## Ranking: log K₁ with Thiocyanate (SCN⁻)

| Metal | log K₁ | Conditions (T, I) | Source |
|-------|--------|--------------------|--------|
| **Hg²⁺** | **9.64** | 25 °C, I = 0 M | SRD-46 (vlm_172457) |
| Cd²⁺ | 1.93 | 25 °C, I = 0 M | SRD-46 (vlm_172411) |
| Pb²⁺ | 1.08* | 25 °C, I = 4 M | SRD-46 (vlm_172486) |

\* No I = 0 value available for Pb²⁺; at I = 1 M, log K₁ = 0.49. Even extrapolated to zero ionic strength, Pb²⁺ remains the weakest binder.


**Hg²⁺ ≫ Cd²⁺ > Pb²⁺**

**Mercury(II) has by far the highest affinity for thiocyanate**, with a log K₁ of **9.64** — nearly 8 orders of magnitude larger than those of Cd²⁺ (1.93) and Pb²⁺ (~0.5–1.1).

### Chemistry Insight

This result is a textbook illustration of **HSAB (Hard-Soft Acid-Base) theory**. Thiocyanate coordinates through its soft sulfur donor to soft metal ions. Hg²⁺ is the softest of the three d-block/p-block cations, so it forms an exceptionally strong Hg–SCN bond (log K₁ ≈ 9.6). Cd²⁺ is borderline-soft and binds moderately, while Pb²⁺ — a harder borderline acid — shows the weakest interaction. The very large, negative ΔH for Hg²⁺ (−49 kJ/mol at I = 0) confirms that the driving force is predominantly enthalpic, consistent with strong covalent Hg–S bonding.
