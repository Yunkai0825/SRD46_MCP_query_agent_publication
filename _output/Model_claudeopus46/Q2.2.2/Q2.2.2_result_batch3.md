Based on the NIST SRD-46 database, **Cu²⁺ (copper(II))** is the metal ion with the largest number of different ligands studied, with **1,951 distinct ligands**.

> **Note:** If H⁺ (the proton) is counted, it tops the list with **4,081 ligands**, reflecting the vast body of protonation/pKa data in the database. However, H⁺ is not a metal ion in the traditional sense, so **Cu²⁺** is the clear leader among true metal ions.

### Chemistry Insight

Cu²⁺'s dominance is unsurprising — its d⁹ electronic configuration and Jahn–Teller-active geometry allow it to form stable complexes with an extraordinary range of N-, O-, and S-donor ligands. It is central to bioinorganic chemistry (blue copper proteins, superoxide dismutase), environmental speciation studies, and industrial catalysis, driving extensive thermodynamic characterization. Ni²⁺, the runner-up with 1,369 ligands, is also a coordination chemistry workhorse, but Cu²⁺ leads by a substantial margin (~42% more ligands).

*Source: NIST Standard Reference Database 46 (Critically Selected Stability Constants of Metal Complexes).*

### Verdict & Explanation

**Verdict:** The subagent answered the question correctly, appropriately distinguishing H⁺ (4,081 ligands) from true metal ions and identifying Cu²⁺ (1,951 ligands) as the top metal ion.

**Explanation:** Cu²⁺'s dominance reflects its versatile coordination chemistry — flexible geometry, Jahn–Teller distortion, and affinity for diverse donor atoms (N, O, S) make it ubiquitous in stability constant studies. The large gap between Cu²⁺ and Ni²⁺ (1,369) underscores copper's outsized role in bioinorganic, environmental, and analytical chemistry. The H⁺ caveat is scientifically appropriate since protonation equilibria are fundamentally different from metal-ligand complexation.
