### Chemistry Insight

The stability trend follows the **denticity and number of carboxylate donor groups**. EDDP — a hexadentate EDTA analogue with two propionate and two acetate arms — tops the list because its larger chelate rings better accommodate the large La³⁺ ion (ionic radius ~1.03 Å). EDTA, the classic hexadentate chelator, is close behind at log K₁ ≈ 10.3. NTA, with only three carboxylate arms and one nitrogen, drops by about 2 log units. Simple dicarboxylates like malonic acid, which lack nitrogen donors and form fewer chelate rings, bind La³⁺ much more weakly (log K₁ ≈ 4.4). This hierarchy underpins practical lanthanide separation chemistry, where EDTA and related polyaminocarboxylates serve as selective complexants in ion-exchange chromatography and hydrometallurgical processes.
