### 🏆 Winner: **L-Cysteine**

**Cysteine is the clear champion**, with a selectivity ratio of roughly **10^9.5 ≈ 3 billion-fold** preference for Cu²⁺ over Ni²⁺. This enormous selectivity arises from three factors:

1. **Thiolate–Cu²⁺ affinity**: Cu²⁺ is a borderline-soft Lewis acid with exceptionally strong affinity for the soft thiolate sulfur donor in cysteine. The log β ≈ 18–19 reflects simultaneous coordination by –NH₂, –COO⁻, and –S⁻ donors.

2. **Ni²⁺ is harder**: Ni²⁺ is a harder Lewis acid that binds thiolate much less effectively, giving log β ≈ 9 — respectable, but ~9.5 orders of magnitude weaker than Cu²⁺.

3. **pH 7 is favorable**: At pH 7, cysteine's thiol group (pKₐ ≈ 8.2) is mostly protonated in free solution, but Cu²⁺ is strong enough to strip the thiol proton well below the pKₐ, while Ni²⁺ competes far less effectively. The carboxylate (pKₐ ≈ 1.9) is fully deprotonated and the amino group (pKₐ ≈ 10.3) remains protonated.

### Why Histidine Is *Not* the Best Choice Here

Histidine has the highest absolute log β values among the non-thiol amino acids (Cu²⁺ ~10.1, Ni²⁺ ~8.6), thanks to its imidazole nitrogen donor. However, its **selectivity is actually the worst** (Δlog β ≈ 1.5) because the imidazole is a borderline-hard nitrogen donor that Ni²⁺ also binds very well. Similarly, aspartic acid (Δlog β ≈ 1.7) has poor selectivity because both carboxylate oxygens coordinate both metals comparably.

### Practical Caveat

While cysteine offers extraordinary thermodynamic selectivity, Cu²⁺ can **oxidize** cysteine to cystine (the disulfide), consuming the ligand. In practical applications, consider:
- Working under **inert atmosphere** (N₂ or Ar)
- Using **D-penicillamine** (β,β-dimethylcysteine), a redox-stable thiol amino acid analogue that retains the soft-donor selectivity

If you need a **redox-stable, non-thiol** alternative, **L-proline** (Δlog β ≈ 3.1) offers the best selectivity, likely because its secondary amine and rigid pyrrolidine ring create a geometry that Cu²⁺ (which favors square-planar) accommodates better than Ni²⁺.

*Source: NIST Standard Reference Database 46 (Critically Selected Stability Constants of Metal Complexes)*
