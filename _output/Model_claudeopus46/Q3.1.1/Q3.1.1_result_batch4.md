## Clinically Used Chelators — Summary

Four ligands in the database are established clinical chelators for lead poisoning:

1. **CaNa₂EDTA** (log K₁ = 18.0) — Administered intravenously; the calcium salt is used to prevent hypocalcemia. It is the gold standard for acute and severe lead poisoning.
2. **DMSA / Succimer** (log K₁ = 17.4) — The preferred oral chelator, especially in children. Its two thiol groups confer strong Pb²⁺ affinity with good selectivity over essential metals like Zn²⁺ and Ca²⁺.
3. **BAL / Dimercaprol** (log K₁ = 14.5) — Used intramuscularly in lead encephalopathy, often combined with CaNa₂EDTA. Despite a lower log K₁ than EDTA, its dithiol motif provides kinetic advantages and selectivity for soft/borderline metals.
4. **D-Penicillamine** (log K₁ = 12.2) — An oral option, but less favored due to side effects (nephrotoxicity, hypersensitivity). More commonly used for copper chelation in Wilson's disease.

---

## Why the Strongest Binders Aren't Used Clinically

**PhDTA** and **CyDTA** show the highest thermodynamic affinity (log K₁ > 19.5), but they are not used clinically because they lack selectivity — they chelate essential metals (Ca²⁺, Zn²⁺, Fe³⁺) almost as avidly as Pb²⁺. They also have poor oral bioavailability and unfavorable pharmacokinetics. Clinical chelation requires a balance of **affinity**, **selectivity**, **bioavailability**, and **safety** — not just the highest log K.

---

## Chemistry Insight

Pb²⁺ is a borderline-soft Lewis acid, which explains why thiol-containing ligands (DMSA, BAL, penicillamine) are effective despite having lower log K₁ values than aminocarboxylates. The soft S-donor atoms provide **kinetic selectivity** for Pb²⁺ over hard divalent cations like Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺, which is critical for minimizing depletion of essential minerals during therapy. DMSA's combination of two thiol groups and two carboxylates gives it an excellent balance of high Pb²⁺ affinity and low toxicity, making it the current standard for outpatient oral chelation.

> **Note:** DMPS (2,3-dimercapto-1-propanesulfonic acid / Unithiol), another clinical chelator used in some countries, was not found in the SRD-46 database under that name and may not be included in this edition.
