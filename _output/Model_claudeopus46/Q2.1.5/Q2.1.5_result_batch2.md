## Ethylenediamine binds Ni²⁺ far more strongly than ammonia

The NIST SRD-46 database provides matched first-formation constants (log K₁) for the reaction **[Ni²⁺] + [L] ⇌ [NiL]²⁺** at 25 °C:

| Ligand | I (mol/L) | log K₁ | vlm_id |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ammonia (NH₃) | 0 | 2.72 | vlm_173185 |
| Ethylenediamine (en) | 0 | 7.32 | vlm_122371 |
| Ammonia (NH₃) | 0.1 | 2.73 | vlm_173182 |
| Ethylenediamine (en) | 0.1 | 7.30 | vlm_122365 |

### Difference in log K₁

| Condition | Δ log K₁ (en − NH₃) |
|---|---|
| I = 0 M, 25 °C | **+4.60** |
| I = 0.1 M, 25 °C | **+4.57** |

**Ethylenediamine binds Ni²⁺ roughly 10⁴·⁶ ≈ 40 000 times more strongly** than ammonia in the first coordination step.

### Why such a large difference?

This is a textbook illustration of the **chelate effect**. Ethylenediamine is a bidentate ligand that forms a five-membered chelate ring with Ni²⁺, gaining a large entropic advantage over monodentate NH₃. The thermodynamic data confirm this: at I = 0.1 M the Ni–en complex has ΔS = +9.2 cal mol⁻¹ K⁻¹ versus only +1.7 cal mol⁻¹ K⁻¹ for Ni–NH₃, while the enthalpy for Ni–en is near zero (ΔH ≈ 0 kJ mol⁻¹) compared to ΔH = −15.1 kJ mol⁻¹ for Ni–NH₃. The stability gain is therefore **entropy-driven**, consistent with the release of additional solvent molecules when a single chelating ligand replaces two monodentate ones.

*Source: NIST Standard Reference Database 46 (Critically Selected Stability Constants of Metal Complexes).*

### Verdict & Explanation

**Verdict:** The subagent fully answered the question with well-matched log K₁ values and a clear quantitative difference.

**Explanation:** Ethylenediamine binds Ni²⁺ ~4.6 log K units more strongly than ammonia (log K₁ ≈ 7.3 vs. 2.7), consistent across ionic strengths. This ~40,000-fold enhancement is the classic chelate effect — the entropic gain from bidentate chelation. The thermodynamic breakdown (entropy-driven stabilization) is scientifically sound. One caveat: comparing a bidentate ligand's K₁ with a monodentate ligand's K₁ is standard but not a per-donor-atom comparison; a fairer comparison would use β₂ for ammonia (two NH₃ replacing one en).
