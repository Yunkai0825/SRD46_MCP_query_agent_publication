## Ranking of First-Row Divalent Transition Metals by log β₁ with Ammonia

All values below are from **NIST SRD-46**, for the equilibrium **M²⁺ + NH₃ ⇌ [M(NH₃)]²⁺** (β₁ = [ML]/[M][L]) in water at **25 °C, I = 0 M** (infinite dilution reference state).

| Metal | log β₁ | vlm_id |
|-------|--------|--------|
| Mn²⁺ | **1.00** | vlm_173099 |
| Co²⁺ | **2.11** | vlm_173133 |
| Zn²⁺ | **2.37** | vlm_173205 |
| Ni²⁺ | **2.80** | vlm_173181 |
| Cu²⁺ | **4.31** | vlm_173157 |

> **Fe²⁺** — No log β₁ entry was found at I ≤ 0.5 M in the database. This is unsurprising: Fe²⁺–NH₃ complexes in aqueous solution are very weak and the Fe²⁺/Fe³⁺ redox couple makes oxidation a persistent experimental problem, so reliable measurements are scarce.

### Observed ordering

**Mn²⁺ (1.00) < Co²⁺ (2.11) < Zn²⁺ (2.37) < Ni²⁺ (2.80) < Cu²⁺ (4.31)**

### Comparison with the Irving–Williams Series

The classic **Irving–Williams series** predicts:

> Mn²⁺ < Fe²⁺ < Co²⁺ < Ni²⁺ < **Cu²⁺** > Zn²⁺

| Feature | Irving–Williams prediction | SRD-46 data | Match? |
|---------|---------------------------|-------------|--------|
| Mn²⁺ weakest | Lowest of the series | log β₁ = 1.00 (lowest) | ✅ |
| Co²⁺ < Ni²⁺ | Monotonic increase | 2.11 < 2.80 | ✅ |
| Cu²⁺ is the maximum | Strongest complex | 4.31 (highest) | ✅ |
| Cu²⁺ > Zn²⁺ | Drop after Cu | 4.31 > 2.37 | ✅ |
| Zn²⁺ < Ni²⁺ | Zn falls back below Ni | 2.37 < 2.80 | ✅ |

**Yes — the ammonia log β₁ values follow the Irving–Williams series.** Every testable prediction of the series is confirmed by the SRD-46 data. The characteristic "hump" at Cu²⁺ is striking: its log β₁ is nearly **1.5 log units** above the next strongest binder (Ni²⁺) and more than **3 log units** above Mn²⁺. This reflects the Jahn–Teller-enhanced ligand-field stabilisation energy (LFSE) of the d⁹ Cu²⁺ ion, which provides extra thermodynamic driving force for complex formation. The subsequent drop to Zn²⁺ (d¹⁰, zero LFSE) is textbook behaviour — Zn²⁺ relies solely on electrostatic and covalent contributions without any crystal-field bonus.

The absence of Fe²⁺ data is itself consistent with the series: Fe²⁺–NH₃ binding is expected to be weak (log β₁ somewhere between Mn²⁺ and Co²⁺), and the experimental difficulty of working with air-sensitive Fe²⁺ solutions explains the data gap.

*Source: NIST Standard Reference Database 46 (Critically Selected Stability Constants of Metal Complexes).*
