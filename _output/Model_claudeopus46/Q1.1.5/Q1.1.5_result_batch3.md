## pKa Values for Oxalic Acid (SRD-46)

Oxalic acid (ethanedioic acid, H₂C₂O₄) is a **diprotic acid** with **two dissociation steps**:

| Step | Equilibrium | pKa | T (°C) | I (mol/L) |
|------|-------------|-----|---------|-----------|
| 1 | H₂C₂O₄ ⇌ HC₂O₄⁻ + H⁺ | **−1.2** | 25 | 0.1 |
| 2 | HC₂O₄⁻ ⇌ C₂O₄²⁻ + H⁺ | **3.8** | 25 | 0.1 |

*(Source: NIST SRD-46, ligand_8872)*

### Chemistry Insight

The very low (negative) first pKa reflects the strong acidity of the first proton — oxalic acid is one of the strongest organic acids, owing to the electron-withdrawing effect of the adjacent carboxyl group. The second dissociation (pKa₂ ≈ 3.8) is much weaker because removing a proton from the already negatively charged hydrogen oxalate ion is less favorable. This large gap (~5 pKa units) between the two steps is characteristic of small dicarboxylic acids and makes oxalate an effective chelating ligand for many metal ions (it forms stable bidentate complexes with Ca²⁺, Fe³⁺, Al³⁺, and others).
