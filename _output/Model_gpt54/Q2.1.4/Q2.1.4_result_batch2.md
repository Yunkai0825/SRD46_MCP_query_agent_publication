**Prompt:** Compare the stability constants of Cu²⁺ with chloride ion vs bromide ion. Which halide forms a stronger copper complex?

Using the matched **1:1 Cu²⁺–halide complex** data (`[M] + [L] <=> [ML]`, `beta_def_812`) in SRD-46, **chloride forms the stronger Cu²⁺ complex than bromide** under comparable conditions.

| Ionic strength, I (M) | T (°C) | logK(CuCl⁺) | logK(CuBr⁺) | Stronger halide |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---|
| 0 | 25 | 0.20 | -0.04 | Chloride |
| 1 | 25 | -0.20 | -0.64 | Chloride |
| 2 | 25 | -0.10 | -0.49 | Chloride |
| 3 | 25 | 0.00 | -0.40 | Chloride |
| 4 | 25 | 0.11 | -0.22 | Chloride |

A few takeaways:

| Observation | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| Chloride has the larger logK at every matched ionic strength | Cu²⁺ binds Cl⁻ more strongly than Br⁻ in these SRD-46 measurements |
| Both sets are weak complexes overall | logK values are near zero or negative, so neither halide forms a very strong 1:1 Cu²⁺ complex in water |
| The chloride advantage is modest but consistent | The difference is about 0.3–0.5 log units across the matched conditions |

So the direct answer is: **Cl⁻ forms the stronger copper(II) complex than Br⁻**.

Source: **NIST SRD-46** stability data for `Cu^[2+]` with **Chloride ion** (`ligand_10163`) and **Bromide ion** (`ligand_10168`).

### Verdict & Explanation

**Verdict**  
The subagent fully answered the question. It directly compared matched Cu²⁺–halide 1:1 stability constants and identified chloride as stronger than bromide.

**Explanation**  
For aqueous Cu²⁺ + X⁻ ⇌ CuX⁺, the reported logK values are consistently higher for Cl⁻ than for Br⁻ across comparable ionic strengths, so CuCl⁺ is thermodynamically more stable than CuBr⁺. The constants are small (near zero or negative under many conditions), meaning both are weak monohalide complexes in water; the chloride preference is real but modest, about 0.3–0.5 log units.
