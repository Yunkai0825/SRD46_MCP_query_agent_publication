# Q2.1.4 -- Result (batch 4)

**Section:** 2.1 -- Comparison & Ranking

**Prompt:** Compare the stability constants of Cu²⁺ with chloride ion vs bromide ion. Which halide forms a stronger copper complex?

**Tool calls:** 11  |  **Time:** 93s planning + 76s execution + 15s verdict = 183s total

---

Using the SRD-46 data for the same equilibrium definition, **β₁ = [ML]/[M][L]** for **Cu²⁺ + X⁻ ⇌ CuX⁺** at **25 °C**, **chloride forms the stronger Cu(II) complex than bromide**.

| Ionic strength, I / M | logK(Cu²⁺ + Cl⁻ ⇌ CuCl⁺) | logK(Cu²⁺ + Br⁻ ⇌ CuBr⁺) | Stronger halide |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| 0 | 0.20 | -0.04 | Chloride |
| 1 | -0.20 | -0.64 | Chloride |
| 2 | -0.10 | -0.49 | Chloride |
| 3 | 0.00 | -0.40 | Chloride |
| 4 | 0.11 | -0.22 | Chloride |

A few additional chloride values in SRD-46 extend to higher ionic strength:

| Ionic strength, I / M | logK(CuCl⁺) |
|---|---:|
| 0.5 | -0.20 |
| 0.7 | -0.20 |
| 5 | 0.17 |
| 6 | 0.27 |

So the answer is: **Cl⁻ binds Cu²⁺ more strongly than Br⁻** in the available SRD-46 measurements.

Chemically, that trend is reasonable: Cu²⁺ is a relatively hard/borderline Lewis acid, and **chloride is a better match than the softer, larger bromide ion**. The absolute binding is weak in both cases here, since the reported **logK values are near zero or negative** under these aqueous conditions.

**Source:** NIST SRD-46 stability data for `metal_41` (Cu²⁺) with `ligand_10163` (chloride ion) and `ligand_10168` (bromide ion), β definition `beta_def_812`.
