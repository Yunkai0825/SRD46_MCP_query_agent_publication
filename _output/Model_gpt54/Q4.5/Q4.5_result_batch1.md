**Prompt:** Examine the relationship between ligand pKa values and their stability constants with Ca²⁺. Is there a correlation? Formulate a hypothesis about how basicity relates to binding strength for alkaline-earth metals.

Using SRD-46 data for a matched Ca²⁺ set, the correlation is **real but only loose/moderate**.

| Ligand | Representative pKa values (25 °C, I≈0.1 M) | Ca²⁺ ML logK |
|---|---:|---:|
| Glycine | 2.33, 9.57 | 1.40 |
| Oxalate | -1.2, 3.8 | 3.22 |
| Tartrate | 2.82, 3.97 | 2.97 |
| Citrate | 2.9, 4.35, 5.65 | 3.50 |
| NTA | -1.81, -1.0, 2.52, 9.46 | 6.41 |
| EDTA | -1.5, 2.0, 2.69, 6.13, 9.52 | 10.65 |

### What the data suggest

| Observation | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| EDTA and NTA have the highest Ca²⁺ stability constants | More basic donor sets help, but **multidentate chelation** is the dominant reason they bind so strongly |
| Citrate binds more strongly than tartrate or glycine | Higher **overall negative charge** and greater **denticity** strengthen Ca²⁺ binding |
| Glycine has a high pKa (~9.6) but weak Ca²⁺ binding | Its high pKa is mainly **amine basicity**, which does **not** translate into strong Ca–O binding by itself |
| Oxalate and tartrate have modest pKa values and moderate logK values | Among simple O-donors, there is some positive relation between deprotonation/basicity and binding, but it is not strict |

### Is there a correlation?
Yes, but it is **not a simple linear pKa → logK relationship** across mixed ligand classes.

A rough trend is:

| Trend | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Higher effective ligand basicity often corresponds to higher Ca²⁺ stability | More basic ligands are more readily deprotonated and donate electron density better to hard cations |
| However, pKa alone does not predict logK well | **Charge, denticity, chelate effect, and donor type** strongly affect Ca²⁺ binding |

### Hypothesis for alkaline-earth metals
For alkaline-earth ions such as Ca²⁺, **binding strength generally increases with the effective basicity of hard O-donor ligands when donor type and denticity are held similar**. However, across diverse ligands, **overall negative charge and multidentate chelation outweigh simple pKa effects**, so basicity is best viewed as a contributing factor rather than a sole predictor.

### Chemical insight
This fits classic hard-acid/hard-base behavior: alkaline-earth metals prefer **oxygen-rich, anionic ligands**. So for Mg²⁺, Ca²⁺, Sr²⁺, and Ba²⁺, the strongest complexes are usually formed not just by the most basic ligands, but by those that combine **hard O donors + multiple binding sites + favorable geometry**.

**Source:** NIST SRD-46.
