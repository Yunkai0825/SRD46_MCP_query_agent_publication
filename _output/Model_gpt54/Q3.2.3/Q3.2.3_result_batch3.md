# Q3.2.3 -- Result (batch 3)

**Section:** 3.2 -- Thermodynamic Reasoning

**Prompt:** Based on the pKa values of oxalic acid and the stability constant of Ca²⁺–oxalate, at what approximate pH would you expect calcium oxalate precipitation to become significant?

**Tool calls:** 11  |  **Time:** 140s planning + 29s execution + 17s verdict = 187s total

---

Using the SRD-46 values, **significant calcium oxalate precipitation should start around pH ~4, and become more favorable above about pH 4–5**.

| Quantity | SRD-46 value | Conditions |
|---|---:|---|
| Oxalic acid pKa₁ | -1.2 | 25 °C, I = 0.1 M |
| Oxalic acid pKa₂ | 3.8 | 25 °C, I = 0.1 M |
| Ca²⁺ + C₂O₄²⁻ ⇌ CaC₂O₄(aq), log β₁ | 3.19 | 25 °C, I = 0 |
| CaC₂O₄·H₂O(s) ⇌ Ca²⁺ + C₂O₄²⁻ + H₂O, log K | -7.86 | 25 °C, I = 0.1 M |

Reasoning:

| Step | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| Speciation of oxalic acid | The key transition is **pKa₂ = 3.8**, so the fully deprotonated ligand **oxalate, C₂O₄²⁻**, becomes important at about this pH. |
| Precipitating species | Calcium oxalate precipitation depends on **free C₂O₄²⁻**, not just total oxalate. |
| Ca–oxalate interaction | The aqueous Ca–oxalate complex is fairly stable (**log β ≈ 3.2**), so once oxalate appears, calcium binds it readily. |
| Solid formation | The solid hydrate equilibrium corresponds to a very small solubility product (approximately **10^-7.86** at 25 °C, I = 0.1 M), so precipitation is favored once enough C₂O₄²⁻ is present. |

A useful rule-of-thumb from these numbers is:

| pH | Expected behavior |
|---:|---|
| < 3 | Most oxalate is still protonated; precipitation is limited |
| ~3.8 | C₂O₄²⁻ begins to become substantial; **onset region** for precipitation |
| 4–5 | Enough dianion is present that **precipitation becomes significant** in typical Ca-containing solutions |
| > 5 | Oxalate is largely deprotonated, so precipitation is strongly favored unless concentrations are very low |

So the best approximate answer is: **around pH 4**, with **clear significance by roughly pH 4–5**.

*Source: NIST SRD-46.*

### Verdict & Explanation

## Verdict
The subagent largely answered the question, giving a reasonable onset range of about pH 4 and stronger precipitation by pH 4–5. A minor gap is that the exact precipitation pH also depends on the calcium and total oxalate concentrations, not just pKa and binding/solubility constants.

## Explanation
Oxalic acid’s second deprotonation near pH 3.8 is the key threshold: below this, little oxalate exists as C₂O₄²⁻, the form needed to bind Ca²⁺ and enter the solid. Because Ca–oxalate binding is appreciable and calcium oxalate is sparingly soluble, precipitation becomes much more likely once pH rises past ~4. In real systems, ionic strength and reactant concentrations can shift the practical onset somewhat.
