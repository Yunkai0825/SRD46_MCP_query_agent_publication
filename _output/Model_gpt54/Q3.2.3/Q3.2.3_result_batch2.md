# Q3.2.3 -- Result (batch 2)

**Section:** 3.2 -- Thermodynamic Reasoning

**Prompt:** Based on the pKa values of oxalic acid and the stability constant of Ca²⁺–oxalate, at what approximate pH would you expect calcium oxalate precipitation to become significant?

**Tool calls:** 7  |  **Time:** 75s planning + 27s execution + 14s verdict = 116s total

---

Using SRD-46 data for oxalic acid and Ca²⁺–oxalate at about room temperature:

| Quantity | Value | Conditions | SRD-46 interpretation |
|---|---:|---|---|
| pKa relevant to forming oxalate \( \mathrm{C_2O_4^{2-}} \) | 3.8 | 25 °C, I = 0.1 M | \( \mathrm{HC_2O_4^- \rightleftharpoons H^+ + C_2O_4^{2-}} \) |
| Ca²⁺ + oxalate(aq) formation | logK = 3.19 | 25 °C, I = 0 | \( \mathrm{Ca^{2+} + C_2O_4^{2-} \rightleftharpoons CaOx(aq)} \) |
| Calcium oxalate monohydrate dissolution | logK = -7.86 to -7.90 | 20–25 °C, I = 0.1 M | \( \mathrm{CaOx \cdot H_2O(s) \rightleftharpoons Ca^{2+} + C_2O_4^{2-} + H_2O} \) |

So the **approximate pH where calcium oxalate precipitation becomes significant is around pH 4**, and more clearly **above about 4–5**.

Why: below pH 3.8, most oxalate is still protonated as hydrogen oxalate, so free \( \mathrm{C_2O_4^{2-}} \) is limited. Around **pH = pKa2 = 3.8**, the fully deprotonated oxalate form starts becoming substantial, which is the form that both **binds Ca²⁺ strongly** and **enters the low-solubility solid**. Because Ca–oxalate complexation is fairly favorable \( \log K \approx 3.2 \), oxalate is effectively “captured” by calcium once enough \( \mathrm{C_2O_4^{2-}} \) appears, so precipitation tends to turn on near this region.

A practical rule-of-thumb from these numbers is:

| pH range | Expected behavior |
|---|---|
| < 3 | Little calcium oxalate precipitation expected |
| ~3.5–4.5 | Onset region; precipitation can become significant |
| > 4.5 | Much more favorable for significant precipitation |

**Answer:** **around pH 4** is the best approximate threshold, with precipitation becoming increasingly significant as pH rises above that.  
*Source: NIST SRD-46.*

---

### Verdict & Explanation

**Verdict**  
The subagent largely answered the question: an onset near **pH ~4** and stronger precipitation above **~4–5** is consistent with the reported acid dissociation and Ca–oxalate equilibria. A minor caveat is that the exact precipitation threshold also depends on total calcium and oxalate concentrations.

**Explanation**  
Oxalic acid’s second deprotonation (**pKa₂ ≈ 3.8**) controls when appreciable **C₂O₄²⁻** appears; that dianion is the form that both complexes Ca²⁺ strongly (**log K ≈ 3.2**) and enters the very sparingly soluble calcium oxalate solid. Thus, below ~3 most oxalate is too protonated for much precipitation, while around **pH 3.5–4.5** precipitation should begin to become significant.
