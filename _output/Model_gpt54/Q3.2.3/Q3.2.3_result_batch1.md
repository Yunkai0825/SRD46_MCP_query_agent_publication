# Q3.2.3 -- Result (batch 1)

**Section:** 3.2 -- Thermodynamic Reasoning

**Prompt:** Based on the pKa values of oxalic acid and the stability constant of Ca²⁺–oxalate, at what approximate pH would you expect calcium oxalate precipitation to become significant?

**Tool calls:** 9  |  **Time:** 132s planning + 29s execution + 32s verdict = 192s total

---

Using SRD-46 data for oxalic acid and Ca–oxalate at about room temperature:

| Quantity | Value | Conditions | SRD-46 note |
|---|---:|---|---|
| pKa₁ of oxalic acid | -1.2 | 25 °C, I = 0.1 M | H₂L ⇌ HL⁻ + H⁺ |
| pKa₂ of oxalic acid | 3.8 | 25 °C, I = 0.1 M | HL⁻ ⇌ L²⁻ + H⁺ |
| log β for Ca²⁺ + oxalate²⁻ ⇌ CaL(aq) | 3.19 | 25 °C, I = 0 | aqueous complex |
| log K for CaC₂O₄·H₂O(s) ⇌ Ca²⁺ + oxalate²⁻ + H₂O | -8.75 | 25 °C, I = 0 | implies Ksp ≈ 10^-8.75 |

A reasonable **onset pH for significant calcium oxalate precipitation is around pH 4.5–5.5**, with **about pH 5** as a practical estimate.

Why: oxalate must be present mainly as **C₂O₄²⁻**, and that rises strongly once the pH moves above **pKa₂ = 3.8**. At pH 3.8, only about half of the deprotonated oxalate is L²⁻; by pH 4.8 it is about 90%, so the ion product for CaC₂O₄ builds quickly. The Ca–oxalate aqueous complex is moderately strong, but not strong enough to prevent precipitation once enough free oxalate²⁻ exists.

| pH | Expected speciation/behavior |
|---:|---|
| < 3.5 | Mostly protonated oxalate; precipitation less favored |
| ~3.8–4.5 | oxalate²⁻ begins rising rapidly; precipitation may start if concentrations are high |
| ~4.5–5.5 | precipitation commonly becomes significant |
| > 5.5 | oxalate²⁻ abundant; precipitation strongly favored if Ca and oxalate are present |

So, **if calcium and total oxalate are both appreciable, expect calcium oxalate precipitation to become important just above pH 4, and clearly significant near pH 5**.

*Source: NIST SRD-46.*

---

### Verdict & Explanation

**Verdict**

The subagent substantially answered the question and gave a reasonable approximate pH range. The only caveat is that the exact precipitation onset also depends strongly on the actual calcium and total oxalate concentrations, not just pKa and stability constants.

**Explanation**

Oxalic acid’s second deprotonation (pKa₂ ≈ 3.8) controls when appreciable oxalate²⁻ appears; above this pH, the dianion rises rapidly and can combine with Ca²⁺. Because calcium oxalate has a very low solubility product (~10^-8.75) and only moderate aqueous complex stability (log β ≈ 3.19), precipitation becomes much more favorable around pH 4.5–5.5, with ~pH 5 as a practical estimate.
