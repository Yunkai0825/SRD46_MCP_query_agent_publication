So at about neutral pH, EDTA is **not fully deprotonated**, which means its **conditional** binding is weaker than the intrinsic constant for fully deprotonated **L**. Even so, SRD-46 shows it forms stable complexes with a wide range of metal ions, which is exactly why it is widely used in titrations, water softening, and metal sequestration.

Practical interpretation:

| Verdict | Why |
|---|---|
| **Yes, EDTA is a very good general chelator** | It binds many di- and trivalent metals, has extensive measured stability data in SRD-46, and is one of the best-studied broad-spectrum aminopolycarboxylate ligands |

Chemistry insight: EDTA is especially useful because it is **broad-spectrum rather than highly selective**. It is strong enough to bind hardness ions like **Ca²⁺/Mg²⁺**, but it is generally even more effective with many transition and trivalent metals such as **Cu²⁺, Fe³⁺, and Al³⁺**.
