Yes — **EDTA is a very good chelator** in SRD-46.

SRD-46 identifies EDTA as **ethylenedinitrilotetraacetic acid** (`ligand_6277`), with **75 metal partners** and **596 measurements**, which already indicates unusually broad metal-binding coverage.

| Property | SRD-46 value |
|---|---:|
| Ligand | Ethylenedinitrilotetraacetic acid (EDTA) |
| Ligand ID | `ligand_6277` |
| Ligand class | EDTA and derivatives |
| HxL definition | H4L |
| Metal partners | 75 |
| Total measurements | 596 |

Its acid-base behavior explains why it chelates well but **works best away from low pH**:

| Protonation step | pKa |
|---|---:|
| H4L → H5L | -1.50 |
| H3L → H4L | 2.00 |
| H2L → H3L | 2.69 |
| HL → H2L | 6.13 |
| L → HL | 9.52 |

From the EDTA reference equilibrium networks, even simple alkali-metal complexes are observed:

| Metal | Complex | logK / logβ |
|---|---|---:|
| Li⁺ | ML | 2.95 |
| Na⁺ | ML | 1.86 |

So for weakly binding alkali ions EDTA still chelates measurably; for harder and transition-metal ions, the catalog shows extensive additional systems, including **Mg²⁺, Ca²⁺, Cd²⁺, Cu²⁺, Ni²⁺, Zn²⁺, Co²⁺, Hg²⁺, Fe³⁺, Al³⁺, lanthanides, Th⁴⁺, VO₂⁺, and UO₂²⁺**.

| Qualitative conclusion | Evidence from SRD-46 |
|---|---|
| Broad-spectrum chelator | 75 metal partners |
| Forms many defined species | 20 metal/ligand systems, 66 species in catalog |
| pH-sensitive but strong when deprotonated | Full protonation ladder through H4L/HL/L |
| Especially useful from neutral to basic pH | Higher deprotonation above pKa 6.13 and 9.52 frees donor sites |

**Bottom line:** EDTA is an **excellent, broadly useful chelator**, especially at **neutral to basic pH**, where more of the ligand is deprotonated and available to bind metals strongly. In acidic solution it is still a chelator, but its effectiveness drops because protonation competes with metal binding.

**Source:** NIST SRD-46.

### Verdict & Explanation

## Verdict
The subagent mostly answered the question: EDTA is indeed a good chelator, and the database evidence supports broad metal binding and pH-dependent effectiveness. A key gap is that it did not provide representative stability constants for the stronger divalent/trivalent metals, so “good” is supported more by breadth than by quantitative strength.

## Explanation
Scientifically, EDTA is a classic multidentate ligand that binds many metals because it offers several donor atoms and forms stable chelate rings. The pKa values show why binding improves near neutral/basic pH: deprotonation frees donor sites, whereas in acidic solution protonation competes with metal coordination.
