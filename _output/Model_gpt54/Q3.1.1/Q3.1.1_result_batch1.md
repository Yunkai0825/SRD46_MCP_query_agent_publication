### Important interpretation note
| Caveat | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Constant definitions vary (L, HL, H₂L, H₃L bases) | Reported log β/log K values are **not directly comparable** unless they refer to the same reaction stoichiometry and protonation basis |
| Conditions differ | Temperature and ionic strength affect measured affinity |
| “Highest affinity” does not mean “best drug” | Clinical usefulness also depends on toxicity, selectivity, oral availability, and ability to form excretable complexes |

Chemically, the pattern makes sense: **Pb²⁺ is a soft/borderline Lewis acid**, so **sulfur donors** often bind more strongly than simple oxygen-donor ligands like citrate. The very high-affinity **polyaza macrocycles** can outperform clinical agents in thermodynamic binding, but that does **not** make them suitable therapeutically.

**Source:** NIST SRD-46.

There are minor gaps: the answer identifies likely top Pb²⁺ binders and a clinically used ligand, but it does not provide the actual highest affinity constants or a definitive clinical picture for standard lead antidotes such as CaNa₂EDTA and succimer.

**Explanation**  
The retrieved data suggest Pb²⁺ binds most strongly to large polyaza macrocycles and to sulfur-donor ligands, with penicillamine the strongest clearly clinical ligand found in this subset.
