SRD-46 does show **very strong Pb²⁺ binding by aminopolycarboxylate ligands**, with the main top-affinity names in the retrieved Pb(II) set being **DTPA, CDTA, and EDTA**, followed by related EDTA/HEDTA-like chelators. Among ligands with clear clinical relevance to **lead poisoning**, **EDTA** is the standout: the therapeutic agent is **CaNa₂EDTA**, an EDTA-family chelator.

A chemistry caveat: **intrinsic stability constants are not the same as in vivo effectiveness**. For lead chelation, protonation, calcium competition, route of administration, toxicity, and redistribution risk matter a lot—this is why **CaNa₂EDTA** remains clinically important even though other ligands may also show very high Pb²⁺ affinity in equilibrium datasets.

### Verdict & Explanation

## Verdict
There are gaps. The answer identifies EDTA, DTPA, and CDTA as top Pb²⁺ binders and correctly notes CaNa₂EDTA is used clinically, but it does not clearly separate extreme/non-physiologic constants from clinically relevant aqueous 1:1 affinities and omits key modern clinical chelators like succimer/DMPS from the clinical discussion.

## Explanation
The retrieved SRD-46 data indicate Pb²⁺ binds especially strongly to multidentate aminopolycarboxylates such as DTPA, CDTA, and EDTA, generally more strongly than simpler thiols or amino acids. Scientifically, very large reported stability constants can depend strongly on reaction stoichiometry, solvent, and ionic conditions, so the highest database affinity does not automatically predict best antidotal performance in vivo.
