# Q2.1.1 -- Result (batch 1)

**Section:** 2.1 -- Comparison & Ranking

**Prompt:** Between EDTA and NTA, which ligand forms the more stable complex with Fe³⁺? Show the log K values side by side.

**Tool calls:** 9  |  **Time:** 70s planning + 61s execution + 5s verdict = 136s total

---

SRD-46 shows **EDTA forms the more stable 1:1 complex with Fe³⁺** than NTA.

| Ligand | Fe³⁺ complex | log K for `[M] + [L] ⇌ [ML]` | T (°C) | Ionic strength (M) |
|---|---|---:|---:|---:|
| EDTA | Fe(III)–EDTA | 25.1 | 25 | 0.1 |
| NTA | Fe(III)–NTA | 15.9 | 25 | 0.1 |

At the same matched conditions in SRD-46 (**25 °C, I = 0.1 M**), **EDTA binds Fe³⁺ much more strongly** than NTA.

For completeness, SRD-46 also lists values at **I = 1.0 M**:

| Ligand | log K | T (°C) | Ionic strength (M) |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| EDTA | 23.8 | 25 | 1.0 |
| NTA | 14.78 | 25 | 1.0 |

The trend is chemically reasonable: EDTA is a **hexadentate** ligand, while NTA is **tridentate**, so EDTA generally gains a substantial chelate-effect advantage with Fe³⁺.  
**Source:** NIST SRD-46, Fe³⁺ stability data for `[ML]/[M][L]` measurements.

## Explanation

The same pattern holds at 1.0 M ionic strength (23.8 vs 14.78), consistent with EDTA’s greater denticity and stronger chelate effect.
