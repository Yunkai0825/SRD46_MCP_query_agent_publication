# Q2.1.3 -- Result (batch 2)

**Section:** 2.1 -- Comparison & Ranking

**Prompt:** Compare the pKa values of glycine and alanine. Are they significantly different?

**Tool calls:** 10  |  **Time:** 106s planning + 54s execution + 11s verdict = 171s total

---

Using SRD-46 data at matched conditions (aqueous, 25 °C, ionic strength 0.1 M), **glycine and alanine have essentially the same lower pKa, but alanine has a modestly higher upper pKa**.

| Ligand | pKa1 | Assignment | pKa2 | Assignment | Conditions |
|---|---:|---|---:|---|---|
| Glycine | 2.33 | HL → H2L (carboxyl-related) | 9.57 | L → HL (ammonium-related) | water, 25 °C, I = 0.1 M |
| Alanine | 2.33 | HL → H2L (carboxyl-related) | 9.71 | L → HL (ammonium-related) | water, 25 °C, I = 0.1 M |

| Comparison | ΔpKa (alanine − glycine) | Interpretation |
|---|---:|---|
| pKa1 | 0.00 | No difference in this matched dataset |
| pKa2 | 0.14 | Small but likely real difference; alanine is slightly more basic at the amino group |

So, **not dramatically different overall**. The **carboxyl pKa is identical** in the matched SRD-46 entries, while the **amino-group pKa differs by 0.14 units**, which is small but a bit larger than typical experimental uncertainty often cited for good pKa measurements (~0.05–0.10). Chemically, that makes sense: the methyl group in alanine is slightly electron-donating relative to glycine, which can slightly increase the basicity of the amino group.

**Source:** NIST SRD-46.
