# Q2.1.5 -- Result (batch 4)

**Section:** 2.1 -- Comparison & Ranking

**Prompt:** Between ammonia and ethylenediamine, which ligand binds more strongly to Ni²⁺? Quantify the difference in log K.

**Tool calls:** 11  |  **Time:** 99s planning + 157s execution + 7s verdict = 263s total

---

Ethylenediamine binds **much more strongly** to Ni²⁺ than ammonia.

Using matched **1:1 formation constants** for  
\[
\mathrm{Ni^{2+} + L \rightleftharpoons NiL^{2+}}
\]
at **25 °C** and **ionic strength \(I=0\)** from SRD-46:

| Ligand | log K₁ |
|---|---:|
| Ammonia (NH₃) | 2.72 |
| Ethylenediamine (en) | 7.32 |

So the difference is:

| Comparison | Δlog K |
|---|---:|
| log K₁(en) − log K₁(NH₃) | 4.60 |

That means ethylenediamine is stronger by about **4.6 log units**, i.e. roughly **10^4.6 ≈ 4 × 10⁴ times** in the 1:1 binding constant.

Chelation is the key reason: ethylenediamine is **bidentate**, so one ligand forms a ring with Ni²⁺, which gives a strong stability boost relative to monodentate ammonia.

**Source:** NIST SRD-46 stability constants for Ni²⁺–ammonia and Ni²⁺–ethylenediamine, 25 °C, \(I=0\).

**Verdict**  
The subagent fully answered the question. It identified ethylenediamine as the stronger ligand for Ni²⁺ and quantified the difference as Δlog K = 4.60.

**Explanation**
