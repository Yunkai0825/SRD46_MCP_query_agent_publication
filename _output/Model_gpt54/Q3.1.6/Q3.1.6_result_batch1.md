According to the SRD‑46 stability data for **La³⁺ 1:1 complexes** (beta definition **812: [M] + [L] ⇌ [ML]**), the strongest **carboxylate-containing ligands** in the retrieved set include the following:

| Ligand | Ligand type | log K |
|---|---|---:|
| trans-1,2-Cyclohexylenedinitrilotetraacetic acid (**CDTA**) | aminopolycarboxylate | 19.6 |
| Diethylenetrinitrilopentaacetic acid (**DTPA**) | aminopolycarboxylate | 19.04 |
| 1,4,7,10-Tetraazacyclododecane-tetraacetic acid (**DOTA**) | macrocyclic aminocarboxylate | 17.7 |
| 1,4,7,10-Tetraazacyclotridecane-tetraacetic acid | macrocyclic aminocarboxylate | 17.06 |
| Oxybis(ethylenenitrilo)tetraacetic acid | aminopolycarboxylate | 16.3 |
| Ethylenedinitrilotetraacetic acid (**EDTA**) | aminopolycarboxylate | 15.5 |

So, if you want **at least three La³⁺ complexes with the highest reported stability**, the top three from this set are:

| Rank | Ligand | log K |
|---|---|---:|
| 1 | CDTA | 19.6 |
| 2 | DTPA | 19.04 |
| 3 | DOTA | 17.7 |

These values come from aqueous SRD‑46 records, mostly around **20–25 °C** and **ionic strengths in the ~0.1–0.5/1 M range**. A useful chemical takeaway is that **multidentate aminopolycarboxylates and macrocyclic tetraacetates bind La³⁺ far more strongly than simple dicarboxylic acids**, because La³⁺ strongly favors **high coordination number and chelation**.

**Source:** NIST SRD‑46 Stability Constants Database.

### Verdict & Explanation

**Verdict**  
The subagent substantially answered the question: it identified more than three carboxylic-acid ligands forming very stable La³⁺ complexes and reported log K values. A minor caveat is that these are carboxylate-containing chelators rather than simple carboxylic acids.

**Explanation**  
The data show La³⁺ binds most strongly to multidentate aminopolycarboxylate ligands such as CDTA (log K 19.6), DTPA (19.04), and DOTA (17.7), with EDTA also strong (15.5). This pattern is chemically sensible because La³⁺ is a hard, highly charged cation that prefers oxygen-donor, highly chelating ligands; simple mono- or dicarboxylic acids are typically much weaker.
