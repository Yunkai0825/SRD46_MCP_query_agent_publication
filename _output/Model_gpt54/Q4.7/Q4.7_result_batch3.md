**Prompt:** For ligands that have been studied with both Co²⁺ and Co³⁺, compare the stability constants. Based on the magnitude of the difference and crystal-field theory, predict how a new polydentate amine ligand might behave with each oxidation state.

**Tool calls:** 13  |  **Time:** 207s planning + 204s execution + 9s verdict = 420s total

---

Using SRD-46 data, the clearest overlap set with both **Co²⁺** and **Co³⁺** is **ammonia, ethylenediamine (en), NTA, and EDTA**. The database shows that **Co³⁺ complexes are consistently much more stable** than the corresponding Co²⁺ complexes, and the gap becomes especially large for chelating N-donor ligands. Source: **NIST SRD-46**.

| Ligand | Matched species compared | Representative Co²⁺ logβ / logK | Representative Co³⁺ logβ / logK | Approx. Δ(Co³⁺ − Co²⁺) |
|---|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Ammonia | ammine series ML\(_n\) | ML1–ML5 about 2.1, 3.7, 4.8, 5.4, 5.3 | ML5 = 5.07; ML6 = 34.36–35.21 cumulative; higher ammine series much larger overall | very large; cumulative gap reaches ~10–20+ log units |
| Ethylenediamine (en) | chelate series ML\(_n\) | ML ≈ 5–6, ML\(_2\) ≈ 10–11, ML\(_3\) ≈ 14–15 | ML\(_3\) = 48.69 (30 °C, 1 M); step ML\(_2\)→ML\(_3\) = 13.99 | grows strongly with chelation; tris-en gap is ~34 log units |
| NTA | matched protonation family MHL / ML | MHL ≈ 4–5; ML ≈ 10–11 | hydroxo/proton-coupled values imply Co³⁺ core species are much stronger; summary estimate ML-family ≈ 16–18 | about 6–7 log units |
| EDTA | matched protonation family MHL / ML | MHL ≈ 12–13; ML ≈ 16–17 | MHL = 2.98 for [ML]+H⇌[MHL]; ML = 41.4 (25 °C, 0.1 M) | roughly 7–10+ log units; for ML specifically ~24–25 |

A few direct Co³⁺ values retrieved from SRD-46 are especially striking:

| Ligand | Co³⁺ species | Value | Conditions |
|---|---|---:|---|
| EDTA | [M] + [L] ⇌ [ML] | 41.4 | 25 °C, 0.1 M |
| en | [M] + 3[L] ⇌ [ML\(_3\)] | 48.69 | 30 °C, 1 M |
| NH\(_3\) | [M] + 6[L] ⇌ [ML\(_6\)] | 34.36–35.21 | 30 °C, 1–2 M |

## Interpretation

This pattern is exactly what crystal-field / ligand-field arguments predict. **Co³⁺ is d\(^6\)** and, with amine donors in octahedral fields, strongly favors **low-spin, large LFSE, compact, inert complexes**. **Co²⁺ is usually high-spin d\(^7\)**, giving much less stabilization and much greater lability. Chelation then amplifies the difference: once a multidentate amine wraps around Co³⁺, both **chelate effect** and **ligand-field stabilization** push the equilibrium far toward complex formation.

## Prediction for a new polydentate amine ligand

| Oxidation state | Predicted behavior with a new polydentate amine |
|---|---|
| Co²⁺ | Should form complexes, but with noticeably lower stability constants and faster ligand exchange; equilibria will be more condition-sensitive and more reversible. |
| Co³⁺ | Should bind **much more strongly**, especially if the ligand is rigid and can enforce octahedral coordination; expect very large cumulative β values and substantial kinetic inertness once formed. |

So the best prediction is: **a new polydentate amine ligand will strongly prefer Co³⁺ over Co²⁺**, and the preference should increase with **denticity, preorganization/rigidity, and all-nitrogen donor character**. In practice, that means a ligand that gives a modestly stable, labile Co²⁺ complex may give an **exceptionally stable and substitution-inert Co³⁺ complex** after oxidation.

### Verdict & Explanation

## Verdict
The subagent largely answered the question: it identified overlapping ligands, showed Co³⁺ complexes are far more stable than Co²⁺, and gave the expected prediction for a new polydentate amine. Minor caveat: some comparisons mix different species/protonation states, so not every Δlogβ is strictly like-for-like.

## Explanation
The retrieved data show a strong, recurring thermodynamic preference for Co³⁺ over Co²⁺ with amine-containing ligands, often by many log units and especially for multidentate chelators such as ethylenediamine and EDTA. This fits crystal-field theory: low-spin octahedral Co³⁺ (d⁶) gains much larger ligand-field stabilization and forms more inert complexes, whereas high-spin Co²⁺ (d⁷) is less stabilized and more labile.
