## Trend across ligand classes

A database-supported qualitative ordering is:

| Approximate Fe(III) stability tendency | Ligand classes | Main reason |
|---|---|---|
| Very high | hydroxamates, catecholate/Tiron-type ligands, EDTA/NTA/CDTA-type aminopolycarboxylates | hard O donors, multiple negative charges, strong chelation |
| High | oxalate, salicylate, 8-hydroxyquinoline | chelation + deprotonated donor atoms |
| Moderate | acetate and related simple carboxylates | hard O donor, but lower denticity and less preorganization |
| Lower to moderate | chloride, thiocyanate, azide | poorer HSAB match and/or weaker monodentate binding |
| Special case | hydroxide | extremely favorable O binding, but hydrolysis/polymerization dominates |

For **Fe(II)**, this pass retrieved much less usable pairwise stability data than for Fe(III), but the database coverage itself indicates Fe(II) is less richly represented for these hard-anionic systems. The chemically consistent interpretation is:

| Class | Fe(III) vs Fe(II) trend |
|---|---|
| Hard O-donor anions and polycarboxylates | Fe(III) much more stable |
| Chelating O,O / O,N ligands | Fe(III) usually stronger |
| Neutral N donors / stronger-field ligands | Fe(II) becomes relatively more competitive |
| Soft/ambidentate ligands like SCN− | more complicated; donor mode matters |

## Thermodynamic explanation

### 1. HSAB dominates the Fe(III) ordering
**Fe(III)** is a small, highly charged, hard Lewis acid. So it strongly prefers:

| Donor type | Consequence |
|---|---|
| hard O donors | strongest stabilization |
| anionic donors | extra Coulombic stabilization |
| multidentate O-rich ligands | very large overall β values |

That explains why the Fe(III) catalog is populated by **hydroxide, oxalate, salicylates, Tiron, hydroxamates, EDTA/NTA/CDTA**.

### 2. Chelate effect raises multidentate ligands above simple anions
Even if two ligands use similar donor atoms, one multidentate ligand generally outcompetes several monodentates because coordination releases more solvent molecules and reduces dissociation probability.

| Comparison | Expected result |
|---|---|
| chloride vs EDTA | EDTA far stronger |
| acetate vs oxalate | oxalate stronger |
| salicylate vs catecholate/hydroxamate-type chelators | chelating/deprotonated O-rich ligands stronger |

### 3. Ligand basicity and deprotonation matter
The pKa data show that ligands such as **8-hydroxyquinoline, salicylate, hydroxamates, Tiron**, and aminopolycarboxylates must be deprotonated to present the strongest donor set. Once deprotonated, they become much better matched to Fe(III).

### 4. Crystal-field effects matter more for Fe(II) edge cases
For **Fe(II)**, ligand-field stabilization can narrow the gap with Fe(III) for strong-field ligands, especially neutral N donors or π-acceptor ligands.

| Metal ion | Ligand-field role |
|---|---|
| Fe(III), often high-spin d5 | CFSE is usually a smaller driver than charge density + HSAB |
| Fe(II), d6 | stronger-field ligands can give extra stabilization and sometimes spin-state effects |

So for ligands like **bipyridine, phenanthroline, cyanide**, Fe(II) can be comparatively more competitive than it is with oxalate, EDTA, or hydroxide.

## Edge cases

| Edge case | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| Hydroxide | strongest evidence in the retrieved data; Fe(III)-OH chemistry quickly becomes hydrolysis, olation/oxolation, and solids rather than simple soluble ML only |
| Thiocyanate | abundant in Fe(III) catalog despite not being a classic hard donor; ambidentate coordination and charge-transfer chemistry make it chemically prominent |
| 8-Hydroxyquinoline | mixed N,O donor but still strong because chelation and deprotonation compensate for not being a pure hard O-donor |
| Salicylate / Tiron | aromatic O,O chelation and resonance stabilization make them stronger than simple monocarboxylates |
| Fluoride vs chloride | Fe(III) should prefer F− over Cl− by HSAB because fluoride is the harder base |

## Bottom line

The SRD-46 survey shows a clear hierarchy: **Fe(III) is most strongly stabilized by hard, anionic, multidentate O-donor ligands**, with hydroxamates, catecholate/Tiron-type ligands, and aminopolycarboxylates at the top; **simple carboxylates and monodentate halides/pseudohalides are weaker**. **Fe(II)** is generally less strongly bound by these hard O-donor classes and becomes relatively more competitive only with stronger-field neutral N-donor ligands. The ordering is best explained by **HSAB + ligand charge + chelate effect**, while **crystal-field effects** mainly account for the Fe(II) edge cases. The biggest exception is **hydroxide**, where very favorable Fe(III)–O interactions push the system into **hydrolysis, polynuclear species, and precipitation** rather than just larger simple formation constants.
