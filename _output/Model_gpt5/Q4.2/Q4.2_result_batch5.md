Hypothesis: Hg²⁺ shows a strong preference for sulfur‑donor ligands over nitrogen‑ and oxygen‑donor ligands. Monodentate N donors are generally much weaker than S donors; O‑donor carboxylates are typically weaker than strong S donors and can be similar to or lower than chelating N donors. Chelation (e.g., ethylenediamine; polycarboxylates) can boost stability, but S donors remain the most potent at comparable conditions.

Interpretation
- S donors are strongest: cysteine (thiolate) and thiourea give high log K1 (14.4 and 11.4), consistent with Hg²⁺ being a soft acid that favors soft S donors.
- N donors vary widely: simple amines are weak (NH3 ≈ 2.4), azoles are moderate (imidazole ≈ 6.2), while chelating diamines can reach S‑like strengths (en ≈ 14.3) due to the chelate effect.
- O donors are generally lower than the strongest S and chelating N systems: bidentate oxalate (9.66) and polydentate citrate (10.9) are moderate.

Overall, the data support Hg²⁺ ≫ preference for sulfur donors, with nitrogen intermediate (strong when chelating; weak when monodentate), and oxygen typically weaker. Small differences in ionic strength/media can shift values by ≲0.5–1 log unit, but the S > N ≥ O trend is robust across the examples shown.
