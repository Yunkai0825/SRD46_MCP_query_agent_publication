Summary answer
- The chelate effect, quantified here as Δ = log K1(en) − 2·log K1(NH3), is not constant across metals. It varies in the order Cu2+ > Ni2+ > Zn2+ for aqueous, near‑ambient conditions.
- Interpretation: The inter‑metal differences arise mainly from enthalpic factors—ligand‑field and geometry effects (including Jahn–Teller for Cu2+), and the degree of covalency/softness—while the entropic gain of replacing two NH3 by one bidentate en is relatively similar across these divalent ions.

Why Δ varies across Cu2+, Ni2+, Zn2+
- Enthalpy dominates the differences: Although chelation always provides a favorable entropy term (fewer particles, reduced solvent organization), that term is similar for these M2+ ions. What changes most with the metal is the enthalpic stabilization of M–N bonds when two adjacent donor atoms bind cooperatively.
- Ligand‑field and geometry:
  - Cu2+ (d9) is strongly Jahn–Teller active; a bidentate en bound in the equatorial plane can relieve distortions and provide a particularly favorable binding geometry compared with two separate NH3, giving a larger enthalpic “bonus” to en.
  - Ni2+ (d8, typically octahedral) benefits from ligand‑field stabilization and good geometric match with en; the chelate advantage is clear but generally smaller than for Cu2+.
  - Zn2+ (d10) has no ligand‑field stabilization in an octahedral field; its additional enthalpic gain from chelation over two NH3 is modest, so Δ is smallest.
- Covalency/HSAB: Cu2+ is a somewhat softer, more polarizable borderline acid than Ni2+ and Zn2+; chelation by en (two adjacent σ‑donor nitrogens) stabilizes a more covalent M–N interaction than two separate NH3 in Cu2+, amplifying Δ relative to the other metals.
- Bite‑angle/fit: Ethylenediamine’s N–M–N bite angle (~85–90°) fits the equatorial coordination preferences of Cu2+ particularly well; the geometric fit is still good for Ni2+, but there is less special stabilization for Zn2+.

Notes on quantification and fair comparison
- To compute Δ rigorously, use stepwise K1 values for M2+ + en and M2+ + NH3 measured at matched conditions (ideally 25 °C, low ionic strength) and, where possible, from the same study.
- Pay attention to competing hydrolysis and higher ammine formation (K2, K3, …) that can complicate extraction of K1; use first stepwise constants for both ligands to keep Δ well‑defined.
