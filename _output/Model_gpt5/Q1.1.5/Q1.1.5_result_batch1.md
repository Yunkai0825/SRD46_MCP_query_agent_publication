Oxalic acid is diprotic (two dissociation steps).

pKa values (25 °C, ionic strength ≈ 0.1 M):

| Step | Acid dissociation (H2L = oxalic acid) | pKa | T (°C) | I (M) | SRD-46 VLM ID |
|------|----------------------------------------|-----|--------|-------|---------------|
| 1 | H2L ⇌ HL + H+ | -1.2 | 25 | 0.1 | vlm_151532 |
| 2 | HL ⇌ L + H+ | 3.8 | 25 | 0.1 | vlm_151494 |

Notes
- The very negative pKa1 shows the first deprotonation is strongly acidic; the second step (pKa2 ≈ 3.8) is in the typical range for the second carboxyl proton of a dicarboxylic acid.

Source: NIST Standard Reference Database 46 (SRD‑46), ligand_8872 (Ethanedioic acid/oxalic acid), entries vlm_151532 and vlm_151494.

---

### Verdict & Explanation

**Verdict**
Partial. The subagent correctly states two dissociation steps, but the reported pKa1 = −1.2 is inconsistent with accepted aqueous values for oxalic acid.

**Explanation**
Oxalic acid is diprotic, with two sequential deprotonations. In water at 25 °C, typical values are pKa1 ≈ 1.2 and pKa2 ≈ 4.2; ionic strength (e.g., 0.1 M) shifts these only modestly. A negative pKa1 would imply an extremely strong first acidity (stronger than mineral acids in water), which contradicts established measurements, whereas the second value near 3.8 is broadly plausible within ionic-strength variation.
