# Q2.1.5 -- Result (batch 3)

**Section:** 2.1 -- Comparison & Ranking

**Prompt:** Between ammonia and ethylenediamine, which ligand binds more strongly to Ni²⁺? Quantify the difference in log K.

**Tool calls:** 8  |  **Time:** 400s planning + 359s execution + 45s verdict = 804s total

---

Short answer: Ethylenediamine (en) binds much more strongly to Ni2+ than ammonia (NH3).

Matched-condition comparison (aqueous, 25 °C, ionic strength → 0)

| Ligand | Reaction (Ni2+ + L ⇌ NiL2+) | log K1 | T (°C) | I (M) | Medium/Note |
|---|---|---:|---:|---:|---|
| Ammonia (NH3) | 1:1 formation constant | 2.77 | 25 | → 0 | Extrapolated to infinite dilution, water |
| Ethylenediamine (en) | 1:1 formation constant | 7.5 | 25 | → 0 | Extrapolated to infinite dilution, water |

Difference: Δlog K1 = 7.5 − 2.77 ≈ 4.73 (about 5 orders of magnitude stronger for en).

Notes
- The en value near room temperature at low ionic strength is reported in the range 7.3–7.7, so Δlog K1 ≈ 4.5–4.9 under similar conditions.
- This reflects the chelate effect: bidentate en forms a more stable five-membered chelate ring with Ni2+ than monodentate NH3.

Source: NIST SRD-46 Stability Constants of Metal Complexes (tool outputs: build_system_catalog for Ni2+–NH3 and Ni2+–en).

### Verdict & Explanation

**Verdict**
No gaps—the subagent identified ethylenediamine as stronger and provided the difference in log K.

**Explanation**
At ~25 C in water and low ionic strength, log K1 for Ni2+-NH3 is ~2.7–2.9, whereas for Ni2+-en it is ~7.3–7.7; Delta log K1 ~4.5–5 (en binds ~10^4.5–10^5 times more strongly). This reflects the chelate effect: bidentate en forms a five-membered ring, stabilizing the Ni2+ complex relative to monodentate NH3. Caveat: values vary with ionic strength and whether stepwise (K1) or overall constants are compared.
