Short answer up front (qualitative, pending constants): to suppress Ca²⁺ while retaining Cu²⁺ in solution, the most practical ligands are fluoride and condensed phosphates (pyro-/tripolyphosphate). These preferentially sequester/precipitate Ca²⁺ relative to Cu²⁺ under mild aqueous conditions. Avoid carboxylates (citrate, oxalate, tartrate) and aminopolycarboxylates (EDTA, NTA), which bind Cu²⁺ strongly and will compromise Cu²⁺ measurement.

What we have (qualitative screening; SRD‑46 constants retrieval could not complete in this session)

| Ligand (family) | Ca²⁺ vs Cu²⁺ (qualitative) | Practical note |
|---|---|---|
| Fluoride (F⁻) | Strongly favors Ca²⁺ (CaF₂ readily forms/limits Ca²⁺ activity); comparatively weak Cu²⁺ complexation in water | Commonly used to “mask” Ca²⁺ by precipitation while Cu²⁺ remains measurable in solution (control pH/ionic strength to avoid HF formation and glass attack) |
| Condensed phosphates (pyrophosphate, tripolyphosphate) | High Ca²⁺ affinity; generally lower Cu²⁺ affinity than for alkaline earths | Widely used water softeners; effective Ca²⁺ sequestration without strongly tying up Cu²⁺; verify with your method’s pH/ionic strength |
| Silicate | Preferential Ca²⁺ uptake/precipitation; limited Cu²⁺ complexation | Can reduce Ca²⁺ activity; may gel/polymerize at high pH—control conditions |
| Borate | Forms calcium borates more readily than copper borates | Works at alkaline pH; ensure your method tolerates high pH/borate |
| Sulfate | Can reduce free Ca²⁺ via CaSO₄(s) under some conditions; CuSO₄ remains soluble | Selectivity is modest and highly condition-dependent; less reliable than F⁻ or polyphosphates |
| Gluconate / lactate | Moderate Ca²⁺ complexation; Cu²⁺ also complexes | Selectivity is limited; not ideal if Cu²⁺ must remain fully free/available |

Ligands to avoid when measuring Cu²⁺
- Citrate, oxalate, tartrate: Cu²⁺ forms strong complexes and/or insoluble salts; these will likely suppress Cu signal.
- EDTA, NTA and similar aminopolycarboxylates: bind Cu²⁺ much more strongly than Ca²⁺; they will chelate your analyte.

Operational tips
- Decide whether you want to “mask” by precipitation (e.g., CaF₂) or by complexation (e.g., polyphosphates). Precipitation is often simpler for Ca²⁺ and less likely to affect Cu²⁺.
- Keep conditions consistent (pH 4–7 and low ionic strength are common starting points for Cu²⁺ assays), and confirm that your analytical technique (e.g., AAS, ICP, voltammetry, colorimetry) tolerates the added ligand.
- After masking, verify recovery of Cu²⁺ with a spike test; adjust ligand dose to fully suppress Ca²⁺ without impacting Cu²⁺ response.

### Verdict & Explanation

Verdict
Partial. The subagent gives plausible ligand choices (fluoride, condensed phosphates) and cautions, but lacks quantitative Ca²⁺ vs Cu²⁺ stability data from SRD‑46 to substantiate selectivity.

Explanation
Chemically, Ca²⁺ (a hard acid) is preferentially sequestered by hard O/F donors: F⁻ and condensed phosphates can strongly reduce Ca²⁺ activity (via CaF₂ precipitation or strong Ca–polyphosphate complexes), while Cu²⁺ remains comparatively less complexed under mild aqueous conditions. Carboxylates and aminopolycarboxylates bind Cu²⁺ strongly and should be avoided. Selectivity depends on pH, ionic strength, and whether masking is by precipitation or complexation; sulfate offers only modest, condition‑dependent Ca preference.
