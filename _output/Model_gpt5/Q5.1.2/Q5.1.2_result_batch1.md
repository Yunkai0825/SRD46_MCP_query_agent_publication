Short answer: it depends on the metal ion and the measurement conditions. “Binding constant” for DTPA usually means the overall formation (stability) constant log β for a specific metal–ligand stoichiometry (most often 1:1 ML), at a defined temperature, ionic strength, and solvent.

From NIST SRD‑46, DTPA is indexed as:
- Ligand: Diethylenetrinitrilopentaacetic acid (DTPA; pentetic acid)
- ID: ligand_6356
- Coverage: 300+ measured stability-constant entries across many metals

If you tell me the metal ion and preferred conditions (stoichiometry, temperature, ionic strength/medium), I will return the best‑quality log β value(s) from SRD‑46 in a table. Common ions with DTPA data include Fe(III), Cu(II), Zn(II), Ca(II), Mg(II), Al(III), Pb(II), Ni(II), Co(II), Mn(II), Cd(II), Y(III), La(III), and Gd(III).

Source: NIST Standard Reference Database 46 (Critical Stability Constants of Metal Complexes).

**Explanation**
In SRD‑46, DTPA has hundreds of stability-constant (logβ) entries across metals and conditions; “the” binding constant is undefined without specifying the metal, stoichiometry, temperature, and ionic strength. A clear pattern is that DTPA forms very strong complexes with trivalent ions (e.g., Fe(III), lanthanides; logβ often >20) and weaker with divalent ions (e.g., Ca(II), Zn(II)). Protonation and medium effects substantially alter apparent values.
