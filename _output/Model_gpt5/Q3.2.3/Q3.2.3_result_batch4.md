# Q3.2.3 -- Result (batch 4)

**Section:** 3.2 -- Thermodynamic Reasoning

**Prompt:** Based on the pKa values of oxalic acid and the stability constant of Ca²⁺–oxalate, at what approximate pH would you expect calcium oxalate precipitation to become significant?

**Tool calls:** 10  |  **Time:** 430s planning + 442s execution + 77s verdict = 949s total

---

Answer in brief
There isn’t a single pH “switch” for CaC2O4 precipitation; it depends strongly on how much Ca2+ and total oxalate are present. Using SRD‑46 values at ~25 °C, precipitation becomes appreciable once the small L2− fraction of oxalate is enough to satisfy [Ca2+][C2O4^2−] ≈ Ksp. With Ksp(CaC2O4·H2O) ≈ 10^−7.86 and pKa2 ≈ 3.8, this typically occurs around:
- pH ≈ 2–3 for millimolar Ca2+ with sub‑millimolar to millimolar oxalate (e.g., ~pH 2.0 for 1 mM Ca and 1 mM oxalate; ~pH 3.0 for 1 mM Ca and 0.1 mM oxalate),
- and may not occur at all in very dilute solutions (e.g., 1 mM Ca with ≤14 µM total oxalate cannot reach Ksp even at high pH).

Key constants from SRD‑46 (25 °C)
| Quantity | Value | Conditions | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| pKa1 (H2L ⇌ HL− + H+) | −1.2 | 25 °C, I ≈ 0.1 M | Oxalic acid first deprotonation |
| pKa2 (HL− ⇌ L2− + H+) | 3.8 | 25 °C, I ≈ 0.1 M | Controls rise of L2− with pH |
| log Ksp (CaC2O4·H2O ⇌ Ca2+ + C2O4^2− + H2O) | −7.86 | 25 °C, I ≈ 0.1 M | Whewellite dissolution; Ksp ≈ 1.4×10^−8 |
| log β1 (Ca2+ + C2O4^2− ⇌ CaC2O4(aq)) | 3.19 | 25 °C, I ≈ 0 | Aqueous complex; reduces free Ca2+ and L2− |

How to estimate the pH threshold
- Oxalate speciation (for pH ≳ 2, H2L is negligible): α2 ≈ 1/(1 + 10^(pKa2 − pH)), where α2 is the fraction present as L2−.
- Precipitation becomes favorable when [Ca2+][C2O4^2−] ≳ Ksp. Neglecting complexation as a first approximation, set [C2O4^2−] ≈ α2·C_ox,T and [Ca2+] ≈ C_Ca,T. Then the threshold pH satisfies:
  pH ≈ pKa2 − log10(C_Ca,T·C_ox,T / Ksp − 1).
- Including CaC2O4(aq) formation (log β1 ≈ 3.19) shifts the threshold slightly upward because it lowers free Ca2+ and L2−; for rough screening, the no‑complexation estimate gives a lower bound on the pH.

Worked examples (illustrative, using Ksp at I ≈ 0.1 M)
| Totals (C_Ca,T, C_ox,T) | Approx. pH where precipitation begins (ignoring complexation) |
|---|---|
| 1 mM Ca, 1 mM oxalate | ≈ 2.0 |
| 1 mM Ca, 0.1 mM oxalate | ≈ 3.0 |
| 1 mM Ca, 10 µM oxalate | Not possible at any pH (C_ox,T < Ksp/[Ca2+]) |

Chemistry insight
- Because pKa2 = 3.8, L2− rises rapidly above pH ≈ 3–4. When Ca2+ and oxalate are moderately concentrated (millimolar range), even a small L2− fraction at lower pH can satisfy Ksp, so precipitation can start well below pKa2. In more dilute systems, significant precipitation requires higher pH (or may not occur), reflecting the strong concentration dependence of a low‑solubility salt.

Source: NIST Standard Reference Database 46 (SRD‑46): pKa values for oxalic acid at 25 °C, I ≈ 0.1 M (vlm_151532, vlm_151494); CaC2O4·H2O dissolution constants at 25 °C (log Ksp ≈ −7.86 at I ≈ 0.1 M; vlm_151601) and CaC2O4(aq) formation (log β1 ≈ 3.19 at I = 0; vlm_151599).

### Verdict & Explanation

Verdict
No major gaps: the subagent gives approximate pH thresholds and shows how pKa2, Ksp, and Ca–oxalate complexation control when precipitation becomes significant.

Explanation
Oxalate’s dianion (C2O4^2−) fraction rises near pH ~3–4 (pKa2 ≈ 3.8 at I ≈ 0.1 M). Precipitation begins when [Ca2+][C2O4^2−] ≈ Ksp (≈1.4×10^−8); with millimolar Ca/oxalate this can occur at pH ~2–3 because only a few percent dianion is needed, whereas dilute solutions may never reach saturation. Formation of CaC2O4(aq) (log β1 ≈ 3.19) reduces free ions and shifts the threshold modestly upward; ionic strength and total concentrations matter.
