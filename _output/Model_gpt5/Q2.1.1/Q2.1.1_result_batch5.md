# Q2.1.1 -- Result (batch 5)

**Section:** 2.1 -- Comparison & Ranking

**Prompt:** Between EDTA and NTA, which ligand forms the more stable complex with Fe³⁺? Show the log K values side by side.

**Tool calls:** 11  |  **Time:** 402s planning + 1500s execution + 39s verdict = 1940s total

---

Answer: EDTA forms the more stable Fe³⁺ complex than NTA.

| Condition (aqueous) | log β1 (Fe³⁺–EDTA) | log β1 (Fe³⁺–NTA) |
|---|---:|---:|
| 25 °C, I = 0.1 M | 25.10 | 15.90 |
| 25 °C, I = 1.0 M | 23.80 | 14.78 |

Notes
- β1 definition: [ML]/([M][L]) for the reaction Fe³⁺ + L ⇌ FeL (no H⁺ or OH⁻ terms; no polynuclear species).
- The Fe³⁺–EDTA complex is ~9 log units more stable than Fe³⁺–NTA at 0.1 M ionic strength. Both constants decrease at higher ionic strength, but EDTA remains much stronger—consistent with its higher denticity (hexadentate) and stronger chelate effect compared to NTA (tridentate).

Source: NIST Standard Reference Database 46 (SRD-46), stability measurements for Fe³⁺ with EDTA (ligand_6277) and NTA (ligand_6165), β_def_812 (ML formation) at 25 °C.

### Verdict & Explanation

**Verdict**
No gaps: EDTA forms the more stable Fe3+ complex, and side-by-side log β1 values at 25 °C and two ionic strengths are provided.

**Explanation**
Fe3+–EDTA (hexadentate) has log β1 ≈25.1 at I=0.1 M versus ≈15.9 for Fe3+–NTA (tridentate), ~9 orders-of-magnitude stronger binding. Both decrease at higher ionic strength (e.g., 23.8 vs 14.8 at I=1.0 M). Values are simple ML formation constants (no H+/OH− or polynuclear terms); Fe3+ hydrolysis and ligand protonation can affect speciation in practice.
