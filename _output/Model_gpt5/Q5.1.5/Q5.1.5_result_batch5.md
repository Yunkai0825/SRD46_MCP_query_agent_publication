Short answer: Yes—EDTA is a very good chelator for many metal ions. At 25 °C and modest ionic strength (~0.1 M), EDTA forms very stable 1:1 complexes (ML) with many transition and heavy metals; it is less strong for alkaline earths. The strength also depends on pH because the fully deprotonated EDTA (L) predominates only above roughly the HL↔L bracket near pH ~9.5 in these datasets.

Representative 1:1 formation constants (logK for [M] + [L] ⇌ [ML], beta_def_812) at 25 °C, I ≈ 0.1 M

| Metal (oxidation) | logK(ML) | Notes |
|---|---:|---|
| Fe(III) | 25.1 | Very strong complex |
| Hg(II) | 21.5 | Very strong |
| Cu(II) | 18.78 | Strong |
| Ni(II) | 18.4 | Strong |
| Zn(II) | 16.5 | Strong; see ionic-strength effect below |
| Cd(II) | 16.5 | Strong; see ionic-strength effect below |
| Co(II) | 16.45 | Strong |
| Al(III) | 16.4 | Strong, though hydrolysis can compete outside these conditions |
| La(III) | 15.36 | Moderate-to-strong among lanthanides |
| Ca(II) | 10.65–10.81 | Moderate (water hardness control) |
| Mg(II) | 8.79–8.96 | Moderate |

Ionic strength matters (same T and reaction, ML):
- Zn(II)–EDTA: logK = 18.0 at I = 0.0 M vs 16.5 at I = 0.1 M
- Cd(II)–EDTA: logK = 18.1 at I = 0.0 M vs 16.5 at I = 0.1 M

What this means:
- Across many transition and heavy metals (Fe3+, Hg2+, Cu2+, Ni2+, Zn2+, Cd2+, Co2+), EDTA shows high logK (≈16–25), indicating strong chelation. That’s why EDTA is widely used for metal sequestration, masking in analytical chemistry, and remediation.
- For alkaline earths (Ca2+, Mg2+), EDTA still binds appreciably (logK ~9–11), but much more weakly than for transition/heavy metals. It still effectively complexes “water hardness,” especially at higher pH where EDTA is more deprotonated.
- pH dependence: The constants above are defined for the fully deprotonated ligand L; in the SRD-46 records the HL↔L bracket is near 9.52. At neutral pH, conditional stability is lower because EDTA is not fully deprotonated; nonetheless, the relative ordering (Fe3+ ≫ Cu2+≈Ni2+≈Cd2+≈Zn2+ > Co2+ > Ca2+ > Mg2+) generally persists.

**Explanation**
SRD-46 shows EDTA forms strong 1:1 complexes with many transition/heavy metals (Fe3+ ~25, Hg2+ ~21.5, Cu2+/Ni2+ ~18–19), but binds alkaline earths more weakly (Ca2+ ~10–11, Mg2+ ~9). For some high-charge cations (e.g., Al3+), hydrolysis/competition can affect observed stability under certain conditions.
