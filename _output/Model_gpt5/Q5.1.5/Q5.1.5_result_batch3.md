Short answer: Yes — EDTA (ethylenediaminetetraacetate) is a very effective, broadly acting chelator in water.

What SRD‑46 shows
- Breadth: EDTA forms well‑defined 1:1 metal–ligand complexes (ML) with a wide range of divalent and trivalent metals (alkaline‑earths, transition metals, main‑group and f‑block).
- Speciation: In addition to ML, protonated complexes (MLH) and, for many 3+ ions, hydrolyzed forms (M(OH)L) are reported — consistent with strong binding modulated by pH and metal hydrolysis.
- Acid–base behavior: EDTA is hexadentate (N2O4 donors; H4L formal acid) and must be sufficiently deprotonated to maximize binding; its deprotonation ladder is well documented (below).

How to interpret this
- Because EDTA becomes fully deprotonated (L4−) mainly above pH ≈ 9–10, its conditional stability is strongly pH‑dependent. At lower pH, protonated ligand and MLH species appear, reducing available donor sites but still allowing complexation with many metals.
- The extensive, multi‑metal ML evidence in SRD‑46 — including for Ca2+/Mg2+ (water treatment and hardness control), transition metals like Cu2+/Ni2+/Zn2+, and hard 3+ ions (Al3+, Fe3+, lanthanides/actinides also present in the catalog) — is consistent with EDTA’s reputation as a strong, versatile chelator.
- Practical note: This breadth of binding means EDTA is powerful but not selective; it can sequester essential ions (e.g., Ca2+, Mg2+), so pH and competing ligands/solids must be considered in real systems.

### Verdict & Explanation

Verdict
Mostly yes. The answer correctly concludes EDTA is an effective, broad chelator and references SRD‑46 species data, but it lacks quantitative stability constants (log β) to substantiate “how strong.”

Explanation
SRD‑46 documents 1:1 EDTA complexes across many divalent and trivalent metals (e.g., Ca, Mg, Cu, Ni, Zn, Fe3+, Al3+), along with protonated (MLH) and hydrolyzed (M(OH)L) species—evidence of robust, widespread chelation. Strength is pH‑dependent, increasing as EDTA deprotonates to L4− (near pH 9–10); hydrolysis competes for 3+ ions. EDTA’s breadth implies high efficacy but low selectivity.
