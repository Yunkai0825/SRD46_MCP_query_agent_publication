**Prompt:** Is EDTA a good chelator?

**Tool calls:** 7  |  **Time:** 359s planning + 1401s execution + 23s verdict = 1783s total

Yes—EDTA is a very good, broad‑spectrum chelator in water, especially for hard and borderline metal ions. It is hexadentate (N2O4 donors), forming very stable 1:1 ML complexes with many divalent, trivalent, and tetravalent metals. Representative overall stability constants at ~25 °C (aqueous, I ≈ 0.1–0.5 M) illustrate its strength:

| Metal ion | log β (ML) | Notes |
|---|---:|---|
| Mg2+ | 8.7 | Moderate; binds appreciably at basic pH |
| Ca2+ | 10.7 | Useful for Ca2+ masking/complexation |
| Mn2+ | 13.9 | Transition‑metal binding stronger than alkaline‑earths |
| Fe2+ | 14.3 | |
| Co2+ | 16.3 | |
| Ni2+ | 18.6 | Very strong |
| Cu2+ | 18.8 | Very strong |
| Zn2+ | 16.5 | Strong |
| Cd2+ | 16.5 | Strong |
| Pb2+ | 18 | Very strong |
| Al3+ | 16 | Strong for trivalent hard ion |
| Fe3+ | 25 | Exceptionally strong for hard 3+ ions |

EDTA’s acid–base behavior controls when these complexes form. At 25 °C and I ≈ 0.1 M, the stepwise pKa values (from H5L → L) are:

| Step | pKa |
|---|---:|
| H5L → H4L | −1.5 |
| H4L → H3L | 2.00 |
| H3L → H2L | 2.69 |
| H2L → HL  | 6.13 |
| HL → L    | 9.52 |

Implications:
- Above pH ~6–7, EDTA becomes increasingly deprotonated and its conditional stability rises; near pH 8–10, many metal–EDTA complexes are very strong and often dominate speciation.
- It binds hard/borderline cations best (e.g., Ca2+, Fe3+, lanthanides), is weak for monovalent alkali ions (Na+, K+), and—while still strong for some soft 2+ (e.g., Hg2+)—is outperformed by thiol‑donor chelators in soft‑metal niches.
- For even higher affinity to M3+/M4+ ions, DTPA typically exceeds EDTA; for Ca2+ selectivity over Mg2+, EGTA is often preferred.

Source: NIST Critically Selected Stability Constants of Metal Complexes (SRD‑46).

Explanation
SRD‑46 data show EDTA (hexadentate N2O4) forms very stable 1:1 aqueous complexes with many di‑ and trivalent metals: log β ≈ 8–19 for common M2+ (e.g., Ca2+ 10.7, Cu2+ 18.8) and ≈ 16–25 for hard M3+ (e.g., Fe3+ ~25). Its chelation strength is strongly pH‑dependent; as EDTA deprotonates (notably above pH ~6–7 and more fully by pH ~9–10), conditional stabilities rise and often dominate speciation, whereas binding of alkali monovalent ions is weak; ionic strength and metal hardness/softness also modulate affinity.
