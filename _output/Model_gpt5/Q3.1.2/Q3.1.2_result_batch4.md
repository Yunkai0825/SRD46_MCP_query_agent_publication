Short answer
Histidine is your best bet for selectively complexing Cu²⁺ over Ni²⁺ at pH ≈ 7. It provides strong imidazole N‑donor binding to Cu²⁺ under near‑neutral conditions, while Ni²⁺ complexes are comparatively weaker or less favored at the same pH. By contrast, glycine tends to complex Ni²⁺ about as readily as Cu²⁺, and cysteine’s thiolate donor becomes available mainly above pH ≈ 8, reducing Cu-selectivity at pH 7.

What we verified in SRD‑46 during this session
- We resolved the metals and amino‑acid ligands in SRD‑46 and confirmed measured ML/ML₂ series in water near ambient conditions for Cu²⁺ and Ni²⁺ with histidine and glycine (others exist but were not numerically retrieved within the time budget).
- Ligand protonation states (pKa brackets) indicate the donor availability at pH 7 (histidine’s imidazole is near its pKa ~6, amine ~9.1; cysteine’s thiol deprotonation is ~8.18, so less thiolate at pH 7).

Why histidine stands out
- SRD‑46 shows robust Cu–histidine complex formation (ML/ML₂ and proton-linked steps) in water near ambient conditions, with more Cu coverage than Ni for histidine networks in our catalog snapshot.
- At pH ~7, histidine’s imidazole donor (near its pKa ≈ 6) is well poised to coordinate Cu²⁺, while Ni²⁺ complexes form but are generally less favored under the same conditions.
- Glycine shows abundant Ni complexes (including up to ML₃ in the Ni catalog), signaling weaker Cu/Ni discrimination.

### Verdict & Explanation

**Verdict**
Partial: The agent identifies histidine as best, but does not provide the quantitative selectivity ratio at pH 7 from SRD‑46, so the answer is not fully substantiated.

**Explanation**
At pH ~7, histidine’s imidazole (pKa ~6) is partly deprotonated and forms stronger Cu(II) complexes than Ni(II), which typically binds imidazole more weakly. Carboxylate-based amino acids (glycine, aspartate, glutamate, proline) show little Cu/Ni discrimination, while cysteine’s thiolate (pKa ≈ 8–8.5) is largely protonated at pH 7, limiting Cu selectivity. Exact selectivity depends on matched ionic strength, temperature, and protonation/competition, and should be confirmed with conditional logβ values.
