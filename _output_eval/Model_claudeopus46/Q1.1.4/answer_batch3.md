**Source:** NIST SRD-46 (Standard Reference Database 46)

### Chemistry Insights

The Ag⁺–ammonia system is one of the classic coordination chemistry examples. The large β₂ values (log β₂ ≈ 7–8) explain why the linear diamine complex [Ag(NH₃)₂]⁺ forms so readily — this is the basis of **Tollens' reagent** used in the silver mirror test for aldehydes. The spread in reported values (e.g., log β₁ from 3.2 to 3.8) reflects measurements at different ionic strengths; values at I → 0 sit at the higher end while those at high ionic strength (up to 5 M) are lower due to activity coefficient effects. The consistently negative ΔH values confirm that both complexation steps are **exothermic**, driven by the strong σ-donor ability of ammonia toward the soft Ag⁺ ion.
