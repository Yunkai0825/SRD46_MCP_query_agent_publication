## pKa Values for Citric Acid (Ligand: 2-Hydroxypropane-1,2,3-tricarboxylic acid)

Citric acid is a **triprotic acid** (defined as H₃L in SRD-46). The three measured pKa values at **25 °C** and **I = 0.1 M** are:

| Step | Equilibrium | pKa | T (°C) | I (mol/L) | VLM ID |
|------|-------------|-----|---------|-----------|--------|
| pKa₁ | H₂L ⇌ H₃L | **2.90** | 25 | 0.1 | vlm_157473 |
| pKa₂ | HL ⇌ H₂L | **4.35** | 25 | 0.1 | vlm_157459 |
| pKa₃ | L ⇌ HL | **5.65** | 25 | 0.1 | vlm_157439 |

### Chemistry Insight

These three dissociation steps correspond to the successive loss of protons from the three carboxylic acid groups. The relatively low pKa₁ (~2.9) reflects the strong electron-withdrawing effect of the adjacent hydroxyl and two other carboxylate groups, making the first proton the easiest to remove. The stepwise increase (ΔpKa ≈ 1.3–1.4 per step) is typical for polycarboxylic acids where each successive deprotonation becomes harder due to increasing negative charge on the molecule. These values make citric acid an excellent **buffer** in the pH 3–6 range and explain its widespread use in biological buffers, food chemistry, and as a chelating agent for metal ions.

*Source: NIST Standard Reference Database 46 (SRD-46)*
