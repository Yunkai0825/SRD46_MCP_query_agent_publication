## Ethylenediamine binds Ni²⁺ far more strongly than ammonia

Comparing the first stepwise stability constant (log K₁) for the reaction **[M] + [L] ⇌ [ML]** at **25 °C** using matched conditions from NIST SRD-46:

| Ligand | log K₁ (25 °C, I = 0.1 M) | log K₁ (25 °C, I = 0) |
|---|---|---|
| **Ammonia (NH₃)** | 2.73 | 2.72 |
| **Ethylenediamine (en)** | 7.30 | 7.32 |
| **Δ log K (en − NH₃)** | **+4.57** | **+4.60** |

### Answer

**Ethylenediamine binds Ni²⁺ roughly 10⁴·⁶ times more strongly than ammonia**, with a difference of **Δ log K₁ ≈ +4.6 log units** under matched conditions (25 °C, aqueous solution).

### Chemical insight

This dramatic enhancement is a textbook illustration of the **chelate effect**. Although each en molecule donates two nitrogen lone pairs (just like two separate NH₃ molecules), the bidentate chelate ring formation is entropically favoured: binding one en displaces more water molecules from the coordination sphere while paying only one translational-entropy penalty instead of two. The SRD-46 thermodynamic data support this — the entropy change (ΔS) for en binding is significantly more positive (+9.2 J mol⁻¹ K⁻¹ at I = 0.1 M) compared to NH₃ (+1.7 J mol⁻¹ K⁻¹), confirming that the chelate effect is largely entropy-driven in this system.

*Source: NIST Standard Reference Database 46 (Critically Selected Stability Constants of Metal Complexes).*
