**H⁺ (the proton)** tops the list with **4,081 different ligands** — more than double the runner-up, Cu²⁺. This makes sense because protonation equilibria (pKₐ values) are measured for virtually every acid-base-active ligand, making H⁺ the most universally studied "metal" in the database.

If we consider only *transition metal ions*, then **Cu²⁺** holds the crown with **1,951 distinct ligands**. Copper(II) is renowned for its versatile coordination chemistry — it readily forms stable complexes with N-, O-, and S-donor ligands — which explains why it has been so extensively studied. The rest of the top 5 transition metals (Ni²⁺, Zn²⁺, Co²⁺, Cd²⁺) are all divalent ions of the Irving–Williams series, consistent with the well-known trend that these ions are the most commonly studied in stability-constant work.

*Source: NIST Standard Reference Database 46 (Critically Selected Stability Constants of Metal Complexes).*
