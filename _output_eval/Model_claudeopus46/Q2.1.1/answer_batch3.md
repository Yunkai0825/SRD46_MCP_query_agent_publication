## Fe³⁺ Stability Constants: EDTA vs NTA

**EDTA forms a dramatically more stable complex with Fe³⁺ than NTA**, with log K₁ values roughly **9–10 orders of magnitude higher**.

### Side-by-Side Comparison (log K₁ for [ML]/[M][L])

| Ligand | log K₁ | T (°C) | I (mol/L) | Electrolyte |
|--------|--------|--------|-----------|-------------|
| **EDTA** | **25.1** | 25 | 0.1 | KNO₃ |
| **EDTA** | **25.1** | 20 | 0.1 | KNO₃ |
| **EDTA** | **25.0** | 25 | 1.0 | KNO₃ |
| **EDTA** | **24.23** | 25 | 1.0 | NaClO₄ |
| **NTA** | **15.9** | 25 | 0.1 | KNO₃ |
| **NTA** | **15.87** | 20 | 0.1 | KNO₃ |
| **NTA** | **15.2** | 25 | 1.0 | NaClO₄ |
| **NTA** | **15.2** | 25 | 0.5 | NaClO₄ |

| Ligand | log K₁ | Δ log K |
|--------|--------|---------|
| EDTA | 25.1 | — |
| NTA | 15.9 | −9.2 |

### Chemistry Insight

The ~9 log-unit advantage of EDTA over NTA is a textbook illustration of the **chelate effect** and **denticity**. EDTA is a hexadentate ligand (2 N + 4 carboxylate O donors) that fully wraps around Fe³⁺, while NTA is only tetradentate (1 N + 3 carboxylate O donors). The two additional donor groups in EDTA provide extra coordination and a much larger entropic driving force for complex formation. This difference is why EDTA is the preferred chelator in analytical chemistry (e.g., complexometric titrations) and in environmental remediation of iron-contaminated systems, where near-quantitative metal sequestration is required.
