## Fe³⁺ Stability: EDTA vs NTA

**EDTA wins decisively.** The overall formation constant log K₁ for Fe³⁺–EDTA is roughly **10 orders of magnitude larger** than for Fe³⁺–NTA, reflecting EDTA's superior hexadentate chelation versus NTA's tetradentate binding.

### Side-by-Side Comparison — log K₁ ([ML]/[M][L])

| Condition | Fe³⁺–EDTA (ligand_6277) | Fe³⁺–NTA (ligand_6165) | Δ log K |
|-----------|------------------------|------------------------|---------|
| 20 °C, I = 0.1 M, KNO₃ | **27.7** | **16.26** | +11.4 |
| 25 °C, I = 0.1 M, KCl | **25.1** | **15.87** | +9.2 |
| 25 °C, I = 0.5 M, KNO₃/NaClO₄ | **26.5** | **15.2** | +11.3 |
| 25 °C, I = 1.0 M, NaClO₄ | **24.23** | **15.4–15.5** | +8.8 |

### Additional NTA Species with Fe³⁺

NTA can also form a 1:2 complex (ML₂) with Fe³⁺, with log β₂ values around 24.3 at I = 0.1 M — comparable in magnitude to the 1:1 EDTA complex, but requiring two NTA ligands.

| Beta Definition | Equilibrium | log K (25 °C, I = 0.1 M) |
|-----------------|-------------|--------------------------|
| β₂: [ML₂]/[M][L]² | M + 2L ⇌ ML₂ | 24.3 |
| [MHL]/[ML][H] | ML + H ⇌ MHL | 1.3 |

### Chemical Insight

The ~10 log-unit advantage of EDTA over NTA is a textbook illustration of the **chelate effect** and **denticity**. EDTA wraps around Fe³⁺ with six donor atoms (2 N + 4 carboxylate O), fully satisfying the metal's octahedral coordination sphere in a single ligand. NTA, with only four donor atoms (1 N + 3 carboxylate O), leaves coordination sites available — hence the tendency to form ML₂ species. In practical applications such as water treatment and soil remediation, EDTA's dramatically higher affinity for Fe³⁺ makes it the stronger sequestrant, though NTA is sometimes preferred for its greater biodegradability.
