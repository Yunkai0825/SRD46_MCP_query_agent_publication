# Hg²⁺ Donor Atom Preference: Sulfur > Nitrogen > Oxygen

## Hypothesis

**Hg²⁺ strongly prefers sulfur-donor ligands over nitrogen-donor ligands, and nitrogen-donors over oxygen-donors.** This is entirely consistent with the classification of Hg²⁺ as a quintessential **soft acid** in Pearson's Hard-Soft Acid-Base (HSAB) theory — it forms its most stable complexes with soft, polarizable donor atoms like sulfur.

## Evidence: log K₁ Values from NIST SRD-46

All values are for the reaction **[Hg²⁺] + [L] ⇌ [HgL]** (beta_def_812) at 25 °C in aqueous solution.

### Sulfur-Donor Ligands

| Ligand | Donor Atom(s) | log K₁ | I (mol/L) |
|--------|--------------|--------|-----------|
| Thiocyanate (SCN⁻) | S (ambidentate) | **17.26** | 0.0 |
| L-Cysteine | S (thiolate) | **14.21** | 0.1 |
| Thiourea | S (thione) | **11.4** | 0.5 |

### Nitrogen-Donor Ligands

| Ligand | Donor Atom(s) | log K₁ | I (mol/L) |
|--------|--------------|--------|-----------|
| Ethylenediamine | N,N (chelate) | **14.3** | 0.1 |
| Ammonia | N | **8.8** | 2.0 |
| Imidazole | N | **6.91** | 3.0 |
| Pyridine | N | **5.13** | 0.5 |

### Oxygen-Donor Ligands

| Ligand | Donor Atom(s) | log K₁ | I (mol/L) |
|--------|--------------|--------|-----------|
| Hydroxide (OH⁻) | O | **10.6** | 0.0 |
| Oxalic acid (oxalate) | O,O (chelate) | **6.98** | 0.1 |

### Halide Reference Series (Soft → Hard)

| Ligand | log K₁ | I (mol/L) |
|--------|--------|-----------|
| Iodide (I⁻) | **12.87** | 0.5 |
| Bromide (Br⁻) | **9.05** | 0.5 |
| Chloride (Cl⁻) | **6.74** | 0.5 |

## Analysis

1. **Sulfur-donors dominate.** The three S-donor ligands show the highest log K₁ values: thiocyanate (17.26), cysteine (14.21), and thiourea (11.4). Even the weakest S-donor here (thiourea, 11.4) exceeds most N-donor and all O-donor monodentate ligands.

2. **Nitrogen-donors are intermediate.** Ethylenediamine's high value (14.3) is partly inflated by the **chelate effect** — it binds through two N atoms simultaneously. For monodentate N-donors, the values are much lower: ammonia (8.8), imidazole (6.91), and pyridine (5.13). This places simple N-donors roughly 6–12 log units below the best S-donors.

3. **Oxygen-donors are weakest.** Hydroxide (10.6) is respectable due to its high charge density, but the neutral O-donor oxalate (6.98, also a chelate) is far weaker. Pure O-donor ligands consistently fall at the bottom of the ranking.

4. **The halide series confirms soft-acid behavior.** The trend I⁻ (12.87) > Br⁻ (9.05) > Cl⁻ (6.74) — increasing stability with increasing softness/polarizability — is the textbook fingerprint of a soft metal ion.

## Chemical Insight

The ~12 log-unit gap between thiocyanate (S-bound, log K₁ = 17.26) and pyridine (N-bound, log K₁ = 5.13) translates to a **free energy difference of ~69 kJ/mol** in favor of Hg–S bonding. This extraordinary sulfur affinity explains why:

- **Mercury toxicity** is driven by its binding to cysteine thiol groups in proteins, disrupting enzyme function — the cysteine log K₁ of 14.21 quantifies this devastating affinity.
- **Cinnabar (HgS)** is the most thermodynamically stable mercury mineral, reflecting the extreme Hg–S bond strength.
- **Chelation therapy** for mercury poisoning uses thiol-containing agents (e.g., DMSA, D-penicillamine) rather than amine- or carboxylate-based chelators, because only S-donors can compete with biological thiol targets.

Note on thiocyanate: SCN⁻ is ambidentate (can bind via N or S). The exceptionally high log K₁ of 17.26 with Hg²⁺ strongly suggests S-coordination, consistent with HSAB predictions — hard metals bind the N-end while soft metals like Hg²⁺ bind the S-end.

*Source: NIST Standard Reference Database 46 (Critically Selected Stability Constants of Metal Complexes)*
