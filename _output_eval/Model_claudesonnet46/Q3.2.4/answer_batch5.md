## 4. Thermodynamic Reasoning

### 4.1 Hard–Soft Acid–Base (HSAB) Theory

| Donor type | Fe³⁺ preference | Fe²⁺ preference | Rationale |
|------------|----------------|----------------|-----------|
| O-donors (OH⁻, F⁻, oxalate, catecholate) | **Very strong** | Moderate | Fe³⁺ is a hard acid (high charge density, d⁵ high-spin); hard O-donors match perfectly |
| N-donors (NH₃, en, bipy, phen) | Moderate | **Strong** | Fe²⁺ is softer (lower charge, more polarizable); N-donors are borderline-soft |
| S/π-donors (SCN⁻, CN⁻) | Weak–moderate | **Stronger** | Fe²⁺ can back-donate into π* of CN⁻/bipy; Fe³⁺ cannot as effectively |
| Halides (F⁻ ≫ Cl⁻) | F⁻ strong, Cl⁻ weak | Both weak | Hard/hard match for F⁻; Cl⁻ is too soft for Fe³⁺ |

**Fe³⁺** (d⁵, high-spin, charge +3, ionic radius ~0.645 Å) is one of the hardest transition-metal acids. It forms its strongest bonds with hard, electronegative O-donors. The Irving–Williams series does not apply directly here because Fe³⁺ is outside the divalent series, but the principle holds: high charge density → preference for hard donors.

**Fe²⁺** (d⁶, charge +2, ionic radius ~0.78 Å) is softer and more polarizable. It shows markedly higher affinity for N-donors, especially aromatic ones that can participate in π-backbonding.

### 4.2 Chelate Effect

Multidentate ligands show dramatically enhanced stability due to the **chelate effect** (favorable entropy of ring closure):

| Ligand | Denticity | Rings formed | Relative log β enhancement |
|--------|-----------|-------------|---------------------------|
| Acetate | 1 | 0 | Baseline |
| Oxalate/Malonate | 2 | 1 | +2–4 log units |
| NTA | 4 | 3 | +6–8 log units |
| EDTA | 6 | 5 | +10–12 log units |
| CDTA | 6 | 5+cyclohexyl | Slightly > EDTA (rigidity pre-organizes) |
| DTPA | 8 | 7 | +14–16 log units |

The **CDTA > EDTA** ordering for Fe(III) is an edge case: the cyclohexylene backbone of CDTA pre-organizes the donor arms, reducing the entropic cost of chelation and giving slightly higher log β than EDTA despite identical donor sets.

### 4.3 Crystal-Field Stabilization Energy (CFSE)

| System | Spin state | CFSE contribution | Effect on log β |
|--------|-----------|------------------|-----------------|
| Fe³⁺ + O-donors (high-spin d⁵) | High-spin | **Zero CFSE** (half-filled d shell) | Stability purely electrostatic/HSAB |
| Fe²⁺ + weak N-donors (high-spin d⁶) | High-spin | Small CFSE | Modest enhancement |
| Fe²⁺ + bipy/phen (strong field) | **Low-spin d⁶** | Large CFSE (~2.4 Δₒ) | **Major enhancement** of log β |

This explains the **bipy/phen anomaly for Fe(II)**: log β₃ for Fe(II)(phen)₃²⁺ is exceptionally high (~21) because the strong-field aromatic ligands induce a high-spin → low-spin transition, releasing substantial CFSE. Fe(III) cannot benefit as much because d⁵ high-spin has zero CFSE regardless.

### 4.4 Hydroxamate and Catecholate: Siderophore Chemistry

The **hydroxamate** and **catecholate/Tiron** ligands show exceptionally high affinity for Fe(III). This is the thermodynamic basis of **siderophore chemistry**: microorganisms exploit these donor types to scavenge Fe³⁺ from the environment. The catecholate dianion (O,O-bidentate, hard donor) forms 5-membered chelate rings with Fe³⁺ and benefits from charge complementarity (3− ligand charge × 3+ metal charge). Tiron (catechol-3,5-disulfonate) is the synthetic analog used in analytical chemistry for exactly this reason.

### 4.5 Hydrolysis Competition

Fe(III) undergoes extensive hydrolysis even at mildly acidic pH (log K_OH ≈ 11–12 for FeOH²⁺), forming polynuclear species like Fe₂(OH)₂⁴⁺, Fe₃(OH)₄⁵⁺, and ultimately Fe(OH)₃(s). The catalog shows **8 distinct hydroxo-species** for Fe(III) vs. only **5 for Fe(II)**, and the Fe(III) network includes solid-phase equilibria (goethite α-FeOOH, hematite α-Fe₂O₃). This means any ligand competing with OH⁻ for Fe(III) must overcome a very high hydrolysis baseline — only the strongest chelators (catecholates, hydroxamates, EDTA-type) can do so effectively at physiological pH.

## 6. Key Conclusions

1. **Fe(III) ≫ Fe(II) for all hard O-donors** by 5–12 log units, driven by the hard acid/hard base match and the high charge density of Fe³⁺.
2. **Fe(II) ≫ Fe(III) for aromatic N-donors** (bipy, phen) by up to 16 log units — the single most dramatic reversal — due to CFSE from the low-spin d⁶ configuration and π-backbonding.
3. **Chelate effect** amplifies stability for both ions proportionally to denticity; CDTA slightly outperforms EDTA for Fe(III) due to geometric pre-organization.
4. **Hydroxamates and catecholates** are the biological solution to Fe(III) scavenging: they combine hard O,O-bidentate coordination with favorable chelate ring geometry, achieving log β values that compete with hydrolysis even at near-neutral pH.
5. **Fe(III) hydrolysis** is a dominant competing equilibrium (8 polynuclear species in the database), making effective ligand competition thermodynamically demanding and explaining why only high-denticity, hard-donor chelators are used in iron-overload therapy (e.g., desferrioxamine, deferasirox).
