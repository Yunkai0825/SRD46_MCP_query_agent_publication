## pKa Comparison: Glycine vs. Alanine

Here are the SRD-46 measured pKa values at matched conditions (25°C, I = 0.1 M):

| Property | Glycine (ligand_5760) | Alanine (ligand_5761) | Difference (ΔpKa) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **pKa₁** (carboxyl, –COOH) | 2.33 | 2.33 | **0.00** |
| **pKa₂** (ammonium, –NH₃⁺) | 9.57 | 9.71 | **+0.14** |
| Temperature | 25°C | 25°C | — |
| Ionic strength | 0.1 M | 0.1 M | — |

---

### Are the differences significant?

- **pKa₁ (carboxyl):** Identical at 2.33 — no difference whatsoever. Both amino acids have the same carboxyl acidity.
- **pKa₂ (ammonium):** Alanine is **0.14 pKa units higher** than glycine (9.71 vs. 9.57). This difference is small but chemically meaningful — it exceeds the typical experimental uncertainty of ±0.02–0.05 pKa units for high-quality potentiometric determinations.

---

### Chemistry Insight

The slightly higher pKa₂ of alanine reflects the **electron-donating methyl group** on the α-carbon. The methyl substituent increases electron density at the nitrogen, making the –NH₃⁺ group slightly more reluctant to release a proton (i.e., a stronger base / weaker acid). This is a classic inductive effect: alkyl groups stabilize the conjugate base (–NH₂) relative to glycine's unsubstituted α-carbon.

The identical pKa₁ values suggest the methyl group has negligible influence on the distant carboxyl group's acidity — the inductive effect attenuates rapidly over two bonds.

In practical terms, both amino acids behave very similarly in aqueous solution and in metal coordination chemistry, which is consistent with the SRD-46 database showing both ligands binding a similar suite of metals (Cu²⁺, Ni²⁺, Zn²⁺, Co²⁺, etc.).

*Source: NIST SRD-46 (Critically Selected Stability Constants of Metal Complexes)*
