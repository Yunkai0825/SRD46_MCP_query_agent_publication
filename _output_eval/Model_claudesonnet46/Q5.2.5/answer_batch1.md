## Electrode Potential of Cu²⁺/Cu⁰

The standard electrode potential for the Cu²⁺/Cu⁰ half-reaction is **outside the scope of the NIST SRD-46 database**, which focuses on **metal–ligand stability constants** (formation constants, β values) and **ligand pKa values** in aqueous solution — not electrochemical reduction potentials.

---

### What you're looking for

The standard reduction potential:

$$\text{Cu}^{2+}(aq) + 2e^- \rightarrow \text{Cu}^0(s) \quad E^\circ = +0.337 \text{ V vs. SHE}$$

This value (+0.337 V at 25 °C, standard hydrogen electrode reference) comes from **electrochemical thermodynamic databases**, such as:

| Source | Value (E°, V vs. SHE) |
|---|---|
| NIST WebBook / CODATA | **+0.3419 V** |
| CRC Handbook of Chemistry | **+0.337 V** |
| Atkins' Physical Chemistry | **+0.34 V** |

---

### Chemistry insight

- Because E° > 0, Cu²⁺ is **more easily reduced than H⁺**, which is why copper does **not** dissolve in dilute HCl (unlike zinc or iron).
- The positive potential also explains why copper is used as a **cathode material** in electroplating and electrorefining.
- In the presence of complexing ligands (e.g., ammonia, EDTA), the **effective reduction potential shifts** — and *that* is where SRD-46 becomes relevant, since it stores the stability constants needed to calculate the conditional potential via the Nernst equation.

For electrode potential data, I recommend:
- 🔗 [NIST Chemistry WebBook](https://webbook.nist.gov)
- 🔗 [NIST Standard Reference Database 20 (NIST-JANAF)](https://janaf.nist.gov)
- 🔗 CRC Handbook of Chemistry and Physics
