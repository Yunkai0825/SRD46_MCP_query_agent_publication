## Results: Ni²⁺ Binding — Ammonia vs. Ethylenediamine

All data from **NIST SRD-46**, reaction: Ni²⁺ + L ⇌ [NiL]²⁺ (β₁, cumulative = stepwise K₁ for 1:1 complex)

| Ligand | log β₁ (25 °C, I ≈ 0 M) | log β₁ (25 °C, I = 0.1 M) |
|---|---|---|
| Ammonia (NH₃) | **2.72** | 2.73 |
| Ethylenediamine (en) | **7.32** | 7.30 |
| **Δ (en − NH₃)** | **+4.60** | **+4.57** |

---

### 🏆 Ethylenediamine wins — by ~4.6 log units

**Ethylenediamine binds Ni²⁺ roughly 40,000× more strongly** than ammonia on a per-ligand basis (10^4.6 ≈ 40,000).

---

### Why? The Chelate Effect

Both ligands donate nitrogen lone pairs to Ni²⁺, but ethylenediamine (en) is a **bidentate chelate** — its two –NH₂ groups simultaneously coordinate to the same metal center, forming a stable five-membered ring. This confers a large **entropic advantage**: replacing two monodentate NH₃ molecules with one en molecule releases one extra water/solvent molecule and increases the disorder of the system (ΔS is more favorable). The thermodynamic data confirm this: for Ni²⁺–en, ΔH ≈ 0 kJ/mol while ΔS = +9.2 J/mol·K, meaning the binding is **entropy-driven** — a hallmark of the chelate effect. In contrast, Ni²⁺–NH₃ binding is **enthalpy-driven** (ΔH = −15.1 kJ/mol) but entropically unfavorable.

This ~4.6 log unit chelate advantage is consistent with the well-known rule of thumb that bidentate chelates are ~10³–10⁵ times more stable than analogous monodentate complexes.

*Source: NIST SRD-46, vlm_173185 (NH₃) and vlm_122371 (en)*
