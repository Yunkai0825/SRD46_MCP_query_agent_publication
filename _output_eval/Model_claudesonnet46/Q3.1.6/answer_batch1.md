---

### Chemistry Insights

**1. Denticity drives stability.** The ranking follows the chelate effect almost perfectly: EDTA (hexadentate: 4 carboxylates + 2 amines, log β = 15.5) > NTA (tetradentate, log β ≈ 11) > EDTAP (tetradentate variant, log β = 10.78) > EDDA (tetradentate, log β = 8.6) > dipicolinic acid (tridentate, log β = 7.9). Each additional donor arm contributes roughly 2–3 log units to overall stability.

**2. La³⁺ is a hard, large acid.** As the largest lanthanide(III) ion (ionic radius ~1.03 Å), La³⁺ has the lowest charge density in the series and forms its strongest bonds with hard oxygen donors (carboxylates). The aminopolycarboxylates dominate the ranking because they combine multiple carboxylate oxygens with amine nitrogens in a preorganized chelate framework perfectly suited to La³⁺'s high coordination number preference (8–10).

**3. Real-world relevance.** The high log K of La³⁺–EDTA (15.5) underpins lanthanide separation chemistry and is structurally analogous to the Gd³⁺–DOTA/DTPA systems used as MRI contrast agents. NTA (log K ≈ 11) is studied as a biodegradable alternative chelator for environmental remediation, where EDTA's environmental persistence is a concern. Dipicolinic acid (log K = 7.9) is notable as a purely aromatic dicarboxylate that achieves moderate stability through its rigid, preorganized tridentate N,O,O binding pocket.
