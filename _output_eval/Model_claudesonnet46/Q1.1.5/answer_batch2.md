## Oxalic Acid pKa Values — SRD-46 Results

Oxalic acid (**ethanedioic acid**, `ligand_8872`, SMILES: `O=C(O)C(=O)O`) is a **diprotic acid** with **two dissociation steps**:

| Step | Reaction | pKa | T (°C) | I (mol/L) |
|------|----------|-----|--------|-----------|
| 1st dissociation | H₂L → HL⁻ + H⁺ | **−1.2** | 25 | 0.1 |
| 2nd dissociation | HL⁻ → L²⁻ + H⁺ | **3.8** | 25 | 0.1 |

### Summary

- **Number of dissociation steps: 2**
- **pKa₁ = −1.2** (H₂C₂O₄ → HC₂O₄⁻): The first proton is extremely acidic — in fact, with a negative pKa, oxalic acid's first dissociation is essentially complete even in moderately acidic solutions, making it behave like a strong acid for this step.
- **pKa₂ = 3.8** (HC₂O₄⁻ → C₂O₄²⁻): The second proton is moderately acidic, typical of a carboxylic acid. At physiological or near-neutral pH, oxalate is predominantly in the fully deprotonated L²⁻ form.

The large gap (~5 pKa units) between the two steps means there is a wide pH window where the monoanion **HC₂O₄⁻** is the dominant species. This is chemically significant: oxalate's strong chelating ability (via the L²⁻ dianion) underpins its role in kidney stone formation and its use as a ligand in coordination chemistry.

*Source: NIST SRD-46 (Critically Selected Stability Constants of Metal Complexes), aqueous solution, 25 °C, I = 0.1 mol/L.*
