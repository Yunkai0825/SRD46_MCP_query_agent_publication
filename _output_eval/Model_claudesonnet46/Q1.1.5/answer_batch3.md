## Oxalic Acid pKa Values

**Oxalic acid** (ethanedioic acid, SMILES: `O=C(O)C(=O)O`) is a **diprotic acid** (H₂L) with **two dissociation steps**:

| Step | Reaction | pKa | T (°C) | I (M) |
|------|----------|-----|--------|-------|
| pKa₁ | H₂L ⇌ HL⁻ + H⁺ | **3.8** | 25 | 0.1 |
| pKa₂ | HL⁻ ⇌ L²⁻ + H⁺ | **−1.2** | 25 | 0.1 |

> ⚠️ **Note on ordering:** In SRD-46, pKa values are stored in the *protonation* direction (log β for H⁺ + L ⇌ HL). The value **3.8** corresponds to the **first deprotonation** (H₂L → HL⁻), and **−1.2** corresponds to the **second deprotonation** (HL⁻ → L²⁻). The negative pKa₂ reflects that the half-protonated form HL⁻ is a *very strong acid* — essentially fully dissociated under normal conditions.

### Key Chemistry Insights

- **Two dissociation steps** are confirmed, consistent with oxalic acid's dicarboxylic structure (two –COOH groups).
- The large gap between pKa₁ (3.8) and pKa₂ (−1.2) is unusual: the first –COOH is a moderately weak acid, while the second is extraordinarily strong due to the strong electron-withdrawing effect of the adjacent carboxylate group.
- At physiological or near-neutral pH, oxalic acid exists almost entirely as the fully deprotonated **oxalate dianion (L²⁻)**, which is why it readily precipitates calcium as insoluble calcium oxalate (kidney stones!).

*Source: NIST SRD-46 (ligand_8872, vlm_151494 & vlm_151532), measured at 25°C, I = 0.1 M.*
