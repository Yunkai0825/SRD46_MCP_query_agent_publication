## 🏆 Verdict: **Chloride forms the stronger Cu²⁺ complex**

At 25 °C extrapolated to zero ionic strength, **Cu²⁺–Cl⁻** has log β₁ = **0.40**, compared to **log β₁ = 0.03** for **Cu²⁺–Br⁻**. The difference of Δlog β₁ ≈ 0.37 is modest but reproducible across many independent measurements in the SRD-46 database (15 entries for chloride, 7 for bromide).

---

### Chemistry Insight

1. **Both complexes are extremely weak.** Log β₁ values near zero mean β₁ ≈ 1–2.5, so the equilibrium barely favors complex formation over free ions. Cu²⁺ has a very stable hydration shell (Jahn–Teller distorted octahedral aqua complex), and simple halides struggle to displace water.

2. **Why Cl⁻ > Br⁻ for Cu²⁺?** Although Br⁻ is a softer base and might be expected to bind better to a borderline metal like Cu²⁺, the +2 oxidation state retains significant hard-acid character. The smaller, harder Cl⁻ wins slightly in this competition. This contrasts with Cu⁺ (a soft acid), which strongly prefers Br⁻ and I⁻.

3. **Real-world relevance.** These tiny constants explain why Cu²⁺ speciation in seawater, biological fluids, or dilute HCl/HBr solutions is dominated by free Cu²⁺ and hydroxo/carbonate species rather than CuCl⁺ or CuBr⁺. Halide complexation of Cu²⁺ is negligible at typical environmental concentrations, and only at very high halide concentrations (e.g., concentrated HCl) do higher-order chloro-cuprate species (CuCl₃⁻, CuCl₄²⁻) become significant.
