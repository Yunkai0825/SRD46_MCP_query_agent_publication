## Fe³⁺ Complex Stability: EDTA vs NTA

Here are the **log K₁ values** (overall 1:1 formation constant, β = [ML]/[M][L]) from NIST SRD-46:

| Condition | Ligand | log K₁ | T (°C) | I (M) |
|-----------|--------|--------|--------|-------|
| Low ionic strength | **EDTA** (ligand_6277) | **25.1** | 25 | 0.1 |
| Low ionic strength | **NTA** (ligand_6165) | **15.9** | 25 | 0.1 |
| High ionic strength | **EDTA** (ligand_6277) | **23.8** | 25 | 1.0 |
| High ionic strength | **NTA** (ligand_6165) | **14.78** | 25 | 1.0 |

### 🏆 Winner: EDTA — by ~9 log units

**EDTA forms a dramatically more stable complex with Fe³⁺ than NTA**, with log K₁ = **25.1** vs **15.9** at 25 °C, I = 0.1 M — a difference of **~9.2 log units**, meaning EDTA binds Fe³⁺ roughly **10⁹× more strongly** than NTA under the same conditions.

### Chemistry Insight

This enormous gap is primarily a **denticity effect**: EDTA is a hexadentate ligand (4 carboxylates + 2 amines), while NTA is tetradentate (3 carboxylates + 1 amine). EDTA wraps around Fe³⁺ more completely, satisfying its octahedral coordination sphere and releasing more water molecules (greater entropic gain, ΔS = 442.7 J/mol·K for EDTA vs 349.8 for NTA). The thermodynamic data also reveals that Fe³⁺–EDTA formation is **exothermic** (ΔH = −11.3 kJ/mol), while Fe³⁺–NTA is **endothermic** (ΔH = +13.4 kJ/mol) — meaning EDTA wins on both enthalpic and entropic grounds. This is why EDTA is the preferred chelating agent in iron-sequestration applications (e.g., micronutrient fertilizers, analytical masking agents).

*Source: NIST SRD-46 (vlm_108639 for EDTA; vlm_105553 for NTA)*
