## Key Observations

1. **Ionic-strength effect:** At 25 °C, log β₁ dips from 8.56 (I = 0) to a minimum of ~8.10 at I = 0.5 M, then rises slightly at higher ionic strengths — a classic Debye–Hückel/salting-out interplay.

2. **Temperature effect:** Both β₁ and β₂ decrease modestly from 10 → 37 °C (e.g., log β₁: 8.80 → 7.99), consistent with the mildly exothermic ΔH values (−25 to −27 kJ/mol for CuL⁺). The large positive ΔS (+79–118 J/mol·K) reflects desolvation of both Cu²⁺ and glycinate upon chelate ring formation.

3. **Chelate stability:** The log β₂ ≈ 15.1–15.7 at 25 °C makes Cu(glycinate)₂ one of the most stable binary amino-acid complexes of Cu²⁺, underpinning its biological relevance in copper transport and its use as a nutritional supplement form of copper.
