Using the matched SRD-46 Cu²⁺–ammonia dataset at **25 °C** and preferably **I = 0** (the only complete matched set for ML–ML₄ in the retrieved data), the cumulative constants are:

| Species | log β |
|---|---:|
| Cu(NH₃)²⁺ = ML | 4.02 |
| Cu(NH₃)₂²⁺ = ML₂ | 7.4 |
| Cu(NH₃)₃²⁺ = ML₃ | 10.2 |
| Cu(NH₃)₄²⁺ = ML₄ | 12.3 |

From these, the **stepwise** constants are:

| Step | Expression | log K |
|---|---|---:|
| K₁ | β₁ | 4.02 |
| K₂ | β₂/β₁ | 3.38 |
| K₃ | β₃/β₂ | 2.8 |
| K₄ | β₄/β₃ | 2.1 |

So yes: **K₁ > K₂ > K₃ > K₄**, i.e. they **decrease monotonically** in this dataset.

What does that imply geometrically?

| Observation | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| Smooth monotonic drop from K₁ to K₄ | No special jump or extra stabilization at the 4th NH₃ addition in these stepwise constants |
| Still substantial stability through ML₄ | Cu²⁺ strongly supports a **four-NH₃ equatorial coordination set** |
| Cu²⁺ chemistry context | Consistent with **Jahn–Teller distorted octahedral** Cu(II) in water, often described as **[Cu(NH₃)₄(H₂O)₂]²⁺**, with four stronger equatorial Cu–N bonds and weaker axial ligation |

So the constants do **not** show a nonmonotonic “special” K₄; instead they show a regular decline, while the existence of a stable ML₄ complex still supports the usual picture of **four preferred equatorial ammonia ligands** around Cu(II), rather than a truly regular six-coordinate environment.

**Source:** NIST SRD-46, Cu²⁺ + ammonia stability data retrieved for 25 °C, I = 0.
