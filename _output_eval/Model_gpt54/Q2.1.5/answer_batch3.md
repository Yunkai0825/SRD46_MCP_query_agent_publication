SRD-46 shows **ethylenediamine binds much more strongly than ammonia to Ni²⁺**.

Using the **1:1 formation constant** for the matched reaction  
\[
\mathrm{Ni^{2+} + L \rightleftharpoons NiL^{2+}}
\]
at **25 °C** and **ionic strength \(I = 0\)**:

| Ligand | SRD-46 ligand ID | Reaction | log K |
|---|---:|---|---:|
| Ammonia | ligand_10103 | \([M] + [L] \leftrightarrow [ML]\) | 2.72 |
| Ethylenediamine | ligand_7029 | \([M] + [L] \leftrightarrow [ML]\) | 6.98–7.90* |

So the difference is:

| Comparison | Δlog K = logK(en) − logK(NH3) |
|---|---:|
| Using the lowest reported en value | 4.26 |
| Using the highest reported en value | 5.18 |

\*SRD-46 summary for Ni²⁺–ethylenediamine reports 1:1 log K values spanning **6.98 to 7.90** over reported measurements (25–37 °C, \(I = 0\)–3 M). Even the lowest value is far above ammonia.

In short: **ethylenediamine is stronger by about 4–5 log units**, i.e. roughly **10,000 to 100,000 times larger K** for the 1:1 complex. That large boost is the classic **chelate effect**: en is bidentate, while ammonia is only monodentate.

**Source:** NIST SRD-46 stability-constant entries for Ni²⁺ with ammonia and ethylenediamine.
