Yes — **ethylenediamine (en)** binds **much more strongly** to **Ni²⁺** than **ammonia** in SRD-46.

Using the **matched 1:1 formation constant**  
\[
\mathrm{Ni^{2+} + L \rightleftharpoons NiL^{2+}}
\]
at **25 °C** and **ionic strength ≈ 1 M**:

| Ligand | log K₁ |
|---|---:|
| Ammonia (NH₃) | 2.80 |
| Ethylenediamine (en) | 7.56 |

So the difference is:

| Comparison | Δlog K |
|---|---:|
| log K₁(en) − log K₁(NH₃) | **4.76** |

That means **ethylenediamine is stronger by about 4.8 log units**, or roughly **10^4.76 ≈ 5.8 × 10⁴ times** in K.

Chemically, this is the classic **chelate effect**: en is bidentate, so it gains extra stability over monodentate NH₃ when binding Ni²⁺.

**Source:** NIST SRD-46 stability-constant records for **Ni²⁺–NH₃** and **Ni²⁺–ethylenediamine**.
