Ni²⁺ binds **ethylenediamine (en)** much more strongly than **ammonia (NH₃)**.

Using the **1:1 formation constant** for `[M] + [L] ⇌ [ML]` at **25 °C** and **I = 0.1 M** from SRD-46:

| Ligand | System | log K₁ |
|---|---|---:|
| Ammonia | Ni²⁺ + NH₃ ⇌ Ni(NH₃)²⁺ | 2.73 |
| Ethylenediamine | Ni²⁺ + en ⇌ Ni(en)²⁺ | 7.30 |

So the difference is:

| Comparison | Δlog K |
|---|---:|
| log K₁(en) − log K₁(NH₃) | 4.57 |

That means **ethylenediamine binds more strongly by 4.57 log units**, i.e. about **10^4.57 ≈ 3.7 × 10⁴ times** stronger for the first binding step.

Chemically, this is the classic **chelate effect**: ethylenediamine is bidentate, so one ligand forms a ring with Ni²⁺, giving a much more stable complex than monodentate ammonia.

*Source: NIST SRD-46 / stability-constant records for Ni²⁺–ammonia and Ni²⁺–ethylenediamine at 25 °C, 0.1 M ionic strength.*
