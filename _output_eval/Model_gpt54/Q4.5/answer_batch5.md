Using SRD-46 data for Ca²⁺ complexes and ligand pKa values, there appears to be a **broad positive relationship** between ligand basicity and Ca²⁺ binding strength, but it is **not a simple one-parameter correlation**. The clearest pattern is that **more basic ligands with multiple hard donor atoms, especially multidentate O-donor chelators, tend to form more stable Ca complexes** than small, more acidic monodentate or simple bidentate ligands.

All pKa values below are from **25 °C, I = 0.1 M**. Ca²⁺ stability coverage was confirmed for 1:1 Ca–ligand systems for these ligands.

| Ligand | Relevant pKa ladder at 25 °C, I = 0.1 M | Basicity note for Ca²⁺ binding | Ca²⁺ 1:1 stability coverage in SRD-46 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Oxalic acid | -1.20, 3.80 | Relatively acidic; small dicarboxylate | Yes |
| Tartaric acid | 2.82, 3.97 | Slightly more basic than oxalate; O-donor diacid | Yes |
| Citric acid | 2.90, 4.35, 5.65 | More basic polycarboxylate/hydroxyl ligand | Yes |

## Qualitative trend

A reasonable **qualitative stability ranking** from ligand chemistry and the confirmed Ca²⁺ coverage is:

| Approximate trend | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| Oxalate, tartrate < citrate < NTA < EDTA | As ligand basicity, negative charge, and denticity increase, Ca²⁺ binding generally strengthens |

## Is there a correlation?

Yes, but it is **moderate and conditional**, not strictly linear.

| Observation | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| More acidic/simple O-donor ligands such as oxalate tend to bind Ca²⁺ less strongly | Lower donor basicity and less structural advantage |
| More basic polycarboxylates such as citrate tend to bind more strongly | Higher charge density and multiple O donors help |
| NTA and especially EDTA are much stronger binders than simple carboxylates | Chelate effect and donor multiplicity dominate over pKa alone |
| Glycine has a high pKa step (9.57), but that mainly reflects ammonium deprotonation | For Ca²⁺, O-donor basicity matters more than amine basicity |

So the data support **a positive trend between ligand basicity and Ca²⁺ stability**, but **denticity, total ligand charge, donor type, and preorganization are at least as important as raw pKa**.

## Hypothesis for alkaline-earth metals

| Hypothesis | Rationale |
|---|---|
| For alkaline-earth metals such as Ca²⁺, binding strength generally increases with the basicity of hard donor sites, especially deprotonated oxygen donors, but the effect is amplified strongly by multidentate chelation and ligand charge. | Ca²⁺ is a hard Lewis acid and prefers hard O donors; therefore more basic O-donor ligands usually bind better, yet chelate structure often determines how much better. |

## Bottom line

| Conclusion | Answer |
|---|---|
| Correlation present? | **Yes, qualitatively positive** |
| Strong linear correlation? | **No** |
| Main controlling factors | **O-donor basicity, ligand charge, denticity/chelate effect, and preorganization** |

In short: **higher basicity usually helps Ca²⁺ binding, but for alkaline-earth metals the strongest ligands are not just the most basic—they are the most basic hard-donor ligands that also chelate efficiently.**

*Source: NIST SRD-46 / Stability Constants of Metal Complexes database.*
