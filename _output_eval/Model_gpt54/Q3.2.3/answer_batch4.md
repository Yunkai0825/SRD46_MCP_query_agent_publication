Using the SRD-46 values for oxalic acid and Ca–oxalate, a **reasonable estimate is that calcium oxalate precipitation starts becoming important around pH 4–5, and becomes much more favorable above pH ~5**.

| Quantity | SRD-46 value | Conditions | Meaning |
|---|---:|---|---|
| pKa₁ (H₂Ox ⇌ H⁺ + HOx⁻) | -1.2 | 25 °C, I = 0.1 M | First proton is already lost at essentially all normal pH |
| pKa₂ (HOx⁻ ⇌ H⁺ + Ox²⁻) | 3.8 | 25 °C, I = 0.1 M | Controls appearance of free oxalate dianion |
| logK for Ca²⁺ + Ox²⁻ ⇌ CaOx(aq) | 3.19 | 25 °C, I = 0 | Aqueous Ca–oxalate complex is fairly stable |
| logK for CaOx·H₂O(s) ⇌ Ca²⁺ + Ox²⁻ + H₂O | -7.86 | 25 °C, I = 0.1 M | Equivalent to very low solubility |
| logK for CaOx·3H₂O(s) ⇌ Ca²⁺ + Ox²⁻ + 3H₂O | -8.32 | 25 °C, I = 0 | Even lower solubility |

A quick speciation estimate from **pKa₂ = 3.8** gives the fraction of oxalate present as **Ox²⁻**:

| pH | Ox²⁻ / HOx⁻ | Approx. fraction as Ox²⁻ |
|---:|---:|---:|
| 3.8 | 1 | 50% |
| 4.8 | 10 | 91% |
| 5.8 | 100 | 99% |

So the key transition is near **pH ≈ pKa₂ ≈ 3.8**. Below that, much of oxalate is still protonated as hydrogen oxalate, reducing free **Ox²⁻**. Once the pH rises above about **4**, the concentration of **Ox²⁻** increases sharply, and because calcium oxalate is very insoluble, precipitation can become significant if Ca and total oxalate are present at typical millimolar levels.

**Practical answer:** expect significant calcium oxalate precipitation to emerge at about **pH 4–5**. If both calcium and oxalate are not extremely dilute, precipitation should be **strongly favored above pH 5**.

SRD-46 source: oxalic acid pKa and Ca–oxalate stability/solid-phase constants retrieved for the Ca²⁺/oxalate system.
