## Main trend seen in SRD-46

### Qualitative ordering
| Approximate stability trend | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| **Fe(III): multidentate hard O-donor chelators** ≫ **simple O-donor chelates / carboxylates / phenolates** > **simple inorganic anions such as F⁻, SCN⁻, N₃⁻** > **Cl⁻** | Strongest support from the database |
| **Fe(II): N-donor chelates and some mixed N/O chelators are expected to rise in relative importance vs Fe(III)**, while hard O-only ligands generally lose relative advantage | Chemically well-motivated, but less directly quantified from the extracted records here |

## Thermodynamic explanation

### 1. HSAB is the biggest driver
| Metal | HSAB character | Favored donors | Consequence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fe(III) | hard, highly charged acid | hard anionic O donors | Strong preference for hydroxamates, catecholates/phenolates, aminopolycarboxylates, hydroxo binding |
| Fe(II) | less hard, more borderline | N donors become more competitive | Bipyridyl/phenanthroline-like and mixed N/O chelators gain relative stabilization |

Fe(III) has higher charge density, so electrostatic attraction and donor–acceptor matching strongly favor **deprotonated O donors**. That is why hydroxamates and related siderophore-type ligands sit at the top of the database’s Fe(III) constants.

### 2. Chelate effect strongly amplifies binding
| Ligand feature | Thermodynamic effect |
|---|---|
| Higher denticity | More favorable ΔS on complexation and lower dissociation probability |
| Preorganization / macrocycles | Can further reduce entropic and conformational penalties |
| Multiple deprotonated donor sites | Raises electrostatic attraction, especially for Fe(III) |

This explains why **EDTA/CDTA/NTA-type systems** and **hydroxamate-rich ligands** outrank simple monodentate ligands. The very large constants at the top of the Fe(III) list are often for **polyligand or multinuclear species**, which further accumulate stabilization.

### 3. Crystal-field effects matter more for Fe(II) than Fe(III)
| Ion | d count | Typical spin-state situation | Effect on stability trends |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fe(III) | d5 | often high-spin; CFSE modest in many aqueous systems | HSAB + charge dominates |
| Fe(II) | d6 | stronger sensitivity to ligand field; low-spin stabilization possible with strong-field ligands | Strong-field N/C ligands can be unusually favorable |

So for Fe(II), **strong-field ligands** can gain extra stabilization through larger ligand-field splitting and possible low-spin formation. For Fe(III), that contribution is usually less decisive than simple hard-acid/hard-base matching.

## Why the Fe(III) ordering looks the way it does

| Class | Expected relative Fe(III) stability | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Hydroxamates / siderophore-like O-donors | Highest | Hard O donors, often multidentate, often multiply deprotonated |
| Aminopolycarboxylates | Very high | Multidentate chelation plus several carboxylate O donors |
| Catecholate / phenolate-rich ligands, Tiron-like ligands | High | Strong anionic O binding; often bidentate or polydentate |
| Simple carboxylates (acetate, malonate, oxalate) | Moderate to high | O-donor match is good, but denticity/charge density lower |
| F⁻ | Moderate among simple anions | Hard donor, so better than softer pseudohalides/chloride |
| SCN⁻, N₃⁻, Cl⁻ | Lower overall | Poorer hard-hard match, often monodentate, less chelate stabilization |

## Edge cases and cautions

| Edge case | Why it is special |
|---|---|
| **OH⁻** | Very strong Fe(III) binding appears in the database, but this is entangled with hydrolysis, polynuclear hydroxo species, and precipitation tendencies rather than simple “ligand strength” |
| **8-hydroxyquinoline** | Often binds strongly because it is chelating and partly O-donor, but poor solubility / precipitation can complicate equilibrium interpretation |
| **SCN⁻** | Spectroscopically dramatic Fe(III) complexation does not necessarily mean the highest thermodynamic stability |
| **Catechol / redox-active ligands** | Can blur pure Fe(III) equilibrium trends because redox chemistry may accompany coordination |
| **Macrocycles with appended hydroxypyridone or hydroxamate donors** | Can exceed ordinary class behavior because they combine hard donor matching, denticity, and preorganization |

## Bottom line

| Conclusion | Summary |
|---|---|
| Dominant database pattern | **Fe(III) overwhelmingly prefers hard, multidentate O-donor ligands** |
| Relative Fe(II) shift | **Fe(II) should show comparatively greater affinity for N-donor and strong-field ligands**, though the extracted records here did not cleanly quantify that side |
| Best explanation | **HSAB + chelate effect** set the main order; **crystal-field stabilization** becomes an important modifier especially for Fe(II) |
| Main edge cases | **Hydroxo species, redox-active ligands, precipitation-prone ligands, and multinuclear stoichiometries** can inflate or distort simple ligand-strength rankings |
