SRD-46 shows a **much broader Fe(III) preference for hard O-donor ligands** and a **broader Fe(II) spread into N-donor and mixed-donor chelators**. The database coverage itself reflects that: the Fe(III) catalog slice highlighted classic hard-base partners (fluoride, carboxylates, oxalate/malonate, salicylate/catechol-type ligands, hydroxamates, NTA/EDTA/CDTA-like aminopolycarboxylates), while Fe(II) includes many **polypyridyls, polyamines, aminopolycarboxylates, phosphonates, and some S-containing ligands**.

## Stability-constant trend by ligand class

A qualitative ordering supported by the retrieved Fe systems is:

| Fe(III) trend | Fe(II) trend |
|---|---|
| hydroxamates / catechol-type & strong multidentate O-donors / aminopolycarboxylates > oxine/salicylate-type chelates > oxalate > malonate > simple monocarboxylates ≳ fluoride > azide / thiocyanate > chloride | strong chelating polypyridyl and polyamine ligands / aminopolycarboxylates / phosphonate-rich chelators > mixed N,O macrocycles > simple N,O amino-acids & pyridylcarboxylates > cyanide / hydroxide / weaker monodentates |

## What trend stands out?

| Observation | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| Fe(III) systems are dominated by O-donor ligands and hydrolysis-related competition | Fe(III) is a small, highly charged **hard acid**; strong electrostatic stabilization is gained with hard bases, especially deprotonated O donors |
| Fe(II) systems include many more neutral N-donor ligands such as bipyridyl and phenanthroline | Fe(II) is more **borderline** in HSAB terms, so N-donor binding is comparatively more competitive |
| Multidentate ligands appear near the “top” for both oxidation states | The **chelate effect** and often macrocyclic preorganization strongly increase overall formation constants |
| Oxalate outranks simple monocarboxylates for Fe(III) | Bidentate O,O chelation plus higher ligand charge gives stronger binding than acetate/propionate-type monodentates |
| Fluoride is unusually good among simple monodentate inorganic ligands for Fe(III) | A classic HSAB match: F⁻ is very hard and strongly hydrated but still binds Fe(III) better than softer pseudohalides/halides |
| Fe(II) polypyridyl complexes are prominent edge cases | Strong-field N donors can add substantial **ligand-field stabilization**, especially when low-spin Fe(II) is favored |

## Thermodynamic reasoning behind the ordering

### 1. HSAB: the main Fe(III) vs Fe(II) split

| Metal ion | HSAB character | Donor types thermodynamically favored |
|---|---|---|
| Fe(III) | hard acid | deprotonated O donors: carboxylate, phenolate, catecholate, hydroxamate, phosphonate, fluoride |
| Fe(II) | borderline-hard / more polarizable than Fe(III) | N donors become much more competitive; mixed N/O and some S-containing ligands can also gain ground |

For **Fe(III)**, the strongest classes in this survey are exactly the ones expected for a hard trication: **hydroxamates, catechol/phenolate-rich ligands, and aminopolycarboxylates**.  
For **Fe(II)**, the database broadens toward **bipyridyl/phenanthroline/pyridylamine frameworks**, consistent with its less strongly hard character.

### 2. Chelate effect and ligand preorganization

| Ligand pattern | Expected stability effect |
|---|---|
| Monodentate < bidentate < polydentate | Higher stability from favorable entropy and reduced dissociation probability |
| Flexible polydentate < preorganized macrocycle / rigid scaffold | Preorganization lowers reorganization penalty and can further increase stability |

This explains why, for Fe(III), **EDTA/NTA-like ligands** and catechol/hydroxamate arrays outrank simple carboxylates, and why **oxalate > acetate-like ligands**. For Fe(II), it explains the strong showing of **polypyridyls, polyamines, aminopolycarboxylates, and macrocycles**.

### 3. Crystal-field effects: especially important for Fe(II)

| Metal | d-count | CFSE consequence |
|---|---|---|
| Fe(III) | d5 | high-spin d5 has little/no octahedral CFSE advantage, so ligand-field effects are usually less decisive than charge-density/HSAB effects |
| Fe(II) | d6 | strong-field ligands can stabilize **low-spin Fe(II)**, adding significant ligand-field stabilization to the complex |

This is why Fe(II) has important exceptions to a simple HSAB story. **Bipyridyl, phenanthroline, and terpyridyl-like ligands** are not the hardest donors, but they can form very stable Fe(II) complexes because they combine:

| Feature | Effect on Fe(II) stability |
|---|---|
| Bidentate/tridentate chelation | Strong chelate effect |
| Aromatic N donors | Good orbital match for Fe(II) |
| Strong-field ligand character | Can favor low-spin d6 Fe(II), increasing thermodynamic stability |

So Fe(II) often shows unusually strong affinity for **polypyridyl N-donors**, even when Fe(III) would generally prefer O-rich ligands.

## Explaining specific edge cases

| Edge case | Why it behaves that way |
|---|---|
| Fluoride with Fe(III) | Strong hard-hard match makes it better than chloride, azide, or thiocyanate despite being monodentate |
| Thiocyanate / chloride with Fe(III) | Poorer HSAB match; softer/more polarizable donors are disfavored versus deprotonated O donors |
| Oxalate vs simple carboxylates | Same O-donor family, but oxalate gains from bidentate chelation and dianionic charge |
| 8-Hydroxyquinoline and salicylate-type ligands for Fe(III) | Mixed donor set plus chelation; deprotonated phenolate O is especially favorable for Fe(III) |
| Hydroxamates for Fe(III) | Excellent siderophore-like O,O donor motif; hard-base match plus chelation gives very high stability |
| Polypyridyl ligands for Fe(II) | Strong-field N donors plus low-spin d6 stabilization can push them above many purely ionic O-donor competitors |
| S-containing Fe(II) ligands (e.g., BAL-type) | Fe(II) is more tolerant of softer donors than Fe(III), so sulfur donors can become competitive, though not generally dominant over optimized chelators |
| Hydroxide / hydrolysis for Fe(III) | Fe(III) hydrolyzes strongly; hydroxo species compete heavily and complicate apparent formation constants |

## Bottom-line ordering

| Overall conclusion | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Fe(III): strongest for hard, deprotonated, multidentate O donors** | Hydroxamates, catechol/phenolate arrays, aminopolycarboxylates sit at the top; simple anions and soft donors sit at the bottom |
| **Fe(II): strongest for chelating ligands, especially N-donor and mixed N/O systems** | Polypyridyls, polyamines, aminopolycarboxylates, and some macrocycles are favored; ligand-field stabilization matters much more here |
| **Chelation is a universal booster** | Within almost any donor class, multidentate ligands outrank monodentates |
| **Hydrolysis is the major Fe(III) complication** | Any Fe(III) comparison must remember that hydroxo complexation competes strongly in water |

## Final interpretation

| Metal | Best simple explanation |
|---|---|
| Fe(III) | “Hard, highly charged, hydrolyzing trication: loves O-donor chelators.” |
| Fe(II) | “More borderline d6 cation: chelation still matters, but N-donor ligand-field stabilization can strongly reorder the series.” |

So the database trend is: **Fe(III) stability constants rise with harder, more highly charged, more chelating O-donor ligands; Fe(II) stability constants rise with chelation too, but especially with strong-field N-donor frameworks that can stabilize low-spin Fe(II).** The major edge cases—fluoride for Fe(III), polypyridyls for Fe(II), and extensive Fe(III) hydrolysis—fit cleanly into HSAB + chelate effect + crystal-field arguments.
