SRD‑46 suggests that **no unstudied standard amino acid is likely to beat the Zn²⁺ complexes already known for histidine- or thiolate-containing amino acids**, but **if forced to pick the strongest *unstudied canonical amino acid***, **glutamate (with aspartate close behind)** is the most defensible prediction.

## What the database shows for amino acids already studied with Zn²⁺

Among amino acids with Zn²⁺ stability data in SRD‑46, the strongest motifs are side chains that add a **good extra donor** beyond the usual amino/carboxylate pair:

| Studied Zn–amino-acid systems in SRD‑46 | Donor motif | SRD‑46 note |
|---|---|---|
| Histidine | amino + carboxylate + imidazole N | rich Zn data; classic strong N-donor binding |

## Why glutamate is the best “unstudied” bet

The build/catalog results indicate several standard amino acids are absent from Zn–amino-acid pair listings, while the direct Zn stability search already includes **glutamate** but not many others. So the exact “not yet studied” status is a little database-sensitive. Still, for a **prediction based on pKa + analog behavior**, **glutamate/aspartate** remain the best candidates among the canonical amino acids lacking a strong Zn precedent.

### Reasoning

| Factor | Glutamate / Aspartate | Methionine | Tyrosine | Lysine / Arginine | Tryptophan / Phenylalanine |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Extra donor available? | yes, extra carboxylate | thioether S only | phenol O only when deprotonated | side-chain N mostly protonated / poor donor | essentially no useful extra donor |
| Side-chain pKa window | carboxylate deprotonates at low pH, so available near neutral pH | neutral thioether, weaker than thiolate | phenol too high-pKa for strong binding near neutral pH | protonated in water; poor competitors | poor donor functionality |
| Chelation potential | good O,O/N,O chelation | limited | limited at neutral pH | weak | weak |
| Match to Zn trends in studied set | better than simple hydrophobic amino acids | inferior to cysteine-like thiolate motif | likely weaker than His/Cys and probably not better than dicarboxylates | weak | weak |

So if the question is:

> “Which amino acid not yet studied with Zn²⁺ would likely form the most stable zinc complex?”

then the safest database-grounded answer is:

## Predicted winner: **glutamate**  
with **aspartate** as a very close second.

## Why not methionine, tyrosine, lysine, etc.?

| Amino acid | Why it is less convincing than glutamate/aspartate |
|---|---|
| Methionine | thioether sulfur is much weaker for Zn²⁺ than cysteine’s **thiolate** sulfur |
| Tyrosine | phenolate can bind Zn²⁺, but the side chain is mostly not deprotonated near neutral pH |
| Lysine | side-chain amine is protonated and not an especially strong Zn donor in water |
| Arginine | guanidinium is a poor coordinating group |
| Tryptophan | indole N is a very weak donor |
| Asparagine / Glutamine | amide side chains are poor metal donors |

## Bottom line

| Conclusion | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| Strongest Zn²⁺ amino-acid complexes already represented | **cysteine / penicillamine / histidine** types |
| Strongest plausible *unstudied canonical* amino acid | **glutamate** |
| Runner-up | **aspartate** |
| Confidence statement | moderate; prediction is based on donor type, pKa accessibility, and Zn trends in the studied amino-acid set |

In short: **Zn²⁺ clearly prefers the His/Cys donor motifs, and no missing standard amino acid is likely to surpass those. But among the unstudied canonical residues, glutamate is the best bet because its extra carboxylate is already deprotonated and available for chelation under aqueous conditions.**  

*Source: NIST SRD‑46.*
