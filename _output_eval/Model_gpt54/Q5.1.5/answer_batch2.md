Yes—**SRD-46 supports EDTA as a very good general-purpose chelator**.

The ligand is resolved in SRD-46 as **ligand_6277, Ethylenedinitrilotetraacetic acid (H4L)**, i.e. EDTA. A key sign is the breadth of reported metal complexes: the EDTA stability search returned many metal–EDTA systems, including **Al³⁺, Fe³⁺, Y³⁺, lanthanides, Zr⁴⁺, Hf⁴⁺, Th⁴⁺, and VO²⁺**.

| Evidence from SRD-46 | What it suggests |
|---|---|
| EDTA appears as **ligand_6277** with many metal partners | Broad chelation scope |
| Reported complexes include **3+ and 4+ ions** | Strong binding, especially to hard/high-charge metals |
| Multiple literature/measurement links exist, including a critical survey | EDTA chemistry is well-established and heavily studied |

Its **acid–base behavior** also explains why it works well but is **pH-dependent**. SRD-46 reports EDTA protonation constants at 25 °C, I = 0.1 M approximately as:

| Ligand | pKa | Protonation step |
|---|---:|---|
| EDTA (ligand_6277) | -1.5 | H4L → H5L |
| EDTA (ligand_6277) | 2.00 | H3L → H4L |
| EDTA (ligand_6277) | 2.69 | H2L → H3L |
| EDTA (ligand_6277) | 6.13 | HL → H2L |
| EDTA (ligand_6277) | 9.52 | L → HL |

So the practical answer is:

| Question | Answer |
|---|---|
| Is EDTA a good chelator? | **Yes** |
| Is it equally strong for every metal? | **No** |
| Does pH matter? | **Very much** |

At **low pH**, EDTA is more protonated and less available to bind metals strongly. At **near-neutral to alkaline pH**, more deprotonated forms are present, so chelation is much more effective. Chemically, that is why EDTA is excellent as a **broad-spectrum chelator**, but not “universally best” under every condition.

One useful pattern from the SRD-46 results is that EDTA shows especially strong affinity for **higher-charge metal ions** such as **Fe³⁺, Al³⁺, Zr⁴⁺, Hf⁴⁺, and Th⁴⁺**, which is exactly what you would expect for a multicarboxylate aminopolycarboxylate ligand.

**Source:** NIST SRD-46.
