Yes — **EDTA is a very good chelator** in SRD-46’s data, especially as a **broad-spectrum hexadentate ligand** that binds many divalent and trivalent metal ions strongly.

A key caveat is that EDTA’s **effective chelating power is strongly pH-dependent**, because the fully deprotonated binding form is generated only stepwise.

### EDTA protonation at 25 °C, I = 0.1 M
These pKa values explain why EDTA chelation is weaker in acidic solution.

| Transition | pKa |
|---|---:|
| H4L → H5L | -1.5 |
| H3L → H4L | 2.00 |
| H2L → H3L | 2.69 |
| HL → H2L | 6.13 |
| L → HL | 9.52 |

So at **low pH**, EDTA is substantially protonated and binds metals less effectively; at **neutral to basic pH**, chelation becomes much stronger.

### Bottom line
| Question | Answer |
|---|---|
| Is EDTA a good chelator? | **Yes** |
| For what metals? | **Many common divalent and trivalent ions** |
| Main limitation | **Its chelation is strongly pH-dependent** because EDTA is protonated at lower pH |
| Practical interpretation | Excellent **general-purpose sequestrant** for metal ions, though not always the most selective ligand |

Chemistry insight: EDTA is widely considered effective because it combines **multiple donor atoms** with the **chelate effect**, giving strong 1:1 complexes across much of the periodic table. Its biggest real-world weakness is not lack of strength, but rather **reduced conditional binding in acidic media** and limited selectivity among similarly sized metal ions.
