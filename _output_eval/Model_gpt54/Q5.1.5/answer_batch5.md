Yes — **EDTA is a very good chelator** in SRD-46.

It has a broad coordination record in the database and binds many metal ions strongly. SRD-46 lists **75 metal partners** and **596 measurements** for EDTA (`ligand_6277`, Ethylenedinitrilotetraacetic acid), which already indicates unusually broad chelation coverage.

|---|---:|
| Ligand | Ethylenedinitrilotetraacetic acid (EDTA) |

| Metal partners | 75 |

SRD-46 also shows EDTA forms complexes across **alkaline-earth, transition, trivalent, lanthanide, actinide, and oxo-metal** systems. The EDTA catalog includes systems such as Mg²⁺, Ca²⁺, Cu²⁺, Ni²⁺, Zn²⁺, Fe³⁺, Al³⁺, lanthanides, UO2²⁺, and Th⁴⁺.

A key caveat: EDTA’s **effective** chelation depends strongly on **pH**, because the free ligand is protonated at lower pH. SRD-46 reports these protonation constants at 25 °C, I = 0.1 M:

| Protonation step | pKa |
|---|---:|
| H4L → H5L | -1.50 |
| H3L → H4L | 2.00 |
| H2L → H3L | 2.69 |
| HL → H2L | 6.13 |
| L → HL | 9.52 |

So the short answer is:

| Question | Answer |
|---|---|
| Is EDTA a good chelator? | **Yes — strongly and broadly so.** |
| Why? | It is a multidentate aminopolycarboxylate ligand with very broad metal coverage in SRD-46. |
| Main limitation | Its apparent binding strength drops at lower pH because protonation competes with metal binding. |

Chemistry insight: EDTA is especially useful because it is **hexadentate**, so it benefits from the **chelate effect** and often forms very stable 1:1 metal complexes. In practice, it is excellent for sequestering many metal ions, though selectivity varies by metal and conditions.

**Source:** NIST SRD-46.
