Interpretation and approximate pH threshold:
- Precipitation begins when the ion product [Ca^2+][C2O4^2−] exceeds Ksp. At 25 °C and I ≈ 0.1 M, Ksp ≈ 10^−7.86.
- The fraction of total oxalate present as C2O4^2− rises sharply around the second acidity step (pKa ≈ 3.8 at 25 °C, I = 0.1 M), so the onset is closely tied to this pKa.
- Without specific concentrations, the general criterion is α2(pH) ≈ Ksp / ([Ca]_total · [oxalate]_total), where α2 is the fraction of oxalate as C2O4^2−. Complexation (logβ1 ≈ 3.19 at I = 0) lowers the free-ion product and nudges the threshold pH upward.

Worked example (to anchor “significant”):
- If [Ca]_total = [oxalate]_total = 1 mM at 25 °C, I ≈ 0.1 M, ignoring CaC2O4(aq) complexation: you need α2 ≈ 10^−7.86 / (10^−3 × 10^−3) ≈ 0.014. That small fraction of C2O4^2− is reached already around pH ≈ 2–3, and precipitation strengthens rapidly as pH approaches and exceeds the second pKa (~3.8). Including CaC2O4(aq) complexation (β1 ≈ 10^3.19) reduces free ions and shifts onset upward somewhat, but the qualitative picture remains: “significant” precipitation typically emerges as pH moves into the vicinity of pKa2 and above.

Bottom line:
- Expect the onset of calcium oxalate precipitation to become significant once pH approaches and exceeds the second dissociation step of oxalic acid, around pH ≈ 4 under typical aqueous conditions, with the exact threshold depending strongly on [Ca^2+] and [oxalate] and modestly on ionic strength and complexation.

Source: NIST SRD-46 (oxalic acid pKa and Ca–oxalate stability/solubility constants).
