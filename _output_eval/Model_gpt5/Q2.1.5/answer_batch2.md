Answer: Ethylenediamine binds markedly more strongly to Ni²⁺ than ammonia. Using the same stability-constant definition (β1 = K1 for ML formation) at 25 °C and the same ionic medium/strength, the difference is about 4 log units.

| Ligand | log β1 (= log K1) | T (°C) | Ionic strength (M) | Medium | Definition |
|--------|--------------------|--------|--------------------|--------|------------|
| Ammonia (NH3) | 2.32 | 25 | 1.0 | KNO3 | β_def_812 ([M]+[L]⇌[ML]) |
| Ethylenediamine (en) | ≈6.3 | 25 | 1.0 | KNO3 | β_def_812 ([M]+[L]⇌[ML]) |

Computed difference: Δlog K1 ≈ 6.3 − 2.32 = 3.98 (≈4.0).

For context at lower ionic strength (25 °C, I ≈ 0.1 M NaNO3): NH3 has K1 ≈ 2.3, while en has K1 ≈ 7.5, giving Δlog K1 ≈ 5.2. Thus, en’s first-step binding is about 10^4–10^5 times stronger than NH3 depending on ionic strength, consistent with the chelate effect (en is bidentate, forming a five-membered chelate ring with Ni²⁺).

Source: NIST SRD-46 (equilibrium catalog entries; β_def_812 for ML formation).
