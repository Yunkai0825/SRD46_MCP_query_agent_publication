Answer in brief: Ethylenediamine (en) binds far more strongly than ammonia (NH3) to Ni2+. At 25 °C and low ionic strength (I → 0), log K1(en) ≈ 7.32 vs log K1(NH3) ≈ 1.99, a ΔlogK ≈ 5.33 (~2 × 10^5-fold stronger). At I ≈ 1.0 M, the gap is even larger (ΔlogK ≈ 5.86).

Matched-condition comparison (25 °C, aqueous; reaction Ni2+ + L ⇌ [NiL]2+)

| Ionic strength (M) | log K1 (NH3) | log K1 (en) | ΔlogK = logK1(en) − logK1(NH3) |
|-------------------:|-------------:|------------:|--------------------------------:|
| ~0 (extrapolated)  | 1.99         | 7.32        | 5.33                            |
| ≈1.0               | 1.70         | 7.56        | 5.86                            |

Notes and context:
- NH3 values (25 °C): log β1 = 1.99 at low I (extrapolated to I→0); 1.70 at I ≈ 1.0 M (NH4NO3). For the first step, β1 = K1, so these are stepwise log K1.
- en values (25 °C): log K1 = 7.32 (I = 0); 7.56 (I = 1.0 M), from ML formation [M] + [L] ⇌ [ML] with neutral en.

Chemistry insight (why so large a difference?): en is a bidentate chelating ligand, so forming the 5-membered chelate ring provides both an entropic and enthalpic advantage (the chelate effect), leading to ~10^5–10^6 higher K1 than monodentate NH3 for Ni2+.

Source: NIST SRD‑46 (IUPAC Stability Constants Database) — Ni2+–NH3 and Ni2+–ethylenediamine entries at 25 °C under matched ionic strengths.
