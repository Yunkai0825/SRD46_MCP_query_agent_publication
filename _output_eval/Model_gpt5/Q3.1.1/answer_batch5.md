What “binds best” in SRD‑46
- In the Pb2+ dataset at 25 °C, polyaminopolycarboxylates (EDTA family, including DTPA/CDTA/EGTA/HEDTA) populate the top of the ranked results when sorting by reported formation constants. These generally show much stronger binding than simple mono/di‑carboxylates (e.g., acetate).
- Pb2+ also binds very strongly to thiol donors. SRD‑46 includes Pb2+ constants for simple model thiols (e.g., 2‑mercaptoethanol; 3‑mercapto‑1,2‑propanediol), illustrating the high affinity of Pb2+ for soft S‑donors. This underpins the effectiveness of vicinal dithiols in therapy, even if the exact therapeutic ligands aren’t all represented with full datasets in SRD‑46.

Clinical relevance (short answer)
- Yes — one of the top SRD‑46 ligands, EDTA, is used clinically for lead: CaNa2EDTA is an established IV chelator for moderate–severe Pb poisoning.
- Succimer (DMSA) is a frontline oral chelator for lead in current medical practice; although SRD‑46 shows Pb2+ binding to thiol model ligands, I did not retrieve a verified Pb–DMSA record in the quick scan above. DMPS and dimercaprol (BAL) also have Pb2+ thiol chemistry; BAL is reserved for severe cases (often with EDTA), and DMPS is less common for lead in the U.S.

Two practical notes for protocol design
- Media/conditions matter: Reported constants in SRD‑46 vary with ionic strength and anion (Cl− vs ClO4−). For therapy relevance, compare values at 25 °C and I ≈ 0.1–0.5 M, and consider conditional stability at pH 7.2–7.4.
- Safety/selectivity: EDTA must be given as CaNa2EDTA to avoid hypocalcemia; polyaminocarboxylates can also sequester Zn2+ and other essential metals. Thiol chelators may be better for intracellular Pb2+ but have their own risk profiles.
