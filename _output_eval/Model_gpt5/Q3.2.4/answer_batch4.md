What trend emerges across ligand classes?
- Fe(III) (hard acid) shows the broadest speciation and strongest complex series with hard O‑donor chelators:
  - Catechols and hydroxamates (e.g., tiron; acetohydroxamic acid) form rich stepwise series up to ML3, consistent with very stable Fe(III)–O bonds.
  - Polyaminocarboxylates (EDTA, CDTA, NTA) form robust 1:1 chelates with hydroxo/acid–base coupled formation, again signaling strong Fe(III) affinity for O‑rich, multidentate ligands.
  - Inorganic anions show the expected halide order: F− (ML–ML3) > Cl− (ML–ML2). Ambidentate SCN− and N‑donor azide nevertheless show extended series (SCN− up to ML4; N3− up to ML5), indicating appreciable Fe(III) affinity when N‑bound and/or at higher ligand excess.
  - Hydrolysis/polynuclear Fe(III)–OHx species are abundant; weak ligands compete poorly at higher pH unless chelation is strong.

- Fe(II) (borderline acid) favors N‑donor ligation and chelation:
  - Neutral N‑donor chelates (bipyridine, phenanthroline, ethylenediamine) show complete octahedral series culminating in ML3 (tris‑chelate) for bipy/phen and ML3 for en, highlighting strong Fe(II)–N binding.
  - Simple ammine complexes extend to ML4 (tetrammine), consistent with moderate but significant affinity of Fe(II) for N donors.
  - Aminocarboxylates and amino acids bind, but are often dominated by ML with more limited progression to ML2/ML3, reflecting weaker O‑donor components for Fe(II) relative to N‑donors.
  - Polyaminocarboxylates (NTA, DTPA) still chelate Fe(II); macrocyclic/polyamine systems are present, though Fe(II) hydrolysis and solid formation (e.g., with phosphate to vivianite) can divert speciation.

Thermodynamic reasoning for the ordering and edge cases
- HSAB (hard–soft acid–base): Fe(III) is a hard Lewis acid and preferentially binds hard O donors. This is reflected in the SRD‑46 coverage by extensive Fe(III) speciation for catecholates, hydroxamates, carboxylates, and fluoride. Fe(II) is more borderline; its observed preference for neutral N‑donors (ammine, polypyridyl, aliphatic diamines) and reduced emphasis on purely O‑donor ligands matches HSAB expectations.
- Chelate effect: Multidentate ligands (EDTA, CDTA, DTPA, bipy/phen, en) show richer stepwise series and robust ML formation than comparable monodentate donors. This entropic advantage and enhanced effective concentration explain why aminocarboxylates and polypyridyls dominate the Fe(III) and Fe(II) survey, respectively, and why tris‑chelate ML3 species are common for Fe(II) with bipy/phen.
- Crystal‑field considerations: For Fe(II) (d6), strong‑field N‑donor chelates (polypyridyls) can stabilize low‑spin octahedral complexes, increasing the driving force for ML3 formation versus aquo/ammine baselines. For Fe(III) (d5), CFSE differences are smaller, and the dominant factor becomes hard‑acid/oxide binding; hence the prominence of O‑donor chelators and fluoride. The solid Fe(III)–oxinate (ML3(s)) exemplifies how chelate rigidity and π‑interaction can tip the balance toward very stable (even insoluble) complexes despite an O,N donor set.
- Edge cases in the survey:
  - Ambidentate SCN− with Fe(III): Although the S terminus is softer, the database shows an extended series up to ML4; this is consistent with predominant N‑coordination to hard Fe(III), and the ability of high ligand activity to drive higher coordination numbers.
  - Azide with Fe(III): Multiple MLn species (to ML5) indicate that linear N3− can act as a competent N‑donor to the hard Fe(III) center under appropriate conditions.
  - Phosphate with Fe(II): The presence of vivianite (solid) underscores that for highly basic O‑donors, precipitation and hydrolysis can outcompete formation of high‑order soluble complexes—an important speciation caveat when comparing “stability” across classes.
