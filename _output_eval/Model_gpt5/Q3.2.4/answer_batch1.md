What overall trend emerges?
- Fe(III): Strong, hard O‑donor chelates (catecholates, hydroxamates, aminopolycarboxylates) show the largest cumulative β, with catecholate/hydroxamate examples reaching log β3 ≈ 28–45 ranges (for cumulative β) under moderate ionic strengths. Pseudohalides/halides are much weaker per ligand (azide, thiocyanate), though high coordination numbers can yield sizable cumulative βn. Hydrolysis, olation/oxolation, and precipitation (e.g., FeOOH, FeQ3) frequently compete and can dominate speciation at modest pH.
- Fe(II): The catalog shows robust complex formation with multidentate N/O ligands. Strong‑field N‑heteroaryl ligands (bipyridyl, phenanthroline) form up to ML3, consistent with enhanced stability via induced low‑spin states and added LFSE. Aminopolycarboxylates (e.g., DTPA, NTA) and phosphonate‑containing ligands also form strong Fe(II) complexes. Simple amines and amino acids give moderate ML–ML3 sets, while hydroxo and phosphate chemistry can lead to polynuclear species or solids (e.g., vivianite in the phosphate system).

Thermodynamic reasoning for the ordering and edge cases
- HSAB: Fe(III) is a hard Lewis acid and preferentially binds hard O donors; hence catecholate (O,O), hydroxamate (O,O), and carboxylate/phosph(on)ate classes outrank halides and pseudohalides. Fe(II) is more borderline; strong‑field N donors (bpy/phen) gain ground relative to simple O donors, but multidentate hard donors still bind well.
- Chelate and multidentate effects: Larger denticity and preorganization (e.g., catecholates, hydroxamates, aminopolycarboxylates) increase β via favorable entropy and enthalpy, explaining the large cumulative constants in Fe(III) and the strong Fe(II) complexes with DTPA/NTA and polypyridyls (ML3).
- Crystal‑field/spin‑state: High‑spin d5 Fe(III) has near‑zero CFSE in octahedral fields; donor hardness and chelation dominate. In contrast, Fe(II) (d6) can gain significant CFSE when driven low‑spin by strong‑field ligands (bpy/phen), boosting stability relative to simple amines.
- Edge cases:
  • Azide/thiocyanate: High coordination numbers can inflate cumulative βn, but per‑ligand strength remains modest; SCN− binds better than N3− due to S‑donor softness.
  • Oxine: Apparent “high stability” reflects precipitation (very low Ksp), not just aqueous β.
  • Hydroxo/acetate: Extensive hydrolysis and polynuclear cluster formation (Fe(III)) complicate simple β comparisons and can dominate under near‑neutral pH.
