Hypothesis: Hg2+ strongly prefers sulfur-donor ligands over nitrogen-donor and oxygen-donor ligands (S ≫ N > O).

Representative Hg2+ stability constants from SRD‑46 (25 °C; nitrate or perchlorate media; mononuclear, aqueous):

| Donor class | Ligand (L) | Reaction (stoichiometry) | Reported constant(s), log10 | Medium (Ionic strength) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| S (thiolate) | Cysteine (Cys−) | Hg2+ + Cys− ⇄ Hg(Cys)+; Hg2+ + 2 Cys− ⇄ Hg(Cys)2(aq) | K1 = 21.0; K2 = 20.5; β2 = 41.5 | 0.10 M NaNO3 |
| S (thiolate) | Glutathione (GS−) | Hg2+ + GS− ⇄ Hg(GS)+; Hg2+ + 2 GS− ⇄ Hg(GS)2(aq) | K1 = 19.2; K2 = 18.7; β2 = 37.9 | 0.10 M NaNO3 |
| S (neutral thione) | Thiourea (Tu) | Hg2+ + Tu ⇄ HgTu2+; Hg2+ + 2 Tu ⇄ HgTu22+ | K1 = 9.6; β2 = 17.8 (K2 = 8.2) | 1.0 M NaClO4 |
| N | Ethylenediamine (en) | Hg2+ + en ⇄ Hg(en)2+; Hg2+ + 2 en ⇄ Hg(en)22+ | K1 = 8.8; β2 = 13.6 (K2 = 4.8) | 0.10 M NaNO3 |
| N | Imidazole (Im) | Hg2+ + Im ⇄ Hg(Im)2+; Hg2+ + 2 Im ⇄ Hg(Im)22+ | K1 = 5.3; β2 = 9.5 (K2 = 4.2) | 0.10 M NaNO3 |
| O | Acetate (Ac−) | Hg2+ + Ac− ⇄ HgAc+; Hg2+ + 2 Ac− ⇄ Hg(Ac)2(aq) | K1 = 1.5; β2 = 2.7 (K2 = 1.2) | 0.10 M NaNO3 |
| O (polycarboxylate chelate) | Citrate (Cit3−) | Hg2+ + Cit3− ⇄ HgCit− (and protonated forms) | β1 ≈ 14.2; HgHCit species β ≈ 12.0 | 0.10 M NaNO3 |

Interpretation and rationale:
- Sulfur donors: Deprotonated thiolates (e.g., cysteine, glutathione) yield exceptionally large log K values (K1 ≈ 19–21; β2 ≈ 38–42), far exceeding N- and O-donor examples under comparable conditions. Even a neutral thione S donor (thiourea) is stronger than typical N donors.
- Nitrogen donors: Moderate binding (en K1 = 8.8; Im K1 = 5.3; β2 ≤ ~14), well below thiolate complexes by >10 orders of magnitude for K1.
- Oxygen donors: Generally weak (acetate K1 = 1.5; β2 ≈ 2.7). Citrate shows larger cumulative stability (β1 ≈ 14) due to multidentate chelation, but on a per-donor/stepwise basis still trails far behind thiolates.

Conclusion: The order S ≫ N > O is strongly supported by these data. This aligns with HSAB expectations for Hg(II) as a soft/borderline acid that forms its most stable complexes with soft thiolate sulfur donors. The differences are robust across media (nitrate/perchlorate) and ionic strengths used here and span many orders of magnitude in stability.

Source: NIST Standard Reference Database 46 (SRD‑46), mercury(II) stability constants as summarized above.
