Here’s a quick “data tour” of rare earths in NIST SRD‑46 that’s fun and revealing.

Coverage snapshot (Ln(III) plus Y and Sc) — how richly each metal is represented in coordination/stability data:

| Metal (ID) | Charge | β-definitions (count) | Ligands (count) | Measurements (count) |
|---|---|---:|---:|---:|
| La (metal_79) | +3 | 30 | 297 | 931 |
| Nd (metal_110) | +3 | 29 | 263 | 794 |
| Gd (metal_65) | +3 | 28 | 288 | 897 |
| Dy (metal_44) | +3 | 23 | 252 | 758 |
| Er (metal_55) | +3 | 29 | 241 | 792 |
| Lu (metal_81) | +3 | 25 | 234 | 671 |
| Y (metal_205) | +3 | 26 | 182 | 533 |
| Sc (metal_169) | +3 | 16 | 44 | 156 |

Rare oxidation-state “cameos” — much sparser than the dominant +3 series:

| Metal state (ID) | β-definitions (count) | Ligands (count) |
|---|---:|---:|
| Ce(IV) (metal_28) | 5 | 3 |
| Eu(II) (metal_59) | 1 | 2 |
| Yb(II) (metal_207) | 0 | 0 |

What’s interesting:
- The trivalent lanthanides are extensively studied, with mid–heavy Ln(III) like Gd and Dy showing hundreds of ligands and measurements. That mirrors the practical importance of their coordination chemistry.
- Sc(III) and Y(III) are present but with leaner coverage than the lanthanides.
- Non‑trivalent states pop up as curiosities: Ce(IV) has a handful of entries, Eu(II) has very few, and Yb(II) is essentially absent in this dataset — consistent with the stability of Ln(III) under typical aqueous conditions.

Source: NIST Standard Reference Database 46 (SRD‑46).
