Is there a correlation between ligand basicity (pKa) and Ca2+ binding? Within a given donor class and similar denticity, a positive trend is visible: more basic, more fully deprotonated O donors at the working pH generally give larger Ca2+ logβ. For example, monocarboxylate acetate (pKa 4.76) binds weakly (logβ1 ≈ 0.6–0.8), while bidentate dicarboxylates and multidentate polycarboxylates escalate to tartrate/citrate (logβ1 ≈ 2.7–3.6) and then to aminopolycarboxylates NTA/EDTA (logβ ≈ 6.4 → 10.7). However, across different families, basicity alone is not predictive: oxalate (lower pKa2 ≈ 3.8) still outperforms malonate/succinate due to tight O,O chelation; phosphates (lower pKa2 than carbonate) bind Ca2+ more strongly because of higher charge density and favorable hard‑hard interactions, and polyphosphates/phosphonates gain further strength via multidentate binding and multiple negative charges.

Hypothesis for alkaline‑earth metals: Binding strength increases with effective oxygen‑donor basicity and total negative charge at the coordination site, but is strongly modulated by denticity and geometry (chelate effect, ring size) and by ligand class (phosphates/phosphonates > polycarboxylates > monocarboxylates). Thus, within a ligand family at matched conditions, higher pKa of the relevant O‑acid group correlates with larger Ca2+ logβ, while cross‑family comparisons must account for charge and chelation first.
