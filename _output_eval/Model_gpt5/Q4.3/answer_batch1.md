Answer to your question
- The chelate-effect magnitude is not constant across metals; it varies noticeably: Cu2+ > Ni2+ > Zn2+ under matched conditions.
- Using your metric (Δ_user = log K_en − 2·log K_NH3), Δ_user is about 2.45 (Cu2+), 2.30 (Ni2+), and 1.03 (Zn2+).
- If we use the more rigorous two-ammonia competitor (Δ′ = log K_en − log β2(NH3) = log K1(en) − [log K1(NH3)+log K2(NH3)]), the same ordering holds and the contrast is even clearer: ~3.09 (Cu2+), 2.66 (Ni2+), 1.19 (Zn2+).

Hypothesis for the differences
- Beyond the “classical” entropic chelate effect (which is similar for these cases), metal-dependent enthalpic factors dominate:
  - Cu2+ (d9) often gains the most from bidentate en: strong M–N covalency and Jahn–Teller preferences favor two robust equatorial Cu–N bonds from one ligand, enhancing stability relative to two separate NH3 donors.
  - Ni2+ (d8, high-spin in water) benefits from chelation and somewhat stronger-field en vs NH3, giving a sizable but smaller Δ than Cu2+.
  - Zn2+ (d10) has zero ligand-field stabilization energy and more ionic, less covalent M–N bonding; chelation still helps but yields the smallest Δ.
- Bite fit of en (five-membered ring) is broadly good across these metals, but increased covalency/LFSE contributions for Cu2+ and Ni2+ amplify the chelate advantage compared to Zn2+.
